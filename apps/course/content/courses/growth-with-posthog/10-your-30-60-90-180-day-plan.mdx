## 10.1 How to use this plan

Everything before this chapter taught the machine; this chapter sequences the build. A
few rules about how to read it:

- **The phases are gates, not dates.** "30/60/90/180" assumes roughly a founder-day per
  week of focused growth work. With more time you can compress; with less, stretch —
  the one thing worth protecting is the *order*, because each phase's exit criteria
  are the entry requirements for the next. Shipping ads (phase 3) before retention
  work (phase 2) is the leaky-bucket pattern from Chapter 2, now with a budget
  attached.
- **The plan is stage-agnostic, the targets aren't.** A pre-launch product runs the same
  sequence but treats phase 1 as "instrument before launch" and phase 3 as launch
  channels rather than paid scaling. Where a number matters, this chapter says what it
  gates rather than pretending one benchmark fits every product — Chapter 2's warning
  about chasing someone else's magic number applies to plans too.
- **This is the same first-90-days shape growth operators run when they land in a new
  role** — learn the data first, find early wins in activation/retention, only then
  scale acquisition ([Reforge's first-90-days
  guide](https://www.reforge.com/guides/succeed-in-your-new-growth-leadership-role),
  [Delivering Value's head-of-growth
  plan](https://deliveringvalue.co/growth-essays/head-of-growth)). You're running it on
  your own company.

## 10.2 Day 0 — commit (one hour, no code)

Chapter 0's commitments, now as a checklist. They're best done in one sitting with the
whole team present — an hour, and it tends to prevent the two most expensive patterns
in the course: nobody owning the loop, and acquisition before retention.

<Checklist
  id="plan-day0"
  title="Day 0 — commitments"
  items={[
    "Name the growth owner — one person accountable for measure → keep → grow (a hat, not a hire)",
    "Write the activation sentence: the one user action that means 'got real value', in one sentence",
    "Get the team to agree the order out loud: retention before acquisition",
    "Book the weekly 30-minute growth review, recurring, starting next week",
    "Pick where growth work lives: the same backlog/repo as product work, not a separate doc nobody opens",
  ]}
/>

The last two matter more than they look. A recurring 30-minute meeting is the smallest
possible container for a growth *ritual* — the thing Chapter 9 argues separates a
growth team from a team that did a growth project once. And putting growth work in the
same backlog as product work is what makes "growth is an engineering discipline"
(Chapter 0) real rather than aspirational.

Two of those commitments are *writing* commitments, so there's space for them right
here. Both save to [your workbook](/workbook) alongside everything else you put down
in the course, linked back to the lesson it came from.

## 10.3 Days 1–30 — Measure

**Goal: see the truth about your funnel.** Nothing in this phase grows the business
directly. All of it determines whether the next five months of work target the real
leak or a guessed one.

Week by week, the shape most teams follow:

| Week | Focus | Chapters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | PostHog live: SDK installed, `identify`/`reset` wired, starter events firing | 1, 3 |
| 2 | Tracking plan written; activation event defined; server-side revenue events | 2, 3 |
| 3 | Reverse proxy; data QA — watch replays, check identities merge correctly | 3 |
| 4 | The daily dashboard built and pinned; alerts to Slack; first honest read | 4 |

<Checklist
  id="plan-30"
  title="Days 1–30 — Measure"
  items={[
    "PostHog account on the right region; one project; website + app tracked together",
    "posthog-js installed with identified_only; identify at login, reset at logout",
    "Starter event kit firing — signup client-side; subscription + payment server-side",
    "Tracking plan written down and shared — even ten rows in a doc beats zero",
    "Your one activation event defined, instrumented, and confirmed firing",
    "Managed reverse proxy set up so tracking survives ad-blockers",
    "Daily dashboard built and pinned: North Star, activation funnel, retention, alerts → Slack",
    "First data QA pass: watch 5 session replays and confirm the events match what you see",
  ]}
/>

**Exit criteria.** You can open the dashboard and say, with numbers: *where* in the
funnel you lose the most people, *what* your week-4 retention roughly is, and *which*
phase of product-market fit you're in (Chapter 4, §4.3). If those three sentences
aren't sayable yet, that's the signal to stay in this phase a little longer —
[YC's analytics-for-startups
guidance](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8H-analytics-for-startups) is blunt that
most startups' first analytics problem is not volume of charts but trusting what the
few important ones say.

**The pattern to watch for here** is instrumenting forever. Thirty days is enough to
measure a funnel honestly; it is not enough to build a data platform — a schema
registry in week three is usually growth work quietly traded for tooling work.

## 10.4 Days 31–60 — Keep

**Goal: plug the biggest leaks before spending a cent on traffic.** This is the phase
with the highest return per hour in the whole plan — the users it saves are users you
already paid (in money or effort) to acquire.

Journeys are best built in the order of expected impact, which for almost every
product looks like:

1. **Welcome + onboarding nudge** — the gap between signup and activation is nearly
   always the biggest leak (Chapter 5). One journey: welcome on signup, a nudge at
   +48h if the activation event hasn't fired.
2. **Dunning** — if you charge money, failed payments are pure, recoverable churn
   (Chapter 6). This tends to be the fastest revenue win in the whole course.
3. **Win-back** — dormant-user re-engagement, triggered by an inactivity window, with
   something concrete to come back to.

<Checklist
  id="plan-60"
  title="Days 31–60 — Keep"
  items={[
    "Hogsend app scaffolded; backend wired to POST /v1/ingest",
    "Welcome + onboarding journey live, triggered by the signup event",
    "Activation nudge at +48h, skipped automatically if the activation event fired",
    "Dunning journey live (if you charge) — failed payment → retry prompt sequence",
    "Win-back journey live for users inactive 14+ days",
    "A holdout on your first journey, and you know how to read its lift in PostHog",
    "Global frequency cap set so overlapping journeys can't spam one user",
    "Unsubscribe/preferences verified working end-to-end (send yourself every email)",
  ]}
/>

**Exit criteria.** Either your activation or early-retention number has visibly moved,
or — more honestly — your **holdout comparison** shows the journeys lifting it
(Chapter 5's incrementality argument: opens and clicks are activity, lift is proof).
The bucket holds water well enough that pouring into it isn't waste.

**The pattern to watch for** is building all eight journeys you can imagine instead of
the three that matter — volume of automation isn't the goal; measured lift is. A
second, subtler one: declaring victory off open rates. Without a holdout, what got
measured was sending, not keeping.

## 10.5 Days 61–90 — Grow

**Goal: open the tap, and make every drop land in an audience you own.** Only now does
acquisition spend make sense — and for some products (dev tools especially, Chapter 7)
"the tap" is content, launches, and community rather than paid ads at all.

/>

<Checklist
  id="plan-90"
  title="Days 61–90 — Grow"
  items={[
    "Channel decision made deliberately: is paid even your channel, or is it content/launches/community? (Ch 7)",
    "If paid: Meta Pixel + PostHog → Meta CAPI destination, same Pixel ID, deduped, EMQ ≥ 6",
    "One campaign live, optimising the deepest conversion event you can feed ~50/week",
    "Consent captured at email collection; no dark-pattern pre-ticks",
    "Discord and/or Telegram connector live with the /link email-verify flow (Ch 8)",
    "Linking perk journey live — linked identity gets something real within a minute",
    "Referral ask placed at the aha milestone, not before; verified referrals rewarded",
    "Verified emails synced as a custom audience + lookalike seed",
  ]}
/>

**Exit criteria.** Traffic is flowing from a channel you chose on purpose;
conversions are tracked accurately enough to optimise on (not Pixel-alone guesswork);
and a visible share of new users end up in a channel you own — a verified email, a
linked Discord or Telegram — rather than only in an ad platform's audience you rent.

**The pattern to watch for** is scaling spend before the measurement is trustworthy.
When EMQ is low or events are missing, the ad platform optimises on noise — and the
tuition comes out of your budget. A day spent on conversion quality routinely beats a
week of creative testing.

## 10.6 Days 91–180 — Compound

The first 90 days build the machine. The next 90 turn it into a *culture* — the
difference between a system that ran once and one that gets a little better every
week. Three threads run in parallel:

**1. An experimentation program, not occasional experiments.** Keep a scored backlog
of growth hypotheses — Sean Ellis's
[ICE](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/ice-scoring-model) (impact, confidence,
ease) is the standard scoring scheme, [PIE](https://conversion.com/framework/pie-framework/)
a close alternative — and run a steady tempo of tests from the top of it. Ellis's
[high-tempo testing](https://www.saastr.com/sean-ellis-founder-ceo-of-growthhackers-building-a-company-wide-growth-culture-video-transcript/)
argument is that learning rate, not any single win, is what compounds; Reforge's
[experiment management system](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-experiment-management-system)
is the same idea with a template and a velocity metric attached. Temper the tempo with
discipline: [not everything deserves an experiment](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-running-an-experiment),
and an underpowered test that "wins" is [worse than no test](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-ab-testing).

**2. Prove the program pays.** By day 180 you should be reporting **incremental lift**
from the lifecycle program (global holdout, Chapter 5) and **blended CAC** including
the referral loop's contribution — is growth getting cheaper as the owned audience
grows? That's the flywheel from Chapter 8 showing up in the finances.

**3. Decide what you are before you scale.** Real acquisition money is worth gating on
evidence of product-market fit — the
[Sean Ellis 40% test](https://pmfsurvey.com/) ("how disappointed would you be if this
product disappeared?") is the standard instrument. It's also worth running the system
yourself for a while before hiring a head of growth to run it for you; First Round's
[founder-led growth playbook](https://review.firstround.com/founder-led-growth-playbook/)
makes the case that premature growth hires fail precisely because the founder skipped
the loop you've just spent 90 days building.

<Checklist
  id="plan-180"
  title="Days 91–180 — Compound"
  items={[
    "Experiment backlog exists, ICE-scored, groomed in the weekly growth review",
    "Steady test tempo: every week at least one experiment launches or reports",
    "Every experiment ships with a hypothesis, a primary metric, and a written result — including the losers",
    "Global holdout on the lifecycle program; incremental lift reported monthly",
    "Blended CAC reviewed monthly, with the referral loop's contribution broken out",
    "PMF check run (40% test) before any major step-up in paid spend",
    "Quarterly prune: kill journeys with no lift, events nobody queries, ad sets that don't convert",
    "The activation metric re-validated — still the best predictor of retention you have?",
  ]}
/>

**Exit criteria — and the real finish line.** There isn't one, and that's the point:
by day 180 the plan should have dissolved into an operating rhythm (Chapter 9's
cadence) that doesn't need this chapter anymore. The observable test: growth work
appears in every sprint, decisions cite the funnel, and the team can name last month's
biggest learning from a *failed* experiment without embarrassment.

## 10.7 What good looks like at each gate

Held deliberately loose — your product's numbers are your own (Chapter 2) — but these
are the questions each gate should answer with a yes:

| Gate | The question | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Day 30 | Do we trust the data? | Funnel + retention read aloud, replays match events |
| Day 60 | Does the bucket hold? | Holdout-measured lift on activation or early retention |
| Day 90 | Is acquisition landing in owned channels? | Conversion quality good; % of signups verified/linked |
| Day 180 | Is the loop compounding? | Test tempo steady; lift + blended CAC reported monthly |

[YC's key startup metrics](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/KR-key-startup-metrics)
is a sane external reference for what investors will eventually ask of the same
numbers; [Reforge on North Star metrics](https://www.reforge.com/blog/north-star-metrics)
for keeping the one metric honest as the machine grows.

## 10.8 Before you move on

Three things worth doing while the plan is in front of you:

1. **Day 0 itself.** It's an hour with the whole team, and everything else in the plan
   assumes it happened. The checklist above will keep your place — tick things off as
   they land and they'll be here when you come back.
2. **The four gate reviews, in the calendar** — day 30, 60, 90, 180 from today. A
   30-minute review against the exit criteria at each, with the checklists above open.
   Gates that exist in the calendar get held; gates that exist in a chapter don't.
3. **A look at [your workbook](/workbook)** — everything you've written across the
   course (activation sentence, North Star, tracking plan, hypotheses, these
   checklists) in one place, each entry linked back to the chapter that produced it.
   That page *is* your growth plan now.

And if you're happy to share it — where are you starting from?

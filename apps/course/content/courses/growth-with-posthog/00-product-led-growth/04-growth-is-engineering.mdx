The single biggest mindset change is this: **growth work looks like engineering work.**

A growth team doesn't "do marketing." It runs experiments. The unit of work is a
hypothesis — *"if we email users who haven't created a project within 48 hours, more of
them will activate"* — and the deliverable is a measured result: it lifted activation by
N points, or it didn't, and you keep or kill it on the evidence. That is the same loop as
shipping a feature behind a flag and reading the metric. (The experiments chapter turns
this into a full experimentation program — a scored backlog, a weekly cadence, and the
honesty rules that keep a high tempo from manufacturing false wins.)

It's worth pausing on the anatomy of that hypothesis, because you'll end up writing
dozens in this format:
**if we [specific change], [specific metric] will move [direction], because
[a reason grounded in something you observed]**. Each clause does work. The change is
specific enough to ship. The metric is named before you look, so you can't declare
victory on whatever moved. And the *because* forces the hypothesis to come from your
data — a replay you watched, a funnel step that leaks — rather than from a tactic you
saw on Twitter. A backlog of these is a growth program; a backlog of "try referral
program?" is a wish list.

It's worth saying what this discipline is *not*, because the word "growth" drags a
decade of baggage behind it. It is not a bag of tricks. Elena Verna's
["10 growth tactics that never work"](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/10-growth-tactics-that-never-work-elena-verna)
is the best available list of the anti-patterns — copying a competitor's onboarding,
redesigning the pricing page on instinct, launching a referral program before anyone
loves the product, hiring a growth lead to fix a product problem. What they share:
each substitutes *activity* for the loop. The move that works is boring and repeatable
— find the constraint in the data, form a hypothesis, ship, measure, keep or kill.

**What to take from it:**

- Most "growth tactics" fail because they substitute activity for the loop — they aren't
  aimed at a constraint anyone found in the data.
- The durable pattern is boring and repeatable: find the constraint, form a hypothesis,
  ship, measure, keep or kill. Borrowed tactics can work, but only by luck.

Three consequences follow, and they shape everything later in the course:

- **Every feature ships with instrumentation.** A feature you can't observe isn't
  quite finished — events are part of "done" (the instrumentation chapter).
- **Decisions cite the funnel, not opinions.** "I think users are confused" becomes "37%
  drop between signup and first project; here are five session replays of it happening"
  (the daily-dashboard chapter). The funnel is the shared source of truth.
- **The messaging layer is code, owned by the people who build.** The emails and Discord
  pings that pull users back to value are journeys in your repo, version-controlled and
  code-reviewed — not a config someone edits in a SaaS UI that no engineer ever sees
  (the lifecycle chapters). This is the specific reason the course pairs PostHog with Hogsend.

This is also why a tiny team has an *advantage* here. You don't need a growth department.
You need one loop, instrumented well, run by people who can change both the product and
the messaging. A founder who can read a retention curve and ship a journey is the whole
growth team — and that's enough to start.

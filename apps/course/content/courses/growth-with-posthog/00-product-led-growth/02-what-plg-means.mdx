The term was popularised around 2016 by the VC firm OpenView (Blake Bartlett gets the
credit for the label —
[their canonical explainer](https://openviewpartners.com/product-led-growth/) is still
the reference). The strict definition: **the product itself is the primary driver
of acquisition, activation, retention, and expansion** — people find it, try it, get
value, and upgrade largely on their own, without a salesperson in the loop. Slack, Figma,
Notion, Calendly, Zoom, Dropbox are the textbook cases: a free or low-friction entry, a
fast "aha", and expansion that happens bottom-up as more of a team adopts it.

[Wes Bush's definition](https://productled.com/blog/product-led-growth-definition) — he
wrote [the book with the term on the cover](https://productled.com/book) — adds the
useful sharp edge: PLG is a **go-to-market strategy**, a choice about how you acquire
and convert customers, not a product-quality aspiration. "We care about the product" is
not PLG; "the product does the selling" is.

Set against it are the other go-to-market motions:

- **Sales-led** — a rep drives the deal; the product is demoed, not self-served. Normal
  for high-price, complex B2B.
- **Marketing-led** — demand-gen and content fill a pipeline that humans then convert.
- **Hybrid** — most real companies. A self-serve bottom that feeds a sales-assisted top
  (this is what Slack and Figma actually became at scale). The pattern is now so
  standard that [a16z describes the modern enterprise motion](https://a16z.com/growthsales-the-new-era-of-enterprise-go-to-market/)
  as PLG and sales working the same funnel from opposite ends: the product generates
  and qualifies the demand, sales harvests the accounts where usage is dense. Elena
  Verna — who ran growth at Miro, Dropbox and Amplitude — has the practical version:
  [you add sales *on top of* a working self-serve motion](https://www.elenaverna.com/p/why-when-and-how-to-add-sales-to),
  to expand and enterprise-ify accounts the product already won. Sales added to a
  product that can't activate users just industrialises the leak.

Does the strategy work when it fits? The honest answer is that the public-market
evidence says yes, with selection-bias caveats:
[OpenView's PLG Index](https://openviewpartners.com/product-led-growth-index/) has
tracked product-led public companies trading at a premium to the broader SaaS index —
the market pays more for growth that compounds through the product than for growth
bought with a sales budget. Treat that as directional, not destiny; an index of
successful PLG companies can't count the ones the motion didn't suit.

Which is the honest part, because this course won't sell you a religion: **not every
business should be purely product-led.** If you sell a $100k platform to banks, a
self-serve free tier is not your growth engine, and pretending otherwise rarely ends
well. PLG-as-a-go-to-market is a *choice* that depends on your price, your buyer, and
your product's time-to-value.

But there is a second, broader thing the same words can mean — and it's the one this
course is built on. The next piece is that broader idea.

**What to take from it:**

- The strict definition still holds — the product does the selling — but AI has lowered
  the cost of building the "aha" that a product-led motion depends on.
- PLG-as-strategy is a *choice* tied to your price, buyer, and time-to-value; it isn't a
  moral stance about caring how good the product is.

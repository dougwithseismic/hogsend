AARRR is the default, not the only lens. A growth team should know what else exists so it
picks the right tool for the question:

- **Growth loops (Reforge).** The sharpest critique of AARRR:
  [a funnel is *linear*](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-loops) — people enter at
  the top and fall out the bottom, and you're always refilling the top. A **loop** is a
  system where the output of one cycle becomes the input of the next: a new user
  creates content → that content gets indexed/shared → it brings the next user. Loops
  compound; funnels leak. The funnel is the right lens for *seeing* leaks; the loop is
  the right lens for *designing* growth that doesn't need constant refilling. The
  owned-audience flywheel later in the course is a growth loop. **Use loops when** you're
  designing how growth sustains itself, not just measuring conversion.
- **The Sean Ellis PMF survey.** Before you optimise activation, it's worth knowing if
  you have product-market fit at all. Ellis's test, from
  [the original Startup Pyramid essay](https://seanellis.substack.com/p/the-startup-pyramid):
  ask users "how would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If **40%+ say
  "very disappointed,"** you likely have PMF and should pour into growth; below it, fix
  the product first. A cheap, fast gut-check that complements the retention curve — and
  the gate the 30-60-90-180-day plan puts in front of scaling paid spend.
- **HEART (Google).** Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task-success — a UX-
  quality framework, more granular than AARRR for *product* health. **Use it when** the
  question is "is this feature good," not "is the business growing."
- **The Pirate-metrics critique itself.** Some teams find AARRR too acquisition-shaped
  and prefer to lead with retention (RARRA, sharpened in the leaky-bucket piece) or with
  loops. There's no prize for orthodoxy — the frameworks are lenses, and the honest move
  is to use AARRR to find the leak, loops to design the fix, and the PMF survey to know
  which phase you're even in.

The retention curve deserves one more beat here, because it's the chart that ties the
PMF question to the leaky bucket. Plot the % of a signup **cohort** — everyone who joined
in the same window — still active N days later: every curve falls at first; the question
is whether it **flattens above zero** (some set of users has made your product a habit —
that plateau *is* product-market fit, expressed as data) or decays to nothing (no amount
of acquisition will save it).

**What to take from it:**

- Weebly took ~18 months to reach product-market fit, and the tell was the *shape* of
  the new-users-per-day curve: every press hit (TechCrunch, Newsweek, Time) spiked then
  settled, but kept drifting *down* between spikes — exactly what "no PMF yet" looks like
  as data.
- The metric that matters early is returning usage — the cohort who come back within
  1/3/7/30 days — not signups, which can climb while active users actually shrink.
- His full gut-check is three independent reads: returning usage, an **NPS** (Net
  Promoter Score — a −100 to +100 gauge of how likely users are to recommend you) above
  ~50 (Weebly's was ~80 in the early days), and cohort-based renewal rates over blended
  churn — with the felt sign being customers "beating a path to your door."

What counts as a *good* plateau varies enormously by category — a consumer social app
and an SMB SaaS have utterly different healthy shapes — so before panicking or
celebrating, it's worth calibrating against
[Lenny Rachitsky's operator-surveyed benchmarks](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-good-retention-issue-29)
for what "good" and "great" retention look like in yours.

The point is not to collect frameworks. It's that a growth team reaches for the lens that
answers the question in front of it, instead of forcing every question through one.

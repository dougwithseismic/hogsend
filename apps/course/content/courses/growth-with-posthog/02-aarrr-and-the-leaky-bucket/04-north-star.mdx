AARRR gives you five categories. A **North Star Metric (NSM)** gives you the *one*
number on top — the single measure that best captures the value your product delivers,
and that the whole team can rally around. It's the metric a growth team pins to the top
of the dashboard and reads first.

A good North Star (criteria from
[Amplitude's North Star Playbook](https://amplitude.com/resources/north-star-playbook),
the framework's main steward):

- **Reflects customer value**, not company vanity. It should rise when users get more
  value, not just when more users exist.
- **Expresses your strategy** — someone should be able to read it and roughly
  understand what your product is for.
- **Is a leading indicator** — an early signal of impact, not a lagging financial
  result.
- **Is not directly movable.** If you can move it with one button, it's not a North
  Star. You move it *through* inputs.

Bad North Stars are vanity or lagging metrics: registered users, downloads, page
views, and — importantly — **revenue itself** (ARR/MRR, annual and monthly recurring
revenue). Revenue is the score at the end of the quarter; by the time it moves, the game
is already played. The North Star is the leading thing that *causes* the revenue.

Examples that capture value:

| Product | North Star |
|---|---|
| Airbnb | nights booked |
| Spotify | time spent listening |
| WhatsApp | messages sent |
| PostHog (their own) | "Discoveries" — a user viewing an insight/replay for 10+ seconds |

PostHog's own choice is instructive: they picked "Discoveries" because it predicted
retention 2.3× better than the alternatives. They chose an *activation-quality* signal
over a vanity count — exactly the lesson from the activation piece earlier in this
chapter.

A North Star sits too high for any one team to move directly, so you operate it through
**3–5 input metrics** that teams *can* move.
[Reforge's treatment](https://www.reforge.com/blog/north-star-metrics) formalises this
as the input/output distinction: outputs — the North Star, revenue — are for *watching*;
inputs are for *working on*. The inputs usually map straight onto AARRR stages:
acquisition volume, activation rate, frequency, referral rate.

When the inputs move, the North Star rises, and that's your early warning *before*
revenue reacts. One headline number on top, a handful of inputs beneath it, each owned by
someone — "you own activation rate, you own weekly frequency." That ownership is how a
small team distributes the growth work without needing a growth department.

A worked input tree, for the design-collaboration tool the activation piece used: North
Star = *weekly files collaborated on* (value delivered, leading, not directly movable).
Inputs: new signups (acquisition), % inviting a teammate in week one (activation — the
metric that piece landed on), files-per-active-team (frequency/depth), % of new signups
arriving via a shared file (a growth loop, unpacked in the other-frameworks piece). Four numbers,
four owners, one story: every input plausibly *causes* the North Star, and the North Star
plausibly causes revenue. If the causal chain won't draw for your candidate NSM, that's
usually the sign to pick again.

In 2007 Dave McClure — then at PayPal, later the founder of 500 Startups — gave a
five-minute talk called *Startup Metrics for Pirates*, and it still frames how growth
teams think about a business. His argument was that a startup drowns in metrics, but only
five categories actually decide whether it grows. They spell **AARRR** — **acquisition,
activation, retention, referral, revenue** — which sounds like a pirate growling, hence
the nickname "pirate metrics." Nearly twenty years of blog posts about growth are
downstream of this one talk, so it's worth the five minutes at the source.

**What to take from it:**

- His founder's rule is to pick five metrics, ten at most, and measure them against a
  customer lifecycle you've *hypothesised* — not against a wall of dashboards.
- Don't switch on referral or "viral marketing" until the product is genuinely good; his
  blunt version is to wait until you're not shipping something people would warn their
  friends about, the same retention-before-scaling logic as the leaky bucket.
- His go-to retention lever is automated lifecycle email timed to the user's journey — on
  join, then +3, +7, +30 days — the exact playbook the back half of this course builds
  with Hogsend.

Nearly two decades on it's still the cleanest map of a business there is, and it's the
thing that turns "growth" from a vague ambition into five owned, measurable numbers — the
shared language a growth team actually runs on. The discipline that grew up around it has
its own primary source too: Sean Ellis coined "growth hacker" in
[a 2010 post](https://www.startup-marketing.com/where-are-all-the-growth-hackers/) to
describe "a person whose true north is growth," working through measurable experiments —
exactly the person this course trains you to be. The term later got debased into "growth
hacks"; the original meaning is just the operating model this course is built around.

The five stages read most cleanly each as a question, with what you'd measure underneath
and a single worked example — 4,000 visitors becoming 22 paying customers — carried right
through them:

### Acquisition — *how do people find you?*
The top of the funnel: people arriving and signing up. You watch visitors by channel,
signups, the signup conversion rate, and **CAC** (customer-acquisition cost — what you pay
to win one customer) broken out by channel. Say 4,000 visitors land this month — 2,200
from organic search, 1,000 from a launch post, 800 from ads — and 260 of them sign up, a
6.5% conversion. Already you can see which channel is cheap and which is vanity.

### Activation — *do new users reach the value?*
The first successful experience — the moment the product clicks — and it is *not* the same
as acquisition: acquisition is that they showed up, activation is that they had a good
first visit. McClure's own words were to "turn the drive-by visitors into users who are
somehow enrolled." You measure the activation rate (the share who complete the key first
action), time-to-value, and onboarding completion. Of those 260 signups, 95 create their
first project in week one — 37%. That 37% is the single most important number here, and
the activation atom later in this chapter is about why.

### Retention — *do they come back?*
Activated users returning and keeping the habit — the engine of compounding growth, and
the stage the rest of the framework exists to feed. You measure N-day retention (D1/D7/D30
— the share still active one, seven, and thirty days after signup), the **DAU/MAU ratio**
(daily active users over monthly active users, a measure of stickiness), churn rate, and
where the retention curve flattens. Of the 95 activated users, 60 are still active at day
30 — and crucially the curve *flattens* there rather than sliding to zero, which is the
signal you have a real product (the retention curve gets its own treatment later in this
chapter).

### Referral — *do they bring others?*
Users liking the product enough to invite people. You measure invites sent per user,
invite acceptance rate, the viral coefficient — the **K-factor** (how many new users each
existing user brings in) — and what fraction of new users arrived by referral. Say 60
retained users send 48 invites and 18 convert: a K-factor of 0.3. Below 1 that's a
discount on CAC, not viral growth; above 1 it compounds on its own.

### Revenue — *do they pay, and how much?*
The business finally capturing value. You measure **MRR and ARR** (monthly and annual
recurring revenue), conversion to paid, **LTV** (lifetime value — the total a customer
pays before they leave), and the **LTV:CAC ratio**, the single number that tells you
whether growth is profitable. Of the retained users, 22 upgrade at \$29/mo; with a CAC of
\$40 and an LTV of \$220, the 3:1-plus LTV:CAC says growth here is worth scaling — *once*
the bucket holds.

> A small note that confuses people: some sources list the last two as Revenue then
> Referral. McClure's own slides put **Referral before Revenue**, but it genuinely
> doesn't matter — the acronym is identical (R-R) either way, so it's not worth
> spending energy on.

AARRR is best read as *the five categories of number to watch*, not as a strict
chronological pipeline every user marches through. A user can refer a friend before they
pay; a user can pay before they're really retained. That distinction is what the next
piece is about.

One habit worth building from day one: when the five numbers get written down, put **who
owns each one** next to them. Five numbers with five owners (fine if one person owns three)
is a growth team; five numbers in a doc is a doc.

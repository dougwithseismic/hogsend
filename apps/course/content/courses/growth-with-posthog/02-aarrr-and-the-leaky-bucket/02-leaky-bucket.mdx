Here is the analogy this course returns to over and over.

**Your business is a bucket.** Water poured in at the top is newly acquired customers.
Water leaking out of holes in the bottom is customers who churn. The water level is
your active customer base. ([Amplitude's explainer](https://amplitude.com/blog/leaky-bucket-problem)
walks the same image with cohort charts attached, if you want it drawn.)

There are only two ways to raise the level: **pour faster** (acquire more) or **plug
the holes** (retain more).

Almost everyone reaches for the tap first. It feels like progress — the signup number
goes up, the chart is up and to the right, the launch looks like a success. But if the
bucket is full of holes, pouring faster mostly reveals the problem: you spend more, the
level barely moves, and you've simply *bought churn*. As one framing puts it, "if the
product cannot retain, paid acquisition becomes a way of purchasing churn."

The mobile world makes this brutal. Data popularised by Andrew Chen found the average
app **loses about 77% of its daily users within three days, and about 90% within 30
days** ([his write-up of the dataset](https://andrewchen.com/new-data-shows-why-losing-80-of-your-mobile-users-is-normal/)).
Run the math on that: acquire 100 users at \$5 each, lose 77 of them in three
days, and you've effectively set \$385 on fire — immediately, before you've done
anything wrong except scale a leaky bucket. *(Directional: the 77%/90% figures come
from one widely-cited dataset; treat them as illustrative of the shape, not gospel.)*
Chen's [four-scenario framing](https://andrewchen.com/is-your-website-a-leaky-bucket-4-scenarios-for-user-retention/)
of the same idea is worth internalising: acquisition and retention multiply, so the
same traffic produces wildly different businesses depending on the leak rate.

**This is why the course fixes things in this order: measure → keep → grow.** Plug the
biggest holes (activation and retention) *first*, because retention is the multiplier
on every acquisition dollar. A user who stays is worth more, refers more, and pays
back their acquisition cost — [Brian Balfour's retention essays](https://brianbalfour.com/essays/category/Retention)
make the compounding case in full: retention improvements cascade into every other
AARRR stage, which is why he calls retention the one metric that multiplies rather
than adds. Only once the bucket holds water do you open the tap. This is the same
"retention before acquisition" line you got the team to nod to in Chapter 0 — this is
the argument behind the nod.

### RARRA: the same point, sharpened

Around 2017, Thomas Petit and Gabor Papp reordered the letters to **RARRA** —
Retention, Activation, Referral, Revenue, Acquisition — specifically to break the
habit of starting with acquisition
([Mind the Product's comparison](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/aarrr-vs-rarra-pirate-metrics-explained/)
covers both orderings). Their blunt version: *"If you don't have retention, you have
nothing."* AARRR is the map of a user's journey; RARRA is the order of *priorities* —
what to fix first for the most leverage.

### The honest counterpoint

So you don't over-rotate: the leaky-bucket idea has a credible critique. The
Ehrenberg-Bass Institute [argues it's over-applied](https://marketingscience.info/the-hole-in-the-leaky-bucket-theory/)
— for many categories, acquiring new and light buyers drives growth *more* than
plugging churn, and obsessing only over retention can cap your ceiling. The balanced
reading: **retention-first is the right default for an early-stage product**, but
"retention is the only thing that matters" is an oversimplification. Fix the bucket,
then absolutely turn on the tap (Chapters 7–8). The skill is knowing which phase you're
in — and the retention curve (§2.5) is how you tell.

Numbers make the multiplier concrete. The level a bucket settles at is roughly arrivals
divided by the leak rate, so a few points of monthly retention move the whole base — far
more than the same effort spent widening the top. Put your own figures in:

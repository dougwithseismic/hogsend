Launch turns the pre-launch question — *does anyone want this?* — into a sharper one you
can finally measure: **do the people who show up reach the point where it's useful?**
That point is activation, the first time a new user feels the product's value (Chapter 2).
It's the one metric that matters now, and it's worth protecting from the two numbers that
will try to stand in for it.

**The metric: activation, not signups.** A signup is a person raising their hand; an
activation is a person actually getting something out of the product. Early on, the gap
between the two is enormous — and it's exactly where nearly every launched product leaks.
Say 500 people sign up in your first week and only 40 ever reach the moment the product
pays off: the number worth staring at is the 40, not the 500. If you only watch one
number this stage, watch the share of new users who hit your first-value moment — and use
Chapter 2's method to *find* that moment rather than borrowing someone else's. You don't
need a full funnel yet; you need this one step measured honestly.

**The traps, both tempting at launch.** The first is celebrating signups: the launch-day
spike feels like proof, but a spike of people who never come back is a vanity metric with
good timing. The second is over-instrumenting — freezing the launch to build the perfect
event schema, tracking forty actions when you can't yet read the one that matters.
Chapter 1's warning applies double here: thirty events you don't look at are worth less
than one you act on.

**What "good enough to advance" looks like:** a repeatable activation moment — you can say
what it is, you capture it as an event, and a meaningful share of new users reach it
without you hand-holding each one. A few of them come back a second time unprompted. That
early return is the first faint signal of the thing the next stage is entirely about:
whether the product retains.

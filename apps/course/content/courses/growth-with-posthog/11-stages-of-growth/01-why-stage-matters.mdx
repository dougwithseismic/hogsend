A growth lever isn't good or bad on its own — it's good or bad *for where your product
is right now*. Take paid acquisition. Point it at a product that already keeps its users
and you've built a machine that turns spend into a bigger active base. Run that same
spend a few months earlier, before the product retains anyone, and you're just buying
churn at ad-market prices. The lever didn't change — the bucket underneath it did
(Chapter 2).

That's the whole idea of this chapter. At every stage of a product's life, one problem
matters more than all the rest — the one that, left unsolved, turns work on everything
else into wasted motion. Pre-launch, it's whether anyone wants the thing at all. Just
after launch, it's whether new users reach value. Later, it's whether one channel
repeats. What to care about changes because that problem moves, and pointing your effort
at the last stage's problem is how a well-run team still stalls.

This is also why copying a later-stage company's playbook is a trap. The case study you
read — a Series B company's referral loop, its paid-and-lifecycle engine, its
growth-team org chart — is the answer to a problem that company had *at the time it wrote
the essay*. Run it against your problem and you spend the one thing you can't get back,
time, on the wrong work.

Two forces make this worse. The playbooks you can read are written by the companies that
survived — you never see the identical playbook that sank an earlier-stage startup. And
they're written after the fact, once the messy pre-fit years have been tidied into a
clean narrative. Paul Graham's
[*Do Things That Don't Scale*](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) is the counter-argument
in one essay: the early playbook is *supposed* to look nothing like the scaled one.

So the honest first move isn't picking a tactic — it's naming where you actually are.
The rest of this chapter is one piece per stage. Read your stage first.

**What to take from it:**

- Front-load the "Goldilocks zone" — projects that are both high-impact *and* squarely
  inside your existing skill set — to bank a few quick wins before reaching for unfamiliar
  bets. The same logic as picking the lever that fits your stage, not the one that worked
  for someone else's.
- The plan is deliberately more prescriptive early (a learning roadmap: the product, the
  customers, the growth model) and looser later — you diagnose before you prescribe, the
  personal version of "name where you are before you pick a tactic."
- Guard capacity on purpose. The two things that derail a strong start are picking the
  wrong projects and saying yes to everything until you burn out — so sequence the work,
  don't run it all in parallel.

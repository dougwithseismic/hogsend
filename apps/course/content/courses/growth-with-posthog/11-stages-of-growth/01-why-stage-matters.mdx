A growth lever isn't good or bad in the abstract. It's good or bad *for your stage*.
Paid acquisition into a product that retains is a machine that turns money into a bigger
active base. The same spend, run three months earlier — before the product keeps
anyone — buys churn at ad-market prices. Nothing about the lever changed; the bucket
underneath it did (Chapter 2).

That's the whole idea of this chapter. At every stage of a product's life, one thing is
the binding constraint — the single problem that, if unsolved, makes work on everything
else wasted. Pre-launch, it's whether anyone wants the thing at all. Just after launch,
it's whether new users reach value. Later, it's whether one channel repeats. "What to
care about" changes because the constraint moves, and pointing your effort at last
stage's constraint is how a well-run team still stalls.

This is also why copying a later-stage company's playbook is a trap. The case study you
read — a Series B company's referral loop, its paid-and-lifecycle engine, its
growth-team org chart — is the answer to a constraint that company had *at the time it
wrote the essay*. Run it against your constraint and you spend the one resource you can't
refill, time, on the wrong problem. Two forces make this worse: the playbooks you can
read are written by the companies that survived (you never see the identical playbook
that sank an earlier-stage startup), and they're written after the fact, once the messy
pre-fit years have been tidied into a clean narrative. Paul Graham's
[*Do Things That Don't Scale*](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) is the counter-argument
in one essay: the early playbook is *supposed* to look nothing like the scaled one.

So the honest first move isn't picking a tactic — it's naming where you actually are.
The rest of this chapter is one piece per stage. Read your stage first.

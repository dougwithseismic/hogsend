The arc of this chapter is one idea, repeated at every stage: a different thing is the
problem that matters most, so what you should care about changes as you grow. And the
fastest way to stall is to run a later stage's playbook against an earlier stage's
problem. Each stage comes down to one honest question:

- **Idea and pre-launch** — *Does anyone actually want this, enough to change what they
  do?* The instrument is conversation, not a dashboard.
- **Just launched** — *Do the people who show up reach value, or do they just sign up?*
  Activation, not the signup count.
- **Finding product-market fit** — *Would my users be genuinely disappointed if this
  vanished — and does the retention curve flatten?* Pull, read as data.
- **Early traction** — *Can I turn on one channel and roughly predict what comes out?*
  Repeatability, held to its unit economics.
- **Scaling** — *Is the machine compounding on something solid, or is volume hiding a
  leak?* Capacity without chaos.

And running underneath all five, one more: *which stage am I actually in — and am I
solving its problem, or borrowing someone else's answer?*

## Go deeper

- [YC — Startup Library](https://www.ycombinator.com/library) — the founder-trusted playbooks, sortable by the stage you're in.
- [Paul Graham — Do Things That Don't Scale](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) — why the early-stage playbook is *supposed* to look nothing like the scaled one.
- [Paul Graham — Startup = Growth](https://paulgraham.com/growth.html) — what "growth" actually means, and why stage frames every decision about it.
- [Paul Graham — How to Get Startup Ideas](https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html) — finding a real problem worth the pre-launch stage.
- [Sean Ellis — the PMF survey](https://pmfsurvey.com/) — the 40% test that gates the tap, from the source.
- [Rahul Vohra — how Superhuman built an engine to find product-market fit](https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/) — treating PMF as a repeatable process, not a lightning strike.
- [Brian Balfour — essays](https://brianbalfour.com/essays) — channel/product fit and why the channel that worked for another company probably won't work for you.
- [Lenny's Newsletter](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/) — operator essays across every stage, including how to find your activation metric.
- Marc Andreessen — "The Only Thing That Matters" (2007) — the essay that popularised the term product-market fit (widely archived; search the title).
- Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares — *Traction* — the Bullseye framework for testing many channels cheaply and committing to the one that responds.

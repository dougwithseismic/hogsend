Product-market fit — PMF — is the point where you stop having to push the product and the
market starts pulling it out of your hands. Everything before this stage was setting it
up; everything after it amplifies it, so it's worth slowing down and getting right.

The term was popularised by Marc Andreessen, who described it bluntly as the only thing
that matters before you have it, and the only thing worth working on until you do. The
hard part is that it's a feeling founders mostly describe in hindsight — *"the market
pulling the product out of the startup"* — so the useful move is to turn that feeling into
something you can measure. Here are three ways to read it, from the cheapest to the most
rigorous:

**1. The retention curve that flattens.** Plot the share of a signup cohort — everyone
who joined in the same week, tracked together — still active by week, and watch the shape.
If it decays to zero, no amount of acquisition saves it — you're refilling a bucket with
no bottom. If it *flattens above zero*, some group made the product a habit, and that
plateau is fit expressed as data. This is the reading you already have the instrument for.

**2. The Sean Ellis 40% test.** Survey users with one question — *"how would you feel if
you could no longer use this product?"* — and measure the share who answer *"very
disappointed."* Ellis found, across roughly a hundred startups, that products clearing
about **40%** tended to have the pull to grow, and those under it usually didn't
*(rule of thumb — a useful benchmark, not a law of physics)*. Run yours:

**3. Rahul Vohra's PMF engine.** Superhuman's founder turned that one survey into a
system: segment to the users who'd be very disappointed, learn what they value, and steer
the roadmap to convert the "somewhat disappointed" fence-sitters into them — raising the
40% number deliberately rather than waiting for it. His
[First Round essay](https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/)
is the fullest write-up of treating PMF as a process, not a lightning strike.

The trap here is the most expensive in the course: **scaling spend before fit.** Paid
acquisition amplifies whatever you already have. Point it at a product with real pull and
it compounds. Point it at one people can take or leave and it just buys churn faster — the
same leaky bucket, a product losing users about as fast as it wins them, now with a budget
bolted on. Fit is the gate that decides which of those you get — so keep the tap turned
down until it's real.

You're good enough to advance when the retention curve plateaus, ideally corroborated by
the 40% test, on a segment you can name. Not perfection everywhere — durable pull with
*someone*. That's what the next stage tries to make repeatable.

**What to take from it:**

- The felt signal: *with* fit, customers pull the product out of you — "beating a path to
  your door"; *without* it you're pushing a rock uphill, and users are too polite to say no
  while quietly not coming back. The retention curve is that feeling turned into data.
- Finding it is iteration, not inspiration. Weebly took 18 months, and Rusenko budgets for
  roughly *10× the iterations you expect* — so listen to customers' **problems**, not their
  proposed solutions, and keep burn low enough to afford that many loops.
- His fit signals line up with this atom's readings: returning cohort usage above all, then
  NPS — Net Promoter Score, the standard "would you recommend this?" measure — sitting
  around 40–50, then paying-renewal rates; and he's blunt that signups are a vanity number
  that flatters a product with no retention underneath it.

Product-market fit is the stage everything before it was setting up and everything after
it amplifies. The term was popularised by Marc Andreessen, who described it bluntly as the
only thing that matters before you have it, and the only thing worth working on until you
do. The hard part is that it's a feeling founders describe in hindsight — *"the market
pulling the product out of the startup"* — so the useful move is to make it measurable.
Three readings, from cheapest to most rigorous:

**1. The retention curve that flattens.** Plot the share of a signup cohort still active
by week, and watch the shape (Chapter 2, Chapter 4). If it decays to zero, no amount of
acquisition saves it — you're refilling a bucket with no bottom. If it *flattens above
zero*, some group made the product a habit, and that plateau is fit expressed as data.
This is the reading you already have the instrument for.

**2. The Sean Ellis 40% test.** Survey users with one question — *"how would you feel if
you could no longer use this product?"* — and measure the share who answer *"very
disappointed."* Ellis found, across roughly a hundred startups, that products clearing
about **40%** tended to have the pull to grow, and those under it usually didn't
*(rule of thumb — a useful benchmark, not a law of physics)*. Run yours:

**3. Rahul Vohra's PMF engine.** Superhuman's founder turned that one survey into a
system: segment to the users who'd be very disappointed, learn what they value, and steer
the roadmap to convert the "somewhat disappointed" fence-sitters into them — raising the
40% number deliberately rather than waiting for it. His
[First Round essay](https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/)
is the fullest write-up of treating PMF as a process, not a lightning strike.

**The trap here is the most expensive in the course: scaling spend before fit.** Paid
acquisition amplifies whatever exists — into a product with pull it compounds, into one
people can take or leave it just buys churn faster. Fit is the gate on the tap.

**What "good enough to advance" looks like:** a retention curve that plateaus, ideally
corroborated by the 40% test, on a segment you can name. Not perfection everywhere —
durable pull with *someone*. That's what the next stage tries to make repeatable.

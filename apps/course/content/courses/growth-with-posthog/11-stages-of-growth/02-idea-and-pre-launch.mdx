This is the very start — you've got an idea, maybe a rough prototype, and no users yet.
Because you have no users, there's almost nothing quantitative worth measuring. A
dashboard built now just charts noise: ten signups from friends, a launch-list that
clicked out of politeness. The instinct to instrument early is the right one for *after*
launch (Chapter 1) — here it's mostly a way to feel productive while dodging the only
question that counts.

**The one question: do people actually want this?** Not "would this be cool" — whether a
real person has the problem badly enough to change what they do about it. The way you
find out is conversation: problem interviews, watching how people cope today, and the
strongest signal of all — someone already hacking a workaround, because a workaround is
demand that existed before you showed up.

Say you're building a tool that helps freelance designers chase late invoices. The
question isn't whether people nod when you describe it. It's whether a real designer is
already, today, nagging clients with a messy spreadsheet and a pile of calendar
reminders. That clumsy workaround is the demand — it means the problem hurts enough to
act on. Paul Graham's
[*How to Get Startup Ideas*](https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html) is the reference
for finding a real problem like that rather than a plausible-sounding one.

**The trap has two faces.** One is building in the dark: months of code, no
conversations, a launch that meets a market that was never there. The other is premature
vanity metrics — treating landing-page signups or waitlist size as proof of demand, when
an email address costs nothing to give and predicts almost nothing. CB Insights'
running analysis of startup post-mortems repeatedly puts *"no market need"* at or near
the top of why startups die *(directional — the exact share moves cohort to cohort, but
the rank is stable)*. Both faces of the trap are ways of not asking the question.

**What "good enough to advance" looks like:** you can name the problem, name the person
who has it, and you've talked to enough of them that the demand is observed rather than
assumed. You're not looking for a statistically clean survey — you're looking for a
handful of people who'd be genuinely put out if your solution disappeared, and ideally
one or two using a rough version already. That's the ground the next stage stands on.

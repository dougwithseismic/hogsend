A metric doesn't lie on its own — it lies *at a stage*, when it's the wrong thing to be
reading for the problem you're actually facing. The same signup count that's meaningless
at launch becomes a perfectly good measure of incoming interest later, once activation and
retention underneath it are solid. The skill is spotting which number is quietly standing
in for the one that actually matters.

The pattern runs the whole length of the arc — a different number tries to fool you at
each stage:

- **Pre-launch** — waitlist and landing-page signups pose as demand. But an email address
  is free to give, so a big list predicts almost nothing.
- **Just launched** — the signup count and the launch-day spike pose as traction. A spike
  of people who never return is activity with good timing, not a base.
- **Finding fit** — revenue can rise while the base churns underneath it. New sales mask a
  leaky bucket, so read retention alongside **MRR** — monthly recurring revenue, the
  predictable subscription income you book each month — never instead of it.
- **Early traction** — blended CAC (customer-acquisition cost — what one customer costs to
  win, averaged across every channel) folds cheap organic in with expensive paid, which
  makes paid look affordable when it isn't. Break CAC out by channel before you scale one.
- **At scale** — opens and engagement pose as proof a **journey** (an automated sequence of
  lifecycle messages, like an onboarding email series) works. But activity isn't lift: only
  a **holdout** — a slice of users deliberately kept out of the journey as a comparison
  group — lets you attribute any change to it.

Underneath all of them is Eric Ries' distinction from *The Lean Startup* between *vanity*
and *actionable* metrics. A vanity number tends to only go up (cumulative totals never
fall), can't be tied to a decision, and measures activity instead of an outcome.
Actionable numbers are rates, they're comparable over time, and they change what you'd
actually do next. When a chart makes you feel good but wouldn't change a decision, treat
that as a warning.

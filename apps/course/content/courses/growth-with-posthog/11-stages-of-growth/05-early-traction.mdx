With fit in hand, the job changes from *does this work?* to *can I make it happen again on
purpose?* Early traction is the search for repeatability: a way to bring in activated
users that you can run again next month and roughly predict the result. The temptation
here is to run everywhere at once, chasing every channel you've read a success story
about. The discipline of this stage is the opposite — go narrow before you go wide.

The metric that matters is **one channel that repeats** — not five channels dabbled in,
but one that reliably produces users who activate, at a cost you can live with. Brian
Balfour's [*channel/product fit*](https://brianbalfour.com/essays) argument is that
channels aren't interchangeable: a given product has a small number of channels that
actually suit it, and most of the ones that work for other companies won't work for you.
The book-length version is Weinberg and Mares' *Traction* and its Bullseye framework —
test many channels cheaply, then pour effort into the one that responds. The goal is a
single channel proven before you take on the complexity of a second.

Unit economics start to matter now. Pre-fit, cost-per-acquisition — CAC, what it costs in
marketing spend to win one customer — was noise; here it becomes a real constraint,
because "repeatable" only counts if you can afford to repeat it. Before you call a channel
scalable, it has to survive its own numbers:

The trap is channel-hopping. Trying six channels for two weeks each teaches you almost
nothing — every channel has a learning curve, and abandoning one before you've climbed it
looks like "it didn't work" when the truth is "I didn't finish." Too many simultaneous
bets also means no single channel gets the attention it needs to actually turn. Depth on
one beats breadth across many at this stage.

You're good enough to advance when you have one channel you can turn on and roughly
predict what comes out — activated users, at a CAC your model and runway can carry. Now
adding a second channel is a considered next step rather than a scramble, which is exactly
what scaling asks for.

**What to take from it:**

- His own cautionary tale is channel-hopping in miniature: he spent ~$5k on generic,
  top-of-funnel content *before* launch — the **funnel** being the path from first
  hearing of you down to buying, with "top" the strangers and "bottom" the nearly-sold —
  and got "crickets": not because content doesn't work, but because he had no framework
  for which content to make, so the effort scattered.
- The fix is depth-first. Pick the channel, then prioritise with the five stages of
  awareness and **start at the bottom** — the most-aware buyer (comparison pages,
  alternatives, case studies) — and work up. Bottom-of-funnel content converts soonest, so
  the channel pays back before you've climbed the whole funnel.
- A bonus of going deep on one channel: bottom-of-funnel content doubles as sales
  collateral — it earns its keep even with zero marketing, which is what "a channel that
  repeats" looks like once you've actually committed to it.

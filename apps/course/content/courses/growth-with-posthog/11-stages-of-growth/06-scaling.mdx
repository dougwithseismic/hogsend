Scaling is the stage where growth stops being a run of one-off wins and turns into a
system that produces them on purpose. You already have one channel that repeats and a
product that keeps people. The work now is compounding what you've got — adding capacity
without adding chaos. Three things tend to arrive together here.

The first is a second channel. With one channel proven and its economics understood, a
second is a calculated diversification rather than a scramble — the earlier discipline of
going narrow was for *before* your first channel turned, and now one has. Two working
channels also reduce the risk that a single platform change — an algorithm tweak, a price
rise — takes your whole flow of new users with it.

The second is the first dedicated growth hire. Until now growth has been a hat someone
wears part-time. At scale the loop is big enough to be someone's whole job — owning the
acquisition-to-retention flow, the experiment backlog, and the lifecycle system end to
end. Hire *into* a working loop, not to go find one: a growth hire inherits a machine to
run faster, not a blank page to invent product-market fit on.

The third is an operating cadence. The thing that separates a team that scales from a
team that had a good quarter is ritual: a standing review, a scored experiment backlog,
and decisions that cite the numbers instead of the loudest voice. The cadence is what
keeps a growing team pointed at the same numbers (the later chapters on running growth
turn this into a weekly rhythm).

The trap is scaling chaos. Volume is a magnifier — it makes a good loop bigger and a
broken one break louder. Two specific failures show up here. The first is pouring spend on
top of a retention leak that fit *mostly* fixed but didn't — that's buying churn all over
again, just at a bigger scale. The second is losing the culture that got you this far: the
shared language, the retention-first instinct, the close contact with users, all of it
quietly thinning out under the weight of new people and new channels. The machine is only
ever as good as what it's built on.

Before you pour volume in, it's worth running down the signals that say the base is solid
— retention holding, one repeatable channel, and the unit economics stacking up, so a
customer's **lifetime value** (LTV, the total revenue they bring before they churn)
comfortably clears the cost of winning them:

There's no "advance" past scaling: the job just becomes keeping the machine honest as it
grows, which is what the later chapters on operating cadence and the day-by-day plan are
about.

**What to take from it:**

- The plan assumes you're hired *into* a working growth function, not to invent
  product-market fit. Month one is a learning roadmap — the growth model qualitatively and
  quantitatively, jobs-to-be-done, actually using the product — because you inherit a
  machine and learn it before you touch it.
- The days-30–60 output is the operating cadence this atom asks for: a "growth operations
  manual" defining team structure now and 12–18 months out, operating principles for
  decisions made when you're not in the room, and how the growth team's rituals differ from
  the rest of the company.
- By days 60–90 the job is to re-plan from evidence — revise the strategy, refine the model
  and the dashboards as reality corrects your first guesses. That's the "keep the machine
  honest as it grows" work scaling never finishes.

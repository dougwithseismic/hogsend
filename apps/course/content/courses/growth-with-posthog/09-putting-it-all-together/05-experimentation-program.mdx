The cadence keeps the machine observed; experiments are how it improves. The difference
between a team that occasionally A/B tests and a team with an experimentation *program*
is three artifacts:

**A scored backlog.** Every growth idea — a new journey, a pricing page change, an
onboarding step removed — goes into one list as a **hypothesis** rather than a task:
*"if we [change], [metric] will move [direction] because [reasoning]."* Scoring each on
**[ICE](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/ice-scoring-model)** — impact, confidence,
ease, each 1–10 — lets the scores order the list.
([PIE](https://conversion.com/framework/pie-framework/) — potential, importance, ease —
is the same idea with different letters; either works, and the only real mistake is
debating frameworks instead of running tests.) The score's job isn't precision — it's
letting the *"this would be huge"* claim meet *"how confident are we, actually?"*, and
giving the weekly meeting a top of the list to pull from instead of the loudest idea
in the room.

**A steady tempo.** Sean Ellis — the man who coined both "growth hacking" and the
**North Star** framing (organising a whole team around the one metric that best captures
the value you deliver) this course uses — argues the compounding asset is your
**velocity of tests**: more shots on goal means faster learning, and the learning is
what compounds even when individual tests fail.

**What to take from it:**

- The best growth "hacks" aren't tricks — they're finding where people get confused in the
  product today and making it simpler, which starts with understanding why your must-have
  users actually love it.
- Volume of experiments is the lever, echoing Bezos's line that Amazon's success is a
  function of how many experiments it runs per day, month and year — nobody knows in
  advance which idea will win, so you find the big one by testing many.
- You miss every shot you don't take: running three-to-five tests a week beats polishing
  one "perfect" test, because any single one might be the outsized winner and you can't
  tell which ahead of time.

Reforge's [experiment management
system](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-experiment-management-system) puts a
number on the same idea — a standard template plus a visible backlog roughly tripled
one team's testing velocity — and [First Round's growth-sprint
process](https://review.firstround.com/the-secret-to-running-effective-growth-sprints-follow-this-process-to-learn-faster/)
wraps it in a sprint so every experiment ends in a written learning. For a startup,
"high tempo" might mean one experiment a week. That's fine. One a week, every week,
with results written down, beats a burst of ten followed by a quarter of nothing.

**Honest rules.** Tempo without discipline manufactures false wins, so the program
needs three:

- **Not everything deserves an experiment.** Obvious fixes, reversible changes, and
  changes too small to measure can just ship —
  [Lenny's guide to when *not* to test](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-running-an-experiment)
  is a good checklist. Experiments earn their overhead on decisions that are expensive
  to get wrong.
- **Sample size decides what you can learn.** At startup traffic, most tests can't
  detect small effects — which is why big swings (whole journeys, whole onboarding
  flows) tend to beat button colours, and why "no detectable difference" is an honest,
  common result rather than a failure.
  [Kohavi and Lenny's A/B testing guide](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-ab-testing)
  covers the traps — peeking, underpowered wins, p-hacking by another name.
- **The losers are worth writing down too.** A result only really counts once it's
  recorded — hypothesis, what happened, what you'd try differently. The write-up
  takes thirty seconds; re-running a forgotten failed experiment takes a month.

Where does the *content* of the backlog come from? The machine you built: the funnel's
biggest drop-off, the journey with no measured lift, the channel with decent volume but
poor activation, the session replay where users rage-click.
[YC's growth curriculum](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6k-growth-for-startups)
makes the same loop explicit at whole-company scale: pick the one metric, find the
constraint, test against it, repeat. If the backlog feels empty, the dashboard is being
read too politely.

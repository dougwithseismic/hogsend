Once it's built, it runs on a rhythm, not on heroics. This is the ritual that makes you
a growth *team* rather than a team that did a growth project once (Chapter 0). The
principle — [Reforge calls it your operating
cadence](https://www.reforge.com/blog/operating-cadence) — is that the *rhythm* of
looking is itself a designed system: decide in advance what gets read daily, weekly,
and monthly, and by whom, so attention stops depending on anyone's memory.

**Daily (2 minutes).** The daily job is *noticing*, not analysing: a glance at the
Slack alerts, the activation funnel, and the active-users trend — and the dashboard
itself only if an alert fired. If the daily glance regularly takes more than five
minutes, that's usually a sign the alerts need tuning, not that you need more
discipline.

**Weekly (30 minutes, the growth review).** The one meeting this course asks you to
hold, and it has a fixed agenda — a compressed version of the weekly growth meeting
[Brian Balfour has documented](https://brianbalfour.com/essays/growth-meeting) from
running growth at HubSpot and Reforge:

1. **Metrics read (10 min).** The retention curve and lifecycle chart — is growth real
   or churn-and-replace? North Star vs last week. Anomalies get noted and assigned an
   owner rather than debugged live; group debugging is how a 30-minute meeting becomes
   a 2-hour one.
2. **Journeys and spend (10 min).** Each live journey's send volume and holdout lift;
   ad performance — cost per conversion, EMQ, which audiences are working. This is
   where kill-or-scale decisions get made, out loud.
3. **Experiments (10 min).** What finished (result and learning, a sentence each),
   what launches next from the top of the backlog (the experimentation program), and
   what's blocked.

The discipline that makes the meeting work: it reviews **learnings and decisions**, not
status. "The welcome-nudge holdout shows +4 points activation, we're keeping it and
removing the day-5 email" is a growth review; "we sent 3,400 emails this week" is
theatre.

Two habits tend to close the weekly loop well. Ending the week with **3–5 session
replays** of the biggest drop-off gives you the qualitative *why* behind the
quantitative *where* (Chapter 4). And **writing the week's one learning down**
somewhere permanent is cheaper than it looks — a learning that lives in one person's
head is one the team usually pays to re-discover six months later.

**Monthly (an hour).** Zoom out to the questions that are too slow to see weekly:

- The **activation metric** — is it still the best predictor of retention? Chapter 2's
  correlation-then-experiment method is there whenever you're unsure.
- **Incremental lift** from the lifecycle program (global holdout) — the number that
  justifies the whole Keep phase to anyone who asks.
- **Blended CAC** and the referral loop's contribution. Is growth getting cheaper as
  the owned audience grows? That's Chapter 8's flywheel, showing up in the finances.
- **Pruning.** Journeys with no lift, events no one queries, ad sets that don't
  convert — a growth system accretes cruft exactly like a codebase, and a monthly
  prune keeps it legible.

The cadence is the point. A growth system that's checked on a rhythm survives a busy
quarter; one that depends on someone remembering to look does not.

**What to take from it:**

- Culture change is an artifact, not a pep talk: write an operating manual and a short
  list of team values — "we experiment to find what works" first — and *launch* it
  internally like a feature so the team knows the rhythm is real.
- The cadence is where it lives or dies: reinforce it in the weekly planning meeting
  and 1:1s ("where did you get to question how we've always done things this week?"),
  so it's a standing question, not a one-time talk.
- Lead by dogfooding failure: a leader who stands up and shares an experiment where
  their own hypothesis was flat wrong gives the team permission to run tests that don't
  pan out — which is the whole point of tempo.

Once it's built, it runs on a rhythm, not on heroics. This is the ritual that turns a
one-off growth project into a growth *team* — a team that keeps looking, not one that
looked once. The principle — [Reforge calls it your operating
cadence](https://www.reforge.com/blog/operating-cadence) — is that the *rhythm* of looking
is itself a designed system: decide in advance what gets read daily, weekly, and monthly,
and by whom, so attention stops depending on anyone's memory.

**Daily (2 minutes).** The daily job is *noticing*, not analysing: a glance at the Slack
alerts, the activation funnel, and the active-users trend — and the dashboard itself only
if an alert fired. If the daily glance regularly takes more than five minutes, that's
usually a sign the alerts need tuning, not that you need more discipline.

**Weekly (30 minutes, the growth review).** The one meeting this course asks you to hold,
and it has a fixed agenda — a compressed version of the weekly growth meeting [Brian
Balfour has documented](https://brianbalfour.com/essays/growth-meeting) from running growth
at HubSpot and Reforge:

1. **Metrics read (10 min).** The retention curve and the **lifecycle chart** — PostHog's
   weekly breakdown of how many users are new, returning, dormant or resurrecting — answer
   one question: is growth real, or just churn-and-replace? Then your **North Star**, the
   single metric that best tracks the value you deliver, read against last week. Anomalies
   get noted and assigned an owner rather than debugged live; group debugging is how a
   30-minute meeting becomes a 2-hour one.
2. **Journeys and spend (10 min).** Each live journey's send volume and its **holdout
   lift** — the extra activation or retention it causes, measured against a held-back
   control group. Then ad performance: cost per conversion, **EMQ** (Event Match Quality —
   Meta's score for how well your conversion data resolves to a real person), and which
   audiences are working. This is where kill-or-scale decisions get made, out loud.
3. **Experiments (10 min).** What finished (result and learning, a sentence each), what
   launches next from the top of the scored backlog, and what's blocked.

The discipline that makes the meeting work: it reviews **learnings and decisions**, not
status. "The welcome-nudge holdout shows +4 points activation, we're keeping it and
removing the day-5 email" is a growth review; "we sent 3,400 emails this week" is theatre.

Two habits tend to close the weekly loop well. Ending the week with **3–5 session
replays** — recordings of real user sessions — of the biggest drop-off gives you the
qualitative *why* behind the quantitative *where*. And **writing the week's one learning
down** somewhere permanent is cheaper than it looks — a learning that lives in one person's
head is one the team usually pays to re-discover six months later.

**Monthly (an hour).** Zoom out to the questions that are too slow to see weekly:

- The **activation metric** — is it still the best predictor of retention? The way to check
  is the method that first proved it: correlate the candidate behaviour against retention,
  then run an experiment to confirm it *causes* the retention rather than merely
  accompanying it.
- **Incremental lift** from the lifecycle program, measured against a **global holdout** (a
  slice of users held out of every journey, so the baseline stays honest) — the number that
  justifies the whole Keep phase to anyone who asks.
- **Blended CAC** — your customer-acquisition cost averaged across every channel — and the
  referral loop's contribution to it. Is growth getting cheaper as the owned audience
  grows? That compounding, where a denser owned audience makes each new user cheaper to
  reach, is the flywheel the whole Grow phase exists to start — and this is where it shows
  up, in the finances.
- **Pruning.** Journeys with no lift, events no one queries, ad sets that don't convert — a
  growth system accretes cruft exactly like a codebase, and a monthly prune keeps it
  legible.

The cadence is the point. A growth system that's checked on a rhythm survives a busy
quarter; one that depends on someone remembering to look does not. Andrew Capland — who
ran growth at Wistia — has watched teams build exactly this rhythm and lose it, and his

**What to take from it:**

- Culture change is an artifact, not a pep talk — write the operating manual and a short
  list of team values, then *launch* it internally like a feature so everyone knows the
  rhythm is real.
- The cadence lives or dies in the small rituals: keep "where did you get to question how
  we've always done things this week?" a standing line in planning and 1:1s, not a one-time
  speech.
- Lead by dogfooding failure — a leader who shares an experiment where their own hypothesis
  was flat wrong gives the team permission to run tests that don't pan out, which is the
  whole point of tempo.

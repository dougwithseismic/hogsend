Every team that builds this system brushes against the same handful of failure modes —
not exotic ones, but the predictable ways a good machine quietly stops working. Naming
them is half the defence, because the escape route for each is something you already have
in hand:

- **Scaling acquisition into a leaky bucket.** The most expensive mistake of the set:
  pouring paid traffic into a product that loses users about as fast as it wins them. The
  escape is order — fix retention first, so the users you buy actually stay long enough to
  pay you back.
- **Chasing someone else's magic number.** "Add seven friends in ten days" was *Facebook's*
  activation threshold, not a law of nature. Find your own by correlating candidate
  behaviours against retention and confirming the winner with an experiment; a borrowed
  number is a rally cry, not physics.
- **Building event names dynamically.** Generating event names on the fly —
  `clicked_button_blue`, `clicked_button_red` — is one of the quickest ways to ruin an
  analytics setup, because nothing stays queryable. One fixed event (`button_clicked`) with
  the colour in a property keeps every chart intact.
- **Installing analytics and never looking.** The **dashboard graveyard** — a beautifully
  instrumented product nobody opens. Measurement without a ritual to act on it is theatre;
  the operating cadence, a rhythm of who reads what and when, is what keeps the dashboard
  from going quiet.
- **Experiment theatre.** Underpowered tests "validating" a decision already made, wins
  nobody can reproduce, losers never written down. The fix is counting learnings rather
  than launches, and being honest about what your sample size can actually detect.
- **Trusting the Pixel alone.** The browser **Pixel** — Meta's client-side tracking
  snippet — is blocked or lost for a third of users or more, so it under-counts conversions
  badly. The reliable shape is Pixel *and* **CAPI** (Meta's server-side Conversions API,
  which reports conversions straight from your backend), deduplicated, and fed from wherever
  you actually hold identity.
- **Treating server-side as a consent loophole.** Sending conversions from your server
  doesn't sidestep privacy law: a hashed email is *pseudonymous*, not anonymous, so consent
  still has to be captured before you send it.
- **Measuring opens instead of lift.** An email open doesn't prove the email caused
  anything — plenty of those users would have come back regardless. Only a **holdout**, a
  comparable group you deliberately don't message, tells you the journey's real effect.
- **Renting your whole audience.** If every user lives only on someone else's platform, you
  pay again each time you want to reach them. Capturing identity into channels you own —
  email, Discord, Telegram — is what turns a rented audience into one you can reach for
  free.

None of these is exotic; each is just the system run with one of its disciplines skipped.
The machine rarely breaks loudly — it drifts, one dropped habit at a time.

title: "The hypothesis format"
description: "A test starts life as an opinion. The hypothesis format turns it into something reality can prove wrong — a change, an audience, an outcome, a metric, and the reasoning that ties them together."
workbook: "Write your next experiment as a hypothesis in the standard format — change, audience, outcome, metric, reasoning — so it's falsifiable before you build it."
---

An idea like "let's redesign onboarding" isn't testable — there's no prediction in it. A
**hypothesis** is the same idea written so reality can prove it wrong. The format that
forces this:

> **We believe** [change] **for** [audience] **will cause** [outcome], **measured by**
> [metric], **because** [reasoning].

Each slot does work:

- **change** — the specific thing you'll build. If you can't name it in one line, it
  isn't ready to test.
- **audience** — who sees it: new signups? mobile users? one segment? A change can win
  for one group and lose for another, so naming the audience keeps you honest about which
  you're measuring.
- **outcome** — the behaviour you expect to shift, *with a direction*. "Increases" or
  "decreases," not "affects."
- **metric** — the single number that will settle it (§12.3). Naming it before you build
  is what stops you picking, afterwards, whichever number happens to look good.
- **because** — your reasoning. This is the most valuable slot: it's the *belief* being
  tested, so when the result comes back you learn whether the belief was right — not just
  whether the button moved.

Filled in, it should read like a sentence you could turn out to be wrong about: *We
believe adding a progress bar to onboarding, for new signups, will cause more of them to
finish setup, measured by the setup-completion rate, because seeing how close they are
pulls them through the last steps.* Now there's a real prediction on the table — and
something to learn whichever way the result lands.

Writing the "because" first is what separates a program from a pile of A/B tests. A test
pinned to a belief still teaches you something when it fails; a test with no reasoning
leaves you holding a number and no idea what it meant. Teams that skip the reasoning tend
to run the same failed experiment twice, because nobody wrote down *why* they thought it
would work.

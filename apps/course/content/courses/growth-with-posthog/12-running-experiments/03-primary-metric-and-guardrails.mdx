The most important choice about a test happens before it runs: what would count as
success? The discipline is to answer with exactly *one* metric. Kohavi calls this the **Overall Evaluation Criterion (OEC)** — the single measure the
experiment is optimising, agreed up front. One metric, chosen in advance, is what stops
the after-the-fact hunt for whichever number happens to look good.

The trap is watching everything. If you track fifteen metrics and declare victory when
*any* of them is up, you'll "win" almost every test — some metric is always up by chance.
More metrics feels more rigorous; it's the opposite.

But you can't watch *only* the primary metric, because a change that lifts your target can
quietly wreck something else. That's what **guardrail metrics** are for: a short list of
things the test is not allowed to break, whatever it does to the primary. Common
guardrails are revenue, unsubscribe/opt-out rate, latency and error rates, support
tickets, and core retention. A new onboarding flow that lifts **activation** — the share
of new users who reach first real value — but doubles the unsubscribe rate hasn't won; the
guardrail caught a bad trade before you shipped it.

Two more properties of a good primary metric:

- **Sensitive enough to move.** Revenue and 30-day retention are true but slow — they
  barely budge inside a two-week test. Pick a leading indicator you can actually detect
  moving: activation rate, a key action in week one, the step-completion you're targeting.
- **Tied to the hypothesis.** The primary metric is the "measured by" slot of your
  hypothesis. If the change is about onboarding, the primary metric is an onboarding metric
  — not a downstream number the change only touches faintly, where any movement is mostly
  noise.

One metric that decides, a few that protect: that's the whole discipline, and it's worth
writing down before the test starts rather than after the result tempts you.

---
title: "Reading results honestly"
description: "The result is in — now the ways to fool yourself. P-hacking, the multiple-comparisons trap, reading the losers as carefully as the winners, and turning every test into ship, kill, or iterate."
---

Getting a number is easy; not fooling yourself with it is the hard part — and the ways to
fool yourself are well-catalogued. The first is the **multiple-comparisons trap**: test
enough metrics or enough variants and something will look significant by pure chance,
because at 95% confidence roughly one in twenty comparisons is a false positive even when
nothing is going on. Slice a result twenty ways — "it didn't work overall, but look, it
won for left-handed users in Canada" — and you'll always find a winner. XKCD
[drew this perfectly](https://xkcd.com/882/) with green jelly beans. The defence is the one
primary metric you committed to before the test: it decides the result, and everything
else is a hypothesis for *next* time, not a victory to claim now.

The same disease wears other names. Peeking at a running test, dropping "bad" days,
redefining the metric after the data lands, running until significant — all of it is
**p-hacking**, and all of it manufactures wins. The rule is to fix the metric, the
audience, the run length, and the success bar *before* the test, then read only what you
agreed to read. Kohavi's **Twyman's law** is the right reflex: any figure that looks
surprisingly good is probably wrong until you've ruled out a bug or a fluke.

The losers deserve as much attention as the winners, and there will be more of them. A
flat or negative result is not a wasted test — it's most of what you'll get, since only
about a third of ideas win *(well-attested)*. "No detectable difference" tells you the
effect is smaller than you hoped, which is a real finding: it saves you from shipping the
change and believing it did something. The losers feed the backlog, too — *why* didn't it
move: wrong audience, too small, or a bad belief?

Every test then ends in one of three decisions, and the discipline is to name it out loud:
**ship** it (a clear win, guardrails intact), **kill** it (flat or negative), or
**iterate** (promising but inconclusive — sharpen the change and rerun). Then record it:
hypothesis, result, decision, what you'd try next. The write-up costs thirty seconds;
re-running a forgotten experiment costs a month. And a documented failure is the one kind
of failure a growth team can say out loud without embarrassment.

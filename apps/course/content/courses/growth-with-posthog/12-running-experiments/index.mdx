> **In this chapter:** which changes earn the fixed cost of a test and which you should
> just ship, the hypothesis format that turns an opinion into a claim reality can prove
> wrong, the one primary metric and the guardrails that protect the rest of the
> business, why an
> underpowered "win" is worse than no test at all, holdouts and the incrementality they
> prove, how to read results without fooling yourself, and the ICE-scored backlog and
> weekly tempo that make the learning rate — not any single win — the thing that
> compounds.

**How this chapter works.** It's broken into eight short pieces — most are a one- to
three-minute read. The first six build the discipline of a single trustworthy
experiment; the last two turn it into a weekly habit and check you on the whole thing.
Work through them in order, or jump to what you need:

1. [What deserves an experiment](./01-what-deserves-an-experiment.mdx) — every test has a fixed cost; not every change earns it.
2. [The hypothesis format](./02-the-hypothesis-format.mdx) — the sentence that forces clarity before you build.
3. [Primary metric and guardrails](./03-primary-metric-and-guardrails.mdx) — one number to move, a few you refuse to break.
4. [Power and sample size](./04-power-and-sample-size.mdx) — why an underpowered win is worse than no test.
5. [Holdouts and incrementality](./05-holdouts-and-incrementality.mdx) — opens are activity; lift is proof.
6. [Reading results honestly](./06-reading-results-honestly.mdx) — p-hacking, the losers, and ship/kill/iterate.
7. [Tempo and the backlog](./07-tempo-and-the-backlog.mdx) — an ICE-scored ritual, because learning rate compounds.
8. [Before you move on](./08-before-you-move-on.mdx) — recap, flashcards, and a quiz to check yourself.

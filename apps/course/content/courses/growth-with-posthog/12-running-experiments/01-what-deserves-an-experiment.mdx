An experiment is not free. It costs **traffic** — you split it, so each variant learns
slower and you need more users to reach an answer. It costs **time** — design, build,
wait for significance, read. And it costs **opportunity** — the change you didn't ship
while this one ran. Testing is a way to *buy certainty* about a decision, and like
anything you buy, it's only worth it when the certainty is worth more than the price.

Two questions decide it:

- **Is the change cheap and reversible?** Then ship it. If a clearer button label is
  obviously better and trivial to roll back, a two-week test to confirm it is time you
  won't get back — and at startup traffic the effect is probably too small to detect
  anyway (§12.4).
- **Is the decision expensive to get wrong, and hard to undo?** Then test it. Pricing
  changes, removing an onboarding step, a new activation flow — these are the decisions
  where being wrong costs months, and where certainty is worth paying for.

Lenny's [guide to when *not* to run an experiment](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-running-an-experiment)
is a good checklist: ship without a test when the change is obviously good, too small to
measure at your traffic, or easily reversed; test when the stakes and the uncertainty are
both high.

There's a humbling reason to test the big ones: you're bad at predicting winners, and so
is everyone else. At Microsoft, only about **one-third of experiments moved the metric
they were designed to move** in the intended direction — another third did nothing, and a
third made things worse (Ronny Kohavi's data from Bing/Microsoft) *(well-attested)*. The
famous example is a Bing ad-headline change one engineer left in the backlog for months
because it looked trivial; it turned out to be worth roughly **\$100M a year** (Kohavi,
["The Surprising Power of Online Experiments," HBR](https://hbr.org/2017/09/the-surprising-power-of-online-experiments))
*(well-attested)*. If experts guessed right, you wouldn't need the test. The point of
experimentation isn't to rubber-stamp good ideas — it's to catch the two-thirds that
would have quietly cost you.

So the rule isn't "test everything." It's: ship the cheap, reversible, obvious changes
fast, and spend your limited testing capacity on the decisions that are expensive to get
wrong and where your intuition genuinely might be.

**What to take from it:**

- The failure rates *rise* with optimisation: ~two-thirds of ideas fail at Microsoft,
  ~85% at Bing, 92% at Airbnb search — and every team assumes it's the exception until the
  data humbles it. That's the case for testing the expensive decisions: intuition is wrong
  more often than anyone guesses.
- When *not* to test comes down to sample size. Below ~tens of thousands of users the
  statistics only see large effects, and the "magic" starts around 200k — so a small
  startup should test big swings, not button colours (§12.4). Some calls (a one-time,
  irreversible decision) can't be A/B tested at all.
- Two guardrails from the same humility: don't ship on flat (a no-difference result still
  adds code and maintenance cost for no gain — a no-ship, not a free launch), and invoke
  Twyman's law — a result that looks too good is probably a bug, so investigate before you
  celebrate.

---
title: "Holdouts and incrementality"
description: "An A/B test compares two versions of one change. A holdout answers a bigger question — is the whole program worth anything? — by keeping a slice of users out of everything. The difference is incrementality."
---

An A/B test compares variant A against variant B of a *single* change. A **holdout** asks
something broader: is this whole *program* producing anything at all? The program here is
your **lifecycle program** — the standing set of automated messages that reach users as
they move through their life with the product: the welcome sequence, the activation
nudges, the win-back **journeys** (a journey being one automated message sequence
triggered by what a user does). A holdout keeps a random slice of users out of all of it —
no lifecycle emails, no journeys, nothing — and compares them to everyone who got the full
treatment. The gap between the two groups is **incremental lift**: the outcome that
happened *because* of the program and wouldn't have happened anyway.

This is why **opens are activity; lift is proof.** A 62% open rate tells you the email was
sent and rendered. It tells you nothing about whether the program *changed* anyone's
behaviour — some of those users would have come back regardless. Only a held-out group,
living without the program, shows you the **counterfactual** — what would have happened
anyway. Without it you can't tell "the program worked" apart from "these were people who'd
have converted regardless."

Two flavours worth naming:

- **A/B test** — for a single change. It runs for a while, you ship the winner, and the
  test ends.
- **Global holdout** — a small, *persistent* control (often 1–5% of users) held out of the
  entire lifecycle program continuously, so at any moment you can read the program's total
  incremental contribution. It turns your standing report from "we send a lot of email"
  into one honest line: incremental lift this month, not open rates.

The cost is real and worth saying out loud: a holdout means deliberately *not* treating
some users, which feels like leaving money on the table. It isn't — it's the price of
knowing whether the program pays for itself. A team that can't say what its lifecycle work
adds *over doing nothing* is flying blind, however good the open rates look.
Incrementality is what turns "we send a lot of email" into "the program is worth \$X a
month," and only the second sentence survives a budget review.

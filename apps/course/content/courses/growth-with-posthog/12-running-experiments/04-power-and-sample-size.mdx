Power is the least intuitive idea in this chapter, and it's the one that quietly wrecks
small-team tests — so it's worth slowing down on. **Statistical power** is the chance a
test *detects* a real effect when one exists. A test at low power is a smoke detector with
a flat battery: the thing can be happening and the alarm stays silent.

Underpowered tests are the quiet failure mode of small-team experimentation, and they fail
in two ways. They miss real wins — reporting "no difference" when there really was one.
And they hand you **false wins**: results that look significant but won't replicate. Acting
on a false win is worse than running no test at all, because you ship the change *and*
trust it.

Three levers set how much traffic you need:

- **Baseline rate** — where the metric starts.
- **Minimum detectable effect (MDE)** — the smallest change you care to catch. This one
  dominates: required sample grows with roughly the *square* of how small an effect you
  want to see, so detecting a 1% lift needs about **four times** the traffic of detecting
  a 2% lift *(well-attested — it's arithmetic, not opinion)*. Small effects are enormously
  expensive to prove.
- **Confidence and power** — conventionally 95% confidence and 80% power.

The practical consequence for a startup: you usually can't detect small effects, so **test
big swings** — whole onboarding flows, whole journeys, pricing — not button colours. And
decide the sample size and run length *before* you start, with a calculator like
[Evan Miller's](https://www.evanmiller.org/ab-testing/sample-size.html), so "how long do
we run this?" has an answer that isn't "until it looks significant."

### The cardinal sin: peeking

The temptation is to watch a running test and stop the moment it crosses significance —
and doing that badly inflates your false-positive rate. A test with *no* real
effect will touch significance surprisingly often if you check it every day. The p-value
is only trustworthy at the sample size you committed to. Pick the finish line first, then
look when you reach it. Evan Miller's
["How Not to Run an A/B Test"](https://www.evanmiller.org/how-not-to-run-an-ab-test.html)
shows exactly how much peeking distorts the result.

**What to take from it:**

- False wins are the rule, not the exception. Kohavi's headline Bing metric — sessions per
  user — improves in only about 1 in 5,000 attempts, so because a genuine move is that rare,
  roughly 99.6% of the *significant* (p < 0.05) results on it are false positives. His
  certification committee's standing line: don't celebrate one significant result, replicate
  it first.
- Sample-ratio mismatch is the trust-killer he checks before anything else — the single most
  common alarm at Microsoft. A test built to split traffic 50/50 that comes back even
  slightly lopsided (his live example: 821,588 vs 815,582 users, about a 1-in-500,000
  imbalance by chance) has a flaw in its execution, and no p-value from it can be trusted.
- Peeking and ramp-ups distort the answer directly: combine data while the treatment's
  traffic share is still changing and a winner can flip to a loser (Simpson's paradox). The
  discipline is to fix the finish line first, run A/A tests to sanity-check the system — they
  should "fail" about 5% of the time, and one vendor's once failed 30%, exposing a broken
  engine — and remember Twyman's law: a result that looks amazing is usually a bug.

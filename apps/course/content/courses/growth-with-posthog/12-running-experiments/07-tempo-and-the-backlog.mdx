The difference between a team that A/B tests and a team with a *program* is two rituals: a
scored backlog and a steady tempo.

**The backlog.** Every growth idea — a new journey, a pricing change, an onboarding step
removed — goes into one list as a *hypothesis* (§12.2), not a task. Score each on
**[ICE](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/ice-scoring-model)** — impact, confidence,
ease, each 1–10 — and let the scores order the list.
[PIE](https://conversion.com/framework/pie-framework/) (potential, importance, ease) is
the same idea with different letters; either works, and the only real mistake is arguing
frameworks instead of running tests. The score's job isn't precision — it's to make "this
would be huge" meet "how confident are we, actually?", and to give the weekly meeting a
top of the list to pull from instead of the loudest idea in the room.

**The tempo.** Sean Ellis — who coined both "growth hacking" and the North Star framing
this course uses — argues the compounding asset is your **velocity of tests**. More shots
on goal means faster learning, and the learning is what compounds even when individual
tests fail ([high-tempo testing](https://www.saastr.com/sean-ellis-founder-ceo-of-growthhackers-building-a-company-wide-growth-culture-video-transcript/)).
Reforge's [experiment management system](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-experiment-management-system)
puts a number on it — a standard template plus a visible backlog roughly tripled one
team's testing velocity *(directional)*. For a startup, "high tempo" might mean one
experiment a week. That's fine: one a week, every week, with results written down, beats a
burst of ten followed by a quarter of nothing.

The thing that holds it together is a 30-minute **weekly review**: groom the backlog,
launch the top-scored test, read last week's, record the decision.

**What to take from it:**

- Ellis leads with the Amazon framing — Bezos: success is "a function of how many
  experiments we run per day, per month, per year." Volume of tests, not the brilliance of
  any one, is the thing that compounds.
- The mistake he warns against is obsessing over one "perfect" test. Because nobody
  reliably knows which idea wins, three-to-five smaller shots a week beat betting the
  quarter on a single swing — the shots-on-goal (Gretzky) logic behind this atom's tempo.
- Velocity only pays off when it's aimed. He starts every cycle with qualitative analysis
  of *who loves the product and why*, so the backlog is fed by real understanding of value,
  not random ideas — which is the ICE-scored hypothesis list this atom builds.

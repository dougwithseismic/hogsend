Referrals are where the owned graph pays off most, because **a referral program only
works if you can attribute it** — and attribution is exactly what the graph gives you.
The prize is real:
[Lenny Rachitsky's survey of referral programs](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/this-week-16-building-a-referrals)
found they commonly drive **15–50% of new-user growth** at companies that get them
right — and, in the same breath, that most programs see **under 10% of users ever
refer anyone**. Both numbers matter: the upside is large, and the median program
underdelivers because it was bolted on rather than designed.

### Incentive structures that work

- **Double-sided (give-get).** Reward *both* the referrer and the new user. It removes
  the "I'm asking a favour" friction. **Dropbox** is the canonical case: 500 MB of
  storage to *each side* per referral, capped at 16 GB. The reward was the product's own
  core value, the ask was the last step of onboarding, and it drove **~3,900% growth in
  15 months.** (The backstory: paid ads were costing Dropbox far more than a customer was
  worth, so a product-value referral was dramatically cheaper.)
- **Milestone / tiered.** An escalating ladder of rewards — **Morning Brew**'s newsletter
  referral program (stickers → shirt → bigger swag → trip to HQ) is the model, with a
  leaderboard for status. Near-zero marginal cost per reward; great for community.
- **Status / access.** For pre-launch and web3/games, the reward is *earlier access*, a
  *whitelist spot*, or an *exclusive role* — scarcity instead of cash. **Robinhood**'s
  skip-the-line waitlist is the classic.

The design principles that run across all of them: sharing that costs nothing
(prewritten copy, a one-click link), rewards on both sides, a reward aligned to product
value, visible progress (a dashboard or leaderboard), and **an ask timed to peak
engagement** — right after the aha moment, which is the referral journey from Chapter
6, §6.8.

The two levers a loop actually turns on are *invites sent per user* and *the share of
invites that convert* — their product is the k-factor. Below 1 a loop doesn't sustain
itself, but it still quietly multiplies every other channel; at or above 1 you have true
organic virality. See what your assumptions imply before you build:

### The honest failure mode: referred users can be your worst users

Before settling on a reward, it's worth sitting with Andrew Chen's
[counterpoint](https://andrewchen.com/worst-users-referral-trials-coupons-gamification/):
users acquired through heavy incentives — referral bounties, coupons, gamified
rewards — are often the *lowest-quality* cohort you acquire, because the incentive
(not the product) did the selecting. The symptoms: referred users who never activate,
reward-farmers inviting throwaway accounts, and a referral "channel" whose cohort
retention embarrasses every other source.

The defences are all things this course already gave you:

- **Cohorting the referred users** (Chapter 4) and comparing their retention against
  other channels — a referral program is judged on retained users, not invites sent.
- **Rewarding verified behaviour, not signups.** When the referee has to verify email,
  link an account, or hit a real activation milestone before the reward fires, farmers
  lose interest. This is exactly the anti-fraud power the identity graph gives you
  (below).
- **A reward aligned to product value** (Dropbox's storage, not cash), so the people
  the incentive attracts are people who wanted the product anyway.

### Why the owned graph makes attribution work

Cookie-based referral tracking breaks on the exact journey referrals take: a friend
shares a link, the recipient clicks on their phone, then signs up on a laptop weeks
later. Last-click misses it; the referrer goes uncredited; the program looks like it
doesn't work and gets cut.

An owned identity graph fixes this:

- First-party referral codes/links are tracked **independent of third-party cookies**,
  so you capture the conversion even as browsers tighten.
- The graph **resolves the new user across devices** and **back-credits the referrer**
  when the anonymous visitor finally identifies (gives email / links Discord).
- You can enforce **anti-fraud rules** — the referee must verify their email or link an
  account or hit a real milestone before the reward fires — because the *whole graph is
  yours*. A Discord-linked referral resolves to a real verified person, not a cookie.

And the natural community fit closes the loop: **"X verified referrals → unlock a Discord
role"** puts the reward *in the owned channel*, which densifies the graph further. The
reward for growing your audience is itself a deeper hook into your audience.

Hogsend ships a referral v1 on exactly this shape: `visited → converted → credited`, two
journeys plus a Discord role granted at a milestone — all on its own identity graph, no
cookies required.

### Build vs buy: the referral tooling landscape

You don't have to build referral attribution from scratch, and a growth team should know
the off-the-shelf options:

- **Rewardful / GrowSurf / Referral Rock / Viral Loops** — hosted referral SaaS, fast to
  stand up, with dashboards and reward logic out of the box. **Buy when** you want a
  program live this week and a simple link-based attribution is enough.
- **PartnerStack / FirstPromoter** — affiliate/partner-program flavoured, for paid
  referrers and influencers rather than user-to-user.
- **Build it on your own graph (Hogsend's approach)** — **worth it when** cross-device
  and cross-channel attribution matters, when the reward lives in your product or
  community (a role, credits, access), and when you want anti-fraud tied to real verified
  identity. The hosted tools mostly rely on cookies/links and can't resolve the
  phone-click → laptop-signup gap; your graph can.

The honest split: a hosted tool is the right first move for a simple cash/credit referral
on a single channel. The owned-graph build wins precisely when referrals are
cross-device, community-rewarded, or fraud-prone — which is most of the interesting cases
for the products this course is written for.

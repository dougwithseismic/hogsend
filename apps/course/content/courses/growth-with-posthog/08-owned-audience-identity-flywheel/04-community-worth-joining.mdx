A warning before the next code block, because this is where most "build a Discord"
advice quietly fails: **the linking mechanic captures identity; it does not create a
community.** A server with a verification gate, twenty channels, and no conversation is
a list with extra steps — and everyone who joins can *see* it's dead, which a mailing
list at least hides.

The practitioners' consensus (Jono Bacon, who wrote the standard books on this, is the
clearest voice) compresses to:

- **Start with a mission, not a server.** People join a movement or a topic, not your
  product's support channel. Frame the community around the thing your users are trying
  to *become* — better at growth, better at shipping — with your product as the venue.
- **Fewer channels than feels right.** Three active channels beat twenty empty ones.
  Conversation density is the product; split channels only when volume forces it.
- **Rituals create the habit.** A weekly show-and-tell, a changelog thread, an office
  hour — recurring, hosted moments give members a reason to come back on a schedule
  (the community version of Chapter 9's operating cadence).
- **The first 100 members are hand-recruited.** Like Chapter 7's do-things-that-don't-
  scale: personally invite, personally welcome, personally introduce members to each
  other until conversation is self-sustaining.

**What to take from it:**

- A community isn't a platform, it's a belief system — "platforms are where people talk;
  movements are why they care." Define your creed (the principles your audience already
  holds), your non-believers (who does it the wrong way), and your rituals (repeatable
  actions) *before* you open any server.
- Don't start with a platform — start with an event. Gather even ten people into a live
  session to share and learn, *then* give them the lightest possible digital home on
  whatever platform they already use (Discord, Slack, even a mailing list).
- Engagement is orchestrated, not automatic: seed two or three easy conversation-starters
  a week, then do the "awkward small talk" — follow up, make introductions, broker
  connections — and measure DAU/MAU as the heartbeat.

**What to take from it:**

- Foundation first: state a clear purpose in the welcome channel, build a real onboarding
  experience (start-here channel, FAQ, a "how did you find us?" prompt), and keep channels
  few — merge or archive underused ones so conversation stays dense.
- Drive engagement with recurring rituals: scheduled events with reminders, polls for
  feedback, celebrating member milestones, icebreakers, and channels for user-generated
  content and contests.
- Let bots do the heavy lifting: welcome bots, automated moderation, reaction-roles for
  self-service interests, gamified XP/leaderboards, and stat bots — then share the metrics
  back to the community to build momentum.

The measurement lens from this course applies here too: instrument the community like
a product. `member.joined`, first message, reactions given/received — Hogsend's
connector events — give you an activation funnel for the community itself (what
fraction of joiners post within a week?) and feed the engagement-role journeys above.
[Discord's own community resources](https://discord.com/community) and the
[CMX community-building guide](https://www.cmxhub.com/reports/the-cmx-guide-to-building-a-community)
are the fuller playbooks.

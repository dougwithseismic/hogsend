**The linking mechanic captures identity; it does not create a community.** A server
with a verification gate, twenty channels, and no conversation is *a list with extra
steps* — and worse than a list, because everyone who joins can *see* it's dead, which a
mailing list at least hides. The tooling delivers members; it cannot deliver a reason for
them to talk to each other. That reason is the whole job of this piece.

The practitioner who wrote the standard books on this, Jono Bacon, is the clearest voice
on what keeps a server alive, and the consensus he anchors comes down to four moves. It
starts with a **mission, not a server** — people join a movement or a topic, not a
product's support channel, so you frame the community around what your users are trying
to *become* (better at growth, better at shipping) with your product as the venue. It
runs on **fewer channels than feels right** — three active channels beat twenty empty
ones, because conversation density is the product, and you split a channel only when
volume forces it. It's held together by **rituals** — a weekly show-and-tell, a changelog
thread, an office hour: recurring hosted moments that give members a reason to come back
on a schedule (the operating-cadence chapter makes the same case for a company's own
week). And it's seeded by hand — **the first hundred members are hand-recruited**, the
community version of doing the things that don't scale: personally invited, personally
welcomed, personally introduced to each other until the conversation sustains itself
without you.

**What to take from it:**

- **A community is a belief system, not a platform** — "platforms are where people talk;
  movements are why they care." Define your creed and your rituals before you open a
  single channel.
- **Start with an event, not a server** — gather even ten people into one live session to
  share and learn, then give them the lightest possible home on a tool they already use.
- **Engagement is orchestrated, not automatic** — seed a couple of conversation-starters
  a week and broker the introductions yourself, watching daily-over-monthly active users
  (DAU/MAU) — the share of members who show up on a given day — as the heartbeat.

The movement comes first, but a movement still needs a room to happen in. Bacon's second
talk is about that room — the concrete server mechanics for once you're past the mission

**What to take from it:**

- **Foundation before tactics** — a clear purpose stated in the welcome channel, a real
  start-here onboarding path, and few enough channels that conversation stays dense.
- **Rituals carry the engagement** — scheduled events with reminders, polls for feedback,
  celebrated member milestones, and channels for member-made content.
- **Let bots do the heavy lifting** — welcome flows, reaction-roles for self-service
  interests, and gamified XP, with the stats shared back to the room to build momentum.

The measurement discipline from the rest of this course applies here too: instrument the
community like a product. `member.joined`, first message, reactions given and received —
Hogsend's connector events — give you an *activation* funnel for the community itself (the
share of joiners who reach first value, here a first post within a week) and feed the same
engagement-role journeys the Discord flow grants roles with.
[Discord's own community resources](https://discord.com/community) and the
[CMX community-building guide](https://www.cmxhub.com/reports/the-cmx-guide-to-building-a-community)
are the fuller playbooks. The tooling builds the room in an afternoon; filling it is the
year of work, and the part no bot does for you.

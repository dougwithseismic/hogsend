Every link event writes an **edge** between two ways of reaching the same person:

```
email ↔ Discord ID ↔ Telegram ID ↔ wallet ↔ anonymous web id ↔ PostHog person
```

Collect enough edges and you have an **identity graph**: one unified profile that spans
devices, sessions, and channels. The payoffs stack:

- **Retroactive stitching.** When an anonymous visitor finally gives an email or links
  Discord, all their prior touchpoints attach to the now-known profile — so attribution
  works on data *you own*, independent of cookies.
- **Channel choice on reactivation.** A lapsed user isn't reachable only by an ad you
  must re-buy — you can email them, ping them in Discord, or DM them on Telegram. The
  denser the graph, the more ways back in, and the lower your cost to re-reach anyone.
- **Better ads, legally.** Those verified, consented emails are the cleanest inputs an
  ad platform can take — a **custom audience** (your own contacts uploaded so you target
  exactly those people) and a **lookalike seed** (that same list handed to the platform
  as an example, so it finds new strangers who resemble your customers). Because each
  address is confirmed and consented, it scores high on **event match quality** (EMQ —
  the platform's 1–10 rating of how well a reported conversion matches a real person),
  which makes the whole ad machine cheaper to run. The graph you build here is the fuel
  the driving-traffic chapter's ads run on.

There's a strategic layer on top of the operational one. Chris Dixon's
[come for the tool, stay for the network](https://cdixon.org/2015/01/31/come-for-the-tool-stay-for-the-network/)
describes the classic ladder: a single-player tool earns the user, and the network they
join is what keeps them (and keeps competitors out). A linked community sits exactly on
that ladder — the product is the tool; the community and its graph are the network. And
a16z's [Hidden Networks](https://a16z.com/hidden-networks-network-effects-that-dont-look-like-network-effects/)
makes the subtler point that defensible networks often don't look like social networks —
an identity graph connecting your users' channels, reachable by you and no competitor,
is precisely such a hidden network. Features can be copied in a quarter; a dense,
consented identity graph has to be earned edge by edge.

Hogsend builds this graph on its **own** identity model (it stitches email ↔ channel ↔
anonymous), and its connectors emit the engagement events — `discord.linked`,
`reaction_added`, `member.joined`, `link.clicked` — that both trigger journeys *and* add
edges. The graph is the hub of the flywheel; every channel you link spins it faster. And
because it's *yours* — not a rented list inside someone else's platform — it's the
compounding asset a growth team is actually building over time. Features come and go;
the identity graph only gets denser.

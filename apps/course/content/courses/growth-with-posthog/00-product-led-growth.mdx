## 0.1 The shift this course is really about

The tools in this course — PostHog, Hogsend, a Meta pixel — are the easy part. You can
install all three in an afternoon. The hard part, and the thing that actually moves the
business, is a change in how you *operate*: you stop treating growth as a thing the
marketing person does over there, and you start treating it as a system your whole team
builds, measures, and improves — the same way you build, measure, and improve the
product itself.

That is what "product-led growth team" means in this course. Not a job title, not a
department, not a SaaS category you have to belong to. It's a way of running a startup
where **the product and its usage data are the engine of growth**, and where growth is an
engineering discipline — instrumented, measured, iterated — rather than a budget you hand
to an agency and hope.

The rest of the course is the concrete version of that idea: measure honestly (Chapters
1–4), keep the users you earn (Chapters 5–6), then grow into an audience you own
(Chapters 7–8). This chapter is the *why* underneath all of it.

## 0.2 What "product-led growth" actually means

The term was popularised around 2016 by the VC firm OpenView (Blake Bartlett gets the
credit for the label). The strict definition: **the product itself is the primary driver
of acquisition, activation, retention, and expansion** — people find it, try it, get
value, and upgrade largely on their own, without a salesperson in the loop. Slack, Figma,
Notion, Calendly, Zoom, Dropbox are the textbook cases: a free or low-friction entry, a
fast "aha", and expansion that happens bottom-up as more of a team adopts it.

Set against it are the other go-to-market motions:

- **Sales-led** — a rep drives the deal; the product is demoed, not self-served. Normal
  for high-price, complex B2B.
- **Marketing-led** — demand-gen and content fill a pipeline that humans then convert.
- **Hybrid** — most real companies. A self-serve bottom that feeds a sales-assisted top
  (this is what Slack and Figma actually became at scale).

Here's the honest part, because this course won't sell you a religion: **not every
business should be purely product-led.** If you sell a $100k platform to banks, a
self-serve free tier is not your growth engine, and pretending otherwise is a mistake.
PLG-as-a-go-to-market is a *choice* that depends on your price, your buyer, and your
product's time-to-value.

But there is a second, broader thing the same words can mean — and it's the one this
course is built on.

## 0.3 Product-led as an operating model (this is the universal part)

Strip away the SaaS specifics and "product-led" describes a *way of working* that applies
to almost any startup, **whatever it is**:

> You instrument what users do, you read those signals honestly, you form hypotheses
> about where you're losing people, you ship changes — in the product *and* in how you
> message users — and you measure whether the change actually moved the metric. Then you
> do it again.

That loop doesn't care whether you're self-serve or sales-assisted. A marketplace, a
consumer app, a dev tool, a Discord-native game, an e-commerce brand, even a productised
service — all of them have users doing measurable things and a relationship that can be
deepened. All of them can be run by a team that treats growth as a measurable system
instead of a vibe.

So when this course says "treat your startup as a product-led growth team," it means:
adopt the *operating model* — the instrumentation, the honesty about metrics, the
build-measure-learn loop, the ownership — even if your go-to-market is hybrid or
sales-assisted. You don't have to be a PLG *company* to work like a PLG *team*.

| Your startup is… | The product-led move that still applies |
|---|---|
| A self-serve SaaS | The classic case — free entry, fast aha, bottom-up expansion |
| A marketplace | Instrument both sides; activation = first successful match/transaction |
| A consumer app | Retention *is* the business; lifecycle messaging is the whole game |
| A dev tool | Content + community + a product that proves itself; paid ads are weak (Ch 7) |
| A community / web3 game | Identity-linking for perks *is* your activation and your moat (Ch 8) |
| E-commerce | Behavioural flows (cart, browse, post-purchase) over batch blasts (Ch 5) |
| A productised service | Even here: measure where leads stall, automate the follow-up, own the list |

The tactics shift by type; the operating model doesn't.

## 0.4 Growth is an engineering discipline, not a department

The single biggest mindset change is this: **growth work looks like engineering work.**

A growth team doesn't "do marketing." It runs experiments. The unit of work is a
hypothesis — *"if we email users who haven't created a project within 48 hours, more of
them will activate"* — and the deliverable is a measured result: it lifted activation by
N points, or it didn't, and you keep or kill it on the evidence. That is the same loop as
shipping a feature behind a flag and reading the metric.

Three consequences follow, and they shape everything later in the course:

- **Every feature ships with instrumentation.** If you can't see whether people use it,
  you didn't finish it. Events are part of "done" (Chapter 3).
- **Decisions cite the funnel, not opinions.** "I think users are confused" becomes "37%
  drop between signup and first project; here are five session replays of it happening"
  (Chapter 4). The funnel is the shared source of truth.
- **The messaging layer is code, owned by the people who build.** The emails and Discord
  pings that pull users back to value are journeys in your repo, version-controlled and
  code-reviewed — not a config someone edits in a SaaS UI that no engineer ever sees
  (Chapters 5–6). This is the specific reason the course pairs PostHog with Hogsend.

This is also why a tiny team has an *advantage* here. You don't need a growth department.
You need one loop, instrumented well, run by people who can change both the product and
the messaging. A founder who can read a retention curve and ship a journey is the whole
growth team — and that's enough to start.

## 0.5 Who owns growth on a small team

"Treat your startup as a growth team" raises an obvious question on a four-person company:
*who?* The answer is not "hire a growth lead." It's **assign the hat, and make the ritual
cheap.**

- **Name one owner.** One person is accountable for the funnel being instrumented, the
  dashboard being read, and the journeys being live. It can be a founder. It's a hat, not
  a hire.
- **Make growth a standing ritual, not a project.** The cadence (built in full in Chapter
  9): a 2-minute daily glance at alerts, a 30-minute weekly read of retention + journeys +
  ad spend, a monthly review of incremental lift and blended CAC. A system that runs on a
  rhythm survives; one that runs on heroics dies the first busy week.
- **Give the whole team the shared language.** Everyone should know the North Star
  (Chapter 2) and what "activated" means for your product. When the same five numbers mean
  the same thing to everyone, you've already beaten most companies.

The maturity ladder most startups climb: **flying blind** (no data) → **analytics
installed but no one looks** → **someone reads a dashboard** → **the team acts on it
automatically through lifecycle journeys** → **growth compounds through an owned audience
and referrals**. This course walks that ladder rung by rung; the goal is the top of it.

## 0.6 The machine you're going to build

Here's the whole system on one page, so the chapters that follow have somewhere to hang.
Three layers, one loop:

- **See it — PostHog (Chapters 1–4).** Collect events, identify people, and read the
  funnel, retention, and the daily dashboard. You can't fix a leak you can't see.
- **Act on it — Hogsend (Chapters 5–6).** Code-first lifecycle journeys that reach the
  right user at the right moment — welcome, nudge, win-back, dunning, referral — triggered
  by the same events you measure, and fired back as events so the loop stays measurable.
- **Own it — identity + ads (Chapters 7–8).** Drive traffic, then capture every visitor
  into a channel you own (verified email, linked Discord/Telegram) on an identity graph,
  so you never pay to reach the same person twice — and so your ads and referrals compound.

The division of labour that keeps it simple no matter how much you add: **PostHog detects,
Hogsend acts, you decide.** Hold that split and the system stays legible.

| Layer | Tool | Chapters | The question it answers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Decide what matters | You (AARRR, North Star) | 0, 2 | What are we even optimising? |
| See it | PostHog | 1, 3, 4 | Where are we losing people? |
| Act on it | Hogsend | 5, 6 | How do we pull them back, automatically? |
| Own & grow it | Ads + Hogsend connectors | 7, 8 | How do we acquire without renting our audience? |
| Run it | The operating cadence | 9 | How does this become a habit, not a project? |

## 0.7 How teams get this wrong

Four failure modes, each one a way of *not* being a growth team. You'll recognise at
least one from a company you've worked at:

- **The vibes startup — no data at all.** Decisions by intuition and the loudest voice in
  the room. Fast at first, blind at scale; you can't tell a real improvement from noise.
  The fix is Chapters 1–4.
- **The dashboard graveyard — analytics installed, nobody looks.** PostHog is firing, the
  charts exist, and no decision has ever changed because of them. Measurement without a
  ritual and without *action* is theatre. The fix is the daily cadence (Ch 4) plus the
  action layer (Ch 5–6).
- **The blast list — action without measurement.** A weekly newsletter to everyone, batch
  sends, no behavioural triggers, no holdouts. It feels like marketing and proves nothing.
  The fix is triggered journeys and incrementality testing (Ch 5).
- **The rented-audience trap — growth you don't own.** All acquisition through paid ads,
  every reach re-bought, nothing captured into a channel you control. The moment you stop
  paying, the pipeline goes dark. The fix is the owned-audience flywheel (Ch 8).

The through-line: a growth team **measures honestly, acts automatically, and owns its
audience.** Miss any one of the three and you've got a department, not a system.

## 0.8 The one rule: measure → keep → grow

If you take one thing from this whole course, take the order.

Almost every founder's instinct is to reach for acquisition first — run ads, drive
signups, watch the top number climb. It's the most expensive instinct there is, because
pouring traffic into a product that can't keep users is, as Chapter 2 will put it, just
*buying churn*. Acquisition is the **last** lever, not the first.

So the sequence is deliberate, and the course follows it exactly:

1. **Measure** what's actually happening (Chapters 1–4) — so you're working on the real
   leak, not a guessed one.
2. **Keep** the users you already earn (Chapters 5–6) — activation and retention, the
   highest-leverage and cheapest growth you have, because those users are already yours.
3. **Grow** into an audience you own (Chapters 7–8) — open the tap only once the bucket
   holds water, and make every drop land somewhere you can reach again for free.

That's the whole philosophy. The next eight chapters are the build.

## 0.9 Do this now

You can't install a mindset, but you can commit to it before you touch a line of code:

1. **Name the growth owner** — one person accountable for measure → keep → grow. (On a
   solo team, that's you; put it in writing anyway.)
2. **Write down what "activated" means for your product** in one sentence — the moment a
   new user first gets real value. You'll make it precise in Chapter 2; commit to the
   concept now.
3. **Agree the order with your team:** retention before acquisition. The most important
   line you can get everyone to nod to before you spend a cent on ads.

Then turn the page: Chapter 1 is the tool you'll measure with, and why measuring is the
job at all.

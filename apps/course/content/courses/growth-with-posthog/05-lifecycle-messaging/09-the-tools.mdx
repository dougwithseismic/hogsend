The messaging-tool landscape is crowded, and a growth team should choose deliberately
rather than default into whatever the last company used. This course builds journeys in
Hogsend, but the honest map is worth knowing:

- **Customer.io** — the closest "serious" behavioural-messaging tool: event-triggered,
  multi-channel, strong segmentation. A genuinely good choice, especially for a
  marketing-owned program. Trade-off: your lifecycle logic lives in their UI, not your
  repo.
- **Loops.so** — clean, developer-friendly transactional + lifecycle email; a lovely
  developer experience (DX — how pleasant a tool is to build on) for simple flows.
  Trade-off: less powerful when your branching gets complex.
- **Klaviyo** — the e-commerce default; superb for cart/browse/post-purchase flows on
  Shopify. If you're commerce, start here. Weaker fit for product-event-driven SaaS.
- **Braze / Iterable** — enterprise-grade multi-channel orchestration. Powerful,
  expensive, and overkill until you're at real scale with a marketing team.
- **Intercom** — strong when messaging and support live together (in-app + email +
  inbox). You pay for the suite.
- **Resend Broadcasts / Mailchimp / ConvertKit** — broadcast-first. Fine for a
  newsletter; not built for behavioural, per-user journeys.
- **PostHog Workflows** — no-code, lives next to your data, good for batch + simple
  drips (introduced back in the PostHog chapter).

**When a no-code tool is the right call:** if a non-engineer owns messaging, if your
flows are simple drips, or if you need to ship something this week without touching the
codebase — Customer.io or Loops will serve you well, and there's no virtue in code for
its own sake.

**Why this course is code-first (Hogsend):** because for a product-led growth team — one
that treats growth as an engineering discipline rather than a budget — the messaging
layer *is* part of the product, and it benefits from the same things your product code
does: version control, code review, real branching logic, testing, and exactly-once
guarantees. When your journey needs to *read* arbitrary event history and *branch* on it,
a flowchart UI becomes the bottleneck; code doesn't. And the identity graph that makes
owning your audience work is much easier when the messaging engine is yours. The
trade-off is real and you should make it with eyes open: code-first asks an engineer to
own lifecycle. For the audience of this course, that's a feature, not a tax.

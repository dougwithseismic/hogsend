A message only earns its result if it reaches the inbox — and reaching the inbox is
earned, not given. Since early 2024 it's also *specified*. Gmail and Yahoo's bulk-sender
rules ([Google's sender guidelines](https://support.google.com/mail/answer/81126) are
the primary source;
[Resend's summary](https://resend.com/blog/gmail-and-yahoo-bulk-sending-requirements-for-2024)
is the readable version) turned the previously-soft best practices into hard
requirements:

- **Authenticate everything: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC.** These are DNS records proving
  your mail actually comes from your domain. No auth, no inbox — it's that binary now
  for senders above trivial volume.
- **One-click unsubscribe** (the `List-Unsubscribe` header), honoured within two days.
  Making people hunt for the link doesn't retain them; it converts unsubscribes into
  spam complaints.
- **Spam complaint rate below 0.3%** — and realistically well below it. This is the
  metric the cadence discipline from the touch-points piece exists to protect.

**What to take from it:**

- Authenticate and isolate: SPF, DKIM and DMARC prove your mail is really yours (DMARC
  also blocks spoofing), and sending marketing from a *subdomain* keeps a reputation
  problem there from dragging down your transactional mail.
- List hygiene *is* deliverability: only mail people who opted in, prune non-engagers and
  complainers, and never hard-bounce fake addresses when testing — the bounces are real
  even when the addresses aren't, and they damage your reputation.
- Small practical wins compound: keep emails under Gmail's ~102KB clip limit with a
  plain-text version, avoid no-reply senders (they signal one-way, low-trust mail), and
  warm up volume gradually instead of blasting a cold domain.

Beyond the hard rules, three habits keep a young domain healthy: **send from a
subdomain** (e.g. `mail.yourapp.com`) so lifecycle reputation is insulated from your
transactional mail; **warm up volume gradually** rather than blasting a cold list on
day one; and **prune non-engagers** — continuing to mail people who never open is how
good lists rot into spam-trap territory. None of this is busywork. A lifecycle program
with a damaged sender domain is a program that silently stopped existing; you'll keep
"sending" for months before anyone notices nothing lands.

---
title: "Lifecycle messaging plugs the bucket"
description: "The right message, triggered by the right behaviour, at the right time — the operational layer that moves users between AARRR stages, and a service to the user rather than a broadcast at them."
---

Fixing retention is not something you do in product code alone. A large part of it is
*reaching the user at the right moment* and pulling them back toward value — and that is
lifecycle messaging: the right message, triggered by the right behaviour, at the right
time. It's the operational layer that plugs a leaky bucket, because each hole in the
bucket has a message shaped to fit it.

- A user signs up but never reaches value → an **onboarding** sequence pulls them to
  *activation* (the first time the product's core value clicks — its **aha moment**).
- An active user goes quiet → a **win-back** message brings them back before they're
  gone for good.
- A card fails → a **dunning** sequence (the retries and reminder emails that recover a
  failed subscription payment) wins back revenue you already earned.

Lifecycle messaging isn't "marketing email." It's the operational layer that moves
people between the stages of the **AARRR funnel** — Acquisition, Activation, Retention,
Referral, Revenue, the five stages a user passes through — and it's the cheapest
retention lever you have, because the users are already yours. Measurement tells a team
where the bucket is leaking; lifecycle messaging is how the team acts on that leak
automatically, every time, without anyone having to remember. The measurement work
earlier in the course is the *seeing*; this chapter is the *acting*.

The framing that changes how you design every message is this: lifecycle messaging is a
*service* to the user, not a broadcast at them. Every good lifecycle message answers a
question the user has at that moment — "what do I do first?", "why did my payment fail?",
"what changed since I left?" A message with no answer behind it is noise, and users bill
you for noise in unsubscribes and spam complaints (the deliverability piece later shows
how expensive that bill gets). The whole discipline of this chapter — triggers, cadence,
exit conditions, holdouts — exists to keep the program on the right side of one line:
every message either answers a question or becomes one.

A single email is a single chance to catch someone at the right moment — and most of
the time you'll miss it. Sequences work because they account for missed opens and rising
intent over days. Multi-touch beats single-touch. The defensible framing: **a 3–5
message sequence materially outperforms a one-shot send.**

But messaging is a dial you can turn too far in either direction:

- **Under-messaging** is the silent failure. No onboarding push means the bucket just
  leaks. There's also a deliverability cost: senders who email irregularly see roughly
  *double* the unsubscribe rate of those who send consistently — inbox reputation is
  built by showing up predictably.
- **Over-messaging** burns the relationship: fatigue, unsubscribes, spam complaints,
  and damaged sender reputation. *(One survey claims ~56% of people unsubscribe after
  4 marketing emails in 30 days — directional, but the effect is real.)* The spam
  complaint is the expensive one — it doesn't just lose that user, it degrades
  delivery to everyone else (§5.6).

A workable default cadence:

- **Welcome series:** every 2–3 days for the first few touches, while intent is high.
- **Active users:** 1–3 per week, depending on your product's rhythm.
- **Dormant users:** slow and value-led — a user who's already drifting is the last
  person a barrage will win back.

The signal that tells you the dial has gone too far: **engagement per send drops with
each added message.** Opens, clicks, or revenue-per-email falling as you add sends is
the program telling you it's reached the useful maximum.

Two practices become load-bearing the moment you run more than one journey:

- **Frequency capping** — a ceiling on total messages per user across all journeys and
  channels, so two journeys don't both fire on the same bad day. The failure mode it
  prevents is real and common: a user's card fails the same week they go dormant and
  hit a plan limit, and three well-intentioned journeys each send twice. Every message
  was individually justified; the pile-up is what gets you marked as spam.
- **Suppression** — stop a sequence the instant its goal is met, and respect an opt-out
  on *every* channel, not just the one they unsubscribed from.

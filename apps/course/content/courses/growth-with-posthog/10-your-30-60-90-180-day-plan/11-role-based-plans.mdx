Growth is a hat before it's a hire (Chapter 0), so the person wearing it shapes how the
plan actually runs. The sequence doesn't change — but knowing where *you* will be slow
lets you plan for it instead of stalling there.

- **The solo / non-technical founder.** Your risk is the instrumentation phase: the
  measure step has real engineering in it. Lean on autocapture and no-code installs
  (Chapter 3), keep the tracking plan to ten events, and treat "good enough to trust" as
  the bar, not "complete". Your advantage is the Keep phase — you can do the unscalable
  onboarding by hand, and you already know the customer. Delegate or contract the pipeline
  work; don't let it become the thing that stops you reaching the dashboard.
- **The technical cofounder.** Instrumentation is your comfort zone, which is exactly the
  trap: the "instrumenting forever" anti-pattern (from the Measure phase) is most seductive for engineers,
  because building the perfect event pipeline feels better than reading the imperfect one.
  Time-box Measure hard. Your real growth edge is that Keep and Grow are *engineering* here
  — journeys as code, holdouts, first-party tracking — so the leverage is in shipping the
  loop, not polishing the ingestion.
- **The first growth hire.** You've landed into someone else's product and data. Your first
  30 days are mostly learning — the funnel, the activation metric, what's already been
  tried — before you commit to projects; this chapter's Measure phase *is* your onboarding
  ([Reforge's first-90-days guide](https://www.reforge.com/guides/succeed-in-your-new-growth-leadership-role)
  is the professional version). Show up with your own version of this plan rather than
  waiting to be handed one, and bank a visible early win in activation or retention before
  reaching for acquisition.
- **The marketer learning to code.** Your instinct is acquisition; the plan deliberately
  makes you earn it. Your growth is in getting comfortable reading a retention curve and a
  holdout — the measurement literacy that turns "I ran campaigns" into "I run a loop"
  (Chapter 14). The code you need for the Keep phase is smaller than it looks; the judgement
  is the hard part, and you already have half of it.

Whoever holds it, the plan works best when the *hard part for that person* is named out
loud and supported — a contractor for the pipeline, a time-box on instrumentation, a
mentor for the measurement — rather than left to quietly become the place the plan dies.

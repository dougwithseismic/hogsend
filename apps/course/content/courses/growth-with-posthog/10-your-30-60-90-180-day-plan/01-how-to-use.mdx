Everything before this chapter taught the machine; this chapter sequences the build. A
few rules about how to read it:

- **The phases are gates, not dates.** "30/60/90/180" assumes roughly a founder-day per
  week of focused growth work. With more time you can compress; with less, stretch —
  the one thing worth protecting is the *order*, because each phase's exit criteria
  are the entry requirements for the next. Shipping ads (phase 3) before retention
  work (phase 2) is the leaky-bucket pattern from Chapter 2, now with a budget
  attached.
- **The plan is stage-agnostic, the targets aren't.** A pre-launch product runs the same
  sequence but treats phase 1 as "instrument before launch" and phase 3 as launch
  channels rather than paid scaling. Where a number matters, this chapter says what it
  gates rather than pretending one benchmark fits every product — Chapter 2's warning
  about chasing someone else's magic number applies to plans too.
- **This is the same first-90-days shape growth operators run when they land in a new
  role** — learn the data first, find early wins in activation/retention, only then
  scale acquisition ([Reforge's first-90-days
  guide](https://www.reforge.com/guides/succeed-in-your-new-growth-leadership-role),
  [Delivering Value's head-of-growth
  plan](https://deliveringvalue.co/growth-essays/head-of-growth)). You're running it on
  your own company.

**What to take from it:**

- The first month is mostly *looking*: build a learning roadmap — product depth first
  (sign up as a real user with a plus-address, hit the paywalls), then how the team and
  tools actually work — before you commit to projects. It's the same "measure before you
  move" instinct as the Measure phase below.
- Pick work in the "Goldilocks zone": important enough to move a metric *and* squarely in
  your existing skill set, so you bank quick wins and trust before the harder bets — the
  operator's version of "retention before acquisition".
- Guard your capacity out loud — cap the work in progress, name your quick-wins-vs-experiments
  split — because the two fastest ways to derail a plan are picking the wrong projects and
  taking on too much.

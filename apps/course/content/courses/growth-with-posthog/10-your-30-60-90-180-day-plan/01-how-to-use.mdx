A plan you can run beats a plan you can admire, and the difference is almost entirely
about sequence. This one turns the whole course into a build order — measure, then keep,
then grow, then compound — and three rules govern how to read it.

- **The phases are gates, not dates.** "30/60/90/180" assumes roughly a founder-day per
  week of focused growth work. With more time you can compress; with less, stretch —
  the one thing worth protecting is the *order*, because each phase's exit criteria
  are the entry requirements for the next. Shipping ads (phase 3) before retention
  work (phase 2) is the leaky-bucket pattern — pouring acquisition into a product that
  can't yet keep users — now with a budget attached.
- **The plan is stage-agnostic, the targets aren't.** A pre-launch product runs the same
  sequence but treats phase 1 as "instrument before launch" and phase 3 as launch
  channels rather than paid scaling. Where a number matters, this chapter says what it
  gates rather than pretending one benchmark fits every product — the same trap the
  leaky-bucket chapter names about activation: chasing someone else's **magic number**
  (the specific usage threshold that predicts retention for one company) misleads a plan
  the same way a borrowed benchmark misleads a funnel.
- **This is the same first-90-days shape growth operators run when they land in a new
  role** — learn the data first, find early wins in activation and retention, only then
  scale acquisition ([Reforge's first-90-days
  guide](https://www.reforge.com/guides/succeed-in-your-new-growth-leadership-role),
  [Delivering Value's head-of-growth
  plan](https://deliveringvalue.co/growth-essays/head-of-growth)). You're running it on
  your own company.

**What to take from it:**

- The first month is mostly *looking* — sign up as a real user with a plus-address, hit the paywalls, learn how the team and tools actually work before you commit to projects — the same measure-before-you-move instinct as the Measure phase below.
- Pick work in the "Goldilocks zone" — important enough to move a metric *and* squarely inside your existing skills — so you bank quick wins and trust before the harder bets, which is the operator's version of "retention before acquisition".
- Guard your capacity out loud — cap the work in progress, name your quick-wins-versus-experiments split — because the two fastest ways to derail a plan are picking the wrong projects and taking on too many at once.

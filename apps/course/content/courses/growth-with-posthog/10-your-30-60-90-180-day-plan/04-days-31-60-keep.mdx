**Goal: plug the biggest leaks before spending a cent on traffic.** This is the phase
with the highest return per hour in the whole plan — the users it saves are users you
already paid (in money or effort) to acquire.

Journeys are best built in the order of expected impact, which for almost every
product looks like:

1. **Welcome + onboarding nudge** — the gap between signup and activation is nearly
   always the biggest leak, which is why the lifecycle chapter builds this journey
   first. One journey: welcome on signup, a nudge at +48h if the activation event
   hasn't fired.
2. **Dunning** — the automated recovery of failed card payments. If you charge money,
   those failures are pure, recoverable churn, and building the sequence in Hogsend
   (the Hogsend chapter) tends to be the fastest revenue win in the whole course.
3. **Win-back** — dormant-user re-engagement, triggered by an inactivity window, with
   something concrete to come back to.

**Exit criteria.** Either your activation or early-retention number has visibly moved,
or — more honestly — your **holdout comparison** shows the journeys lifting it. A holdout
is a slice of users you deliberately leave *out* of the journey, kept as a baseline; the
gap between them and the messaged group is the **incremental lift**, the change the
journey actually caused rather than one that would have happened anyway. It's the
lifecycle chapter's whole argument in one number: opens and clicks are activity, lift is
proof. The bucket holds water well enough that pouring into it isn't waste.

**The pattern to watch for** is building all eight journeys you can imagine instead of
the three that matter — volume of automation isn't the goal; measured lift is. A
second, subtler one: declaring victory off open rates. Without a holdout, what got
measured was sending, not keeping.

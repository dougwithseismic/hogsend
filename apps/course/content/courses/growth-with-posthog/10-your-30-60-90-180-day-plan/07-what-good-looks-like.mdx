---
title: "What good looks like at each gate"
description: "Each gate is a single yes/no question, not a benchmark — day 30 (do we trust the data?), 60 (does the bucket hold?), 90 (is acquisition landing in owned channels?), 180 (is the loop compounding?) — answered by evidence you can defend out loud."
---

Every gate in this plan is a yes/no question, not a benchmark. What "good" looks like is
deliberately loose, because the right numbers are your product's own rather than a
competitor's threshold — chasing someone else's **magic number** (the usage bar that
predicts retention for one specific company) is the trap the leaky-bucket chapter warns
about. So each gate is framed as a single question the evidence should be able to answer
with a *yes*:

| Gate | The question | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Day 30 | Do we trust the data? | Funnel + retention read aloud, replays match events |
| Day 60 | Does the bucket hold? | Holdout-measured lift on activation or early retention |
| Day 90 | Is acquisition landing in owned channels? | Conversion quality good; % of signups verified/linked |
| Day 180 | Is the loop compounding? | Test tempo steady; lift + blended CAC reported monthly |

Read down the Evidence column and the through-line is the same at every gate: a number
you can say out loud and defend, not a chart that merely exists. Two outside yardsticks
keep those numbers honest as the machine grows —
[YC's key startup metrics](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/KR-key-startup-metrics)
is a sane preview of what investors will eventually ask of the very same funnel and
retention figures, and [Reforge on North Star metrics](https://www.reforge.com/blog/north-star-metrics)
is the discipline for keeping your **North Star** (the single number that best captures
the value users get) meaningful rather than vanity as you scale. Pass all four gates with
a straight face and the plan has done its job — the gates were never about the calendar,
only about whether the evidence is real.

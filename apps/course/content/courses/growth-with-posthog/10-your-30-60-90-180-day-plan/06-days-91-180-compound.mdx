The first 90 days build the machine. The next 90 turn it into a *culture* — the
difference between a system that ran once and one that gets a little better every
week. Three threads run in parallel:

**1. An experimentation program, not occasional experiments.** Keep a scored backlog
of growth hypotheses — Sean Ellis's
[ICE](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/ice-scoring-model) (impact, confidence,
ease) is the standard scoring scheme, [PIE](https://conversion.com/framework/pie-framework/)
a close alternative — and run a steady tempo of tests from the top of it. Ellis's
[high-tempo testing](https://www.saastr.com/sean-ellis-founder-ceo-of-growthhackers-building-a-company-wide-growth-culture-video-transcript/)
argument is that learning rate, not any single win, is what compounds; Reforge's
[experiment management system](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-experiment-management-system)
is the same idea with a template and a velocity metric attached. Temper the tempo with
discipline: [not everything deserves an experiment](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-running-an-experiment),
and an underpowered test that "wins" is [worse than no test](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-ab-testing).

**2. Prove the program pays.** By day 180 you should be reporting **incremental lift**
from the lifecycle program (global holdout, Chapter 5) and **blended CAC** including
the referral loop's contribution — is growth getting cheaper as the owned audience
grows? That's the flywheel from Chapter 8 showing up in the finances.

**3. Decide what you are before you scale.** Real acquisition money is worth gating on
evidence of product-market fit — the
[Sean Ellis 40% test](https://pmfsurvey.com/) ("how disappointed would you be if this
product disappeared?") is the standard instrument. It's also worth running the system
yourself for a while before hiring a head of growth to run it for you; First Round's
[founder-led growth playbook](https://review.firstround.com/founder-led-growth-playbook/)
makes the case that premature growth hires fail precisely because the founder skipped
the loop you've just spent 90 days building.

**Exit criteria — and the real finish line.** There isn't one, and that's the point:
by day 180 the plan should have dissolved into an operating rhythm (Chapter 9's
cadence) that doesn't need this chapter anymore. The observable test: growth work
appears in every sprint, decisions cite the funnel, and the team can name last month's
biggest learning from a *failed* experiment without embarrassment.

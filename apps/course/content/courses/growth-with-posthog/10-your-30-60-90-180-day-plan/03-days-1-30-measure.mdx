**Goal: see the truth about your funnel.** Nothing in this phase grows the business
directly. All of it decides whether the next five months of work target the real
leak or a guessed one.

Week by week, the shape most teams follow:

| Week | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1 | PostHog live: SDK installed, `identify`/`reset` wired, starter events firing |
| 2 | Tracking plan written; activation event defined; server-side revenue events |
| 3 | Reverse proxy; data QA — watch replays, check identities merge correctly |
| 4 | The daily dashboard built and pinned; alerts to Slack; first honest read |

Weeks 1–3 are the instrumentation chapter's work applied to your own product; week 4 is
the daily-dashboard chapter's. The schedule is a suggestion; the order inside it isn't.

**Exit criteria.** You can open the dashboard and say, with numbers: *where* in the
funnel you lose the most people, *what* your week-4 retention roughly is, and *which*
phase of product-market fit you're in — pre-fit, hunting, or found, read off whether your
retention curve flattens rather than falling to zero (the stages chapter maps these
phases in full). If those three sentences aren't sayable yet, that's the signal to stay
in this phase a little longer — [YC's analytics-for-startups
guidance](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/8H-analytics-for-startups) is blunt that
most startups' first analytics problem is not volume of charts but trusting what the
few important ones say.

**The pattern to watch for here** is instrumenting forever. Thirty days is enough to
measure a funnel honestly; it is not enough to build a data platform — a schema
registry in week three is usually growth work quietly traded for tooling work.

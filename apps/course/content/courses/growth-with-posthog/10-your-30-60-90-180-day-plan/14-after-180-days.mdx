By day 180 the plan should have dissolved into a rhythm that no longer needs this chapter
(the [Compound phase](./06-days-91-180-compound.mdx) is where it starts to). That's the
finish line, and it's the start of the next stretch. Three moves define
the six months after:

- **Add the second channel — only once the first genuinely works.** A single working
  channel is a single point of failure, and platforms change their rules on their own
  schedule. But adding a channel before the first is measured and profitable just splits
  your attention across two things you don't understand yet. The signal to diversify is a
  channel with a stable, known CAC and payback — then you add the next one deliberately,
  and hold it to the same gate. Brian Balfour's
  [growth-loops framing](https://brianbalfour.com/four-fits-growth-framework) is the map for
  which channels actually fit your model rather than just being available.
- **Your first growth hire — now that you know what to hire for.** Having run the loop
  yourself, you can write the job description from experience and evaluate a candidate's
  answers against reality instead of buzzwords. First Round's
  [founder-led growth playbook](https://review.firstround.com/founder-led-growth-playbook/)
  makes the case that premature growth hires fail precisely because the founder skipped the
  loop and can't tell a good hire from a plausible one. Hire to *scale* the loop you
  understand, never to *skip* it.
- **Raise the tempo and defend the flywheel.** The experimentation program (Chapter 12) is
  where compounding now lives — more tests, faster learning. And the owned-audience flywheel
  (Chapter 8) should be showing up in the finances: blended CAC falling as referrals and the
  owned channel grow. The job shifts from *building* the machine to *defending* it — keeping
  the culture and the loop intact as the team grows (Chapter 11's scaling stage is about
  exactly this).

What you care about also changes. The first 180 days were about *existence* — does the
data hold, does the bucket hold, does acquisition land in owned channels. From here it's
about *efficiency and defensibility*: is growth getting cheaper, is the moat (owned
audience, retention, referral) getting deeper, and can the machine survive more people
running it.

One more thing, and it's not about the company. Running this loop end to end is rare and
valuable — it's the difference between having done a growth project and being able to run a
growth program. That's career capital, and [Chapter 14](../14-growth-as-career-capital) is
about what to do with it.

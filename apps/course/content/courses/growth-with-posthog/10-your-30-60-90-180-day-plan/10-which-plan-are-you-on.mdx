The plan is one sequence, but you're not running it from a standing start unless you're
truly at zero. Most teams pick it up somewhere in the middle, and where you pick it up
changes how much time each phase deserves and what its exit gate should say. The order is
fixed; the *weighting* is yours.

- **Pre-launch / just launched.** Phase 1 is "instrument before launch", phase 3 is
  "launch channels", and paid barely features. You spend most of your energy in Measure
  and Keep, by hand (see [the zero-to-one version](./09-zero-to-one.mdx)). The day-30 gate
  isn't "trust the dashboard" so much as "we can see whether the first users reach value".
- **Early traction.** You have users and some retention; the machine is worth building
  properly. This is the plan as written — the four phases, roughly the timings given, with
  the gates read against real numbers. Most of this chapter is aimed here.
- **Scaling a working channel.** You already have one channel that works and cash to
  spend. Measure and Keep are a tune-up, not a build; the real weight moves to the Compound
  phase — an experimentation program, proving incremental lift, and the discipline not to
  over-scale before the evidence supports it. Your gates are about *efficiency* (blended
  CAC, payback, lift), not existence.

The one rule that survives every stage: **don't skip a phase because your stage lets you
feel past it.** A scaling team that never verified its measurement is optimising on noise;
a just-launched team that jumps to ads is buying churn. The gates exist precisely to catch
the stage-appropriate version of that mistake.

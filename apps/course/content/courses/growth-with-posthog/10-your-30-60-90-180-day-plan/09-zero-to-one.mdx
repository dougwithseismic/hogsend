Everything in this chapter so far assumes you have a funnel to measure. If you're
pre-launch, or you launched last week and the dashboard would show single digits, the
plan doesn't change *order* — it changes *shape*. The order still protects you: chasing
acquisition before you can keep anyone is the leaky-bucket mistake (Chapter 2) whether you
have ten users or ten thousand. But at zero-to-one, the Measure phase isn't "build the
daily dashboard" — it's "find evidence a real person wants this at all".

**What each phase becomes before product-market fit:**

- **Measure → talk to users, instrument the one signal.** With no traffic, quantitative
  dashboards are noise. The measurement that matters is qualitative: problem interviews,
  and watching the first handful of people try the product. Instrument exactly one thing —
  the activation event that would mean "they got it" (Chapter 2) — so the moment you *do*
  have users, you can already see whether they reach value.
- **Keep → make the first ten users successful by hand.** This is the phase Paul Graham
  means by [*Do Things That Don't Scale*](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html): onboard people
  personally, fix their problems over a call, do the unscalable work that a lifecycle
  journey will later automate. You're not building the Keep machine yet — you're learning,
  by hand, what it will eventually need to do.
- **Grow → founder-led distribution, not paid.** Launches, communities, writing, direct
  outreach, being useful in the places your users already are. Paid acquisition into a
  product with no proven retention is the most expensive way to learn you don't have
  product-market fit (Chapter 7). Keep the tap off until the bucket holds a little water.

**The trap at this stage** is building the whole machine before anyone is using it — a
schema registry, eight lifecycle journeys, a paid funnel — because building feels like
progress and talking to five users feels like it isn't. It's the "instrumenting forever"
anti-pattern, moved earlier: the work that looks like growth work is quietly standing in
for the uncomfortable work of finding out whether anyone cares.

**What "good enough to start the real plan" looks like** is a pulse: a small group of
users who would be genuinely disappointed if the product vanished (the seed of the Sean
Ellis 40% test you'll run properly at day 180), and a retention curve — even over a dozen
users — that doesn't fall to zero. That's the flattening from Chapter 2, expressed in
miniature. Chapter 11 maps these stages in full; this atom is just the on-ramp.

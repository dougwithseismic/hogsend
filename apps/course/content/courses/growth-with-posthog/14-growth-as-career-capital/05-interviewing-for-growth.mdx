Growth interviews vary by company, but the question types are stable enough to prepare for.
Knowing the shape lets you rehearse the substance instead of being surprised by the format.

What you'll be asked falls into four recurring types, each testing a different muscle:

- **Metrics fluency.** "What metrics would you watch for this product? Which is the North
  Star, and why?" The North Star metric is the single number that best reflects the value
  customers get, and they're checking whether you can pick one that reflects that value
  rather than a vanity number — the value-versus-vanity judgement the course drills, live.
  Being able to explain why revenue is a *lagging* indicator — a scoreboard, not the thing
  you actually pull on — separates real fluency from jargon.
- **A past experiment.** "Walk me through a growth experiment you ran." This is the
  project-vs-loop test in disguise — tell it as a loop, land the result on lift against a
  control.
- **A product teardown.** "Sign up for our product — where's the funnel leaking, and what
  would you fix first?" They want to see you *look* before you prescribe, and prioritise by
  leverage rather than by whatever's most visible.
- **A case study or take-home.** Bigger roles often include a written exercise or a
  presentation — frequently the first-90-days plan itself, or a "how would you grow X"
  prompt. Guides on preparing for PM and growth interviews (Lenny's Newsletter,
  [lennysnewsletter.com](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com)) consistently make the same
  point: judgement and structure beat a pile of tactics.

The throughline: in every one of these, **measurement judgement beats buzzwords**. The
candidate who says "I'd want a holdout before I claim that worked" outscores the one who
lists ten growth hacks, because the first sounds like someone who's been accountable for a
number and the second sounds like someone who's read about it.

The questions you ask them are themselves a signal — they reveal whether you think like an
operator. Strong ones probe the conditions you'd need to actually do the job: *Is the product
instrumented, and will I have access to the data? Who owns the growth loop today? What's been
tried, and how did you know whether it worked? Is growth in the same backlog as product, or a
separate function?* Their answers also tell *you* whether the role is real — reading a growth
job description is the field guide to those signals.

**What to take from it:**

- Almost every favourite question probes *judgement and self-awareness*, not tactics: "the
  hardest thing you've ever done" (agency when it's hard), "a time something went wrong" (how
  you relate to failure), "the work you're most proud of" (your taste, and what "good" looks
  like to you). That's this atom's "measurement judgement beats buzzwords," heard from the
  interviewer's chair.
- Strong operators simplify, asking the highest-information questions. Coda's founder uses a
  teleportation-device test: you get only two questions before you must produce a plan, and
  the good answers cut straight to the decisions that matter ("is it safe for humans?",
  "capex or opex?") instead of piling on detail — the same instinct as the teardown's "look
  before you prescribe."
- The best questions are built to break a rehearsed script. "What conventional wisdom do you
  bet against?" and "three years from now, what's different about you?" are designed to get
  past what the candidate thinks the interviewer wants to hear and surface a genuine answer —
  the person who sounds like they've been accountable for a number, not one who's only read
  about it.

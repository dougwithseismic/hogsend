Growth interviews vary by company, but the question types are stable enough to prepare
for. Knowing the shape lets you rehearse the substance instead of being surprised by the
format.

**What you'll be asked.** Four recurring types, each testing a different muscle:

- **Metrics fluency.** "What metrics would you watch for this product? Which is the North
  Star, and why?" They're checking whether you can pick a metric that reflects customer
  value and isn't a vanity number — the Chapter 2 material, live. Being able to explain why
  revenue is a *lagging* indicator — a scoreboard, not the thing you actually pull on —
  separates real fluency from jargon.
- **A past experiment.** "Walk me through a growth experiment you ran." This is the
  project-vs-loop test from the third piece — tell it as a loop, land the result on lift
  against a control.
- **A product teardown.** "Sign up for our product — where's the funnel leaking, and what
  would you fix first?" They want to see you *look* before you prescribe, and prioritise
  by leverage rather than by whatever's most visible.
- **A case study or take-home.** Bigger roles often include a written exercise or a
  presentation — frequently the 90-day plan from the fourth piece, or a "how would you
  grow X" prompt. Guides on preparing for PM and growth interviews (Lenny's Newsletter,
  [lennysnewsletter.com](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com)) consistently make the same
  point: judgement and structure beat a pile of tactics.

The throughline: in every one of these, **measurement judgement beats buzzwords**. The
candidate who says "I'd want a holdout before I claim that worked" outscores the one who
lists ten growth hacks, because the first sounds like someone who's been accountable for a
number and the second sounds like someone who's read about it.

**What you should ask them.** The questions you ask are themselves a signal — they reveal
whether you think like an operator. Strong ones probe the conditions you'd need to
actually do the job: *Is the product instrumented, and will I have access to the data? Who
owns the growth loop today? What's been tried, and how did you know whether it worked? Is
growth in the same backlog as product, or a separate function?* Their answers also tell
*you* whether the role is real (the sixth piece is the field guide to reading those
signals).

Most people applying for a growth role have a résumé that *asserts* things — "grew
activation," "owned lifecycle." You have something better: a record of the work itself.
The [workbook](/workbook) you filled in across this course is the raw material for a
portfolio, and a portfolio beats an assertion because it shows judgement, not just
outcomes.

Look at what's already in there. An **activation** sentence — the one line that says when a
new user has actually got value, not merely signed up — that you defined and defended. A
tracking plan: the events you chose to capture, and why those. A dashboard that reads the
funnel at a glance. **Lifecycle journeys** — automated message sequences that fire on what
a user does or doesn't do — that you actually shipped, with holdout results (a holdout is a
slice of users deliberately held back from the change, so the difference shows what it
added) that say whether they moved anything. A view on where traffic should come from and how you'd measure
it. A 30/60/90 plan for the next quarter. That is the full loop, documented, with your
reasoning attached — which is exactly the thing an interviewer can't get from a bullet
point.

The job now is compression. A portfolio nobody opens is worth nothing, so aim for a single
page (or one short doc, or a slide or two) that a hiring manager can read in two minutes
and a curious one can drill into. The shape that works: **the problem you saw in the data →
the fix you shipped → how you knew it worked (or didn't) → what you'd do next.** That last
clause matters more than it looks. Showing what you learned from a journey that *didn't*
move the metric reads as more credible than an unbroken run of wins — because real growth
work is mostly negative results.

One honest caveat: your course workbook is built on your own product, or a practice one.
That's a strength — self-directed work on a real funnel is rarer than it sounds — but say
so plainly rather than dressing it up as something it isn't. The attribution humility this
course keeps circling back to begins right here, in how you frame your own evidence.

Most people applying for a growth role have a résumé that *asserts* things — "grew
activation," "owned lifecycle." You have something better: a record of the work itself.
The [workbook](/workbook) you filled in across this course is the raw material for a
portfolio, and a portfolio beats an assertion because it shows judgement, not just
outcomes.

Look at what's already in there. An activation sentence you defined and defended
(Chapter 2). A tracking plan — the events you chose to capture and why (Chapter 3). A
dashboard that reads the funnel (Chapter 4). Lifecycle journeys you actually shipped,
with the holdout results that say whether they moved anything (Chapters 5–6). A view on
where traffic should come from and how you'd measure it (Chapters 7–8). A 30/60/90 plan
(Chapter 10). That is the full loop, documented, with your reasoning attached — which is
exactly the thing an interviewer can't get from a bullet point.

The job now is compression. A portfolio nobody opens is worth nothing, so aim for a
single page (or one short doc, or a slide or two) that a hiring manager can read in two
minutes and a curious one can drill into. The shape that works: **the problem you saw in
the data → the fix you shipped → how you knew it worked (or didn't) → what you'd do
next.** That last clause matters more than it looks — showing what you learned from a
journey that *didn't* move the metric reads as more credible than an unbroken string of
wins, because real growth work is mostly negative results.

One honest caveat: your course workbook is built on your own product, or a practice one.
That's a strength — self-directed work on a real funnel is rarer than it sounds — but say
so plainly rather than dressing it up as something it isn't. Attribution humility (the
seventh piece) starts here, in how you frame your own evidence.

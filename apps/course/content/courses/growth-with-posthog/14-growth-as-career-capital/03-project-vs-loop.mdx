There's a line an experienced interviewer is listening for, usually without saying so out
loud: is this person telling me about a *project* they ran once, or a *loop* they run?
The difference decides a lot of growth offers, and it's worth understanding before you
tell your story.

**A project** has a start and an end. "We wanted more signups, so we ran a referral push,
and signups went up 20%." It's a fine thing to have done — but it's a one-off, and it
leaves the interviewer with questions the candidate didn't answer: up 20% versus what?
Would they have signed up anyway? What happened after? Did it repeat?

**A loop** is a system that keeps running. Same story, told as a loop: "New users weren't
reaching activation, so I instrumented the drop, shipped an onboarding journey, and held
out 10% to measure it. It lifted 30-day retention a few points against the holdout, so I
kept it and moved to the next-biggest leak — that measure-fix-measure cycle is how I
work." Notice what changed. There's a system, a control group, a decision about where to
go next, and a *cadence*. That's the growth team versus the team that did a growth
project once — the same distinction Chapter 9 draws.

Three things strong interviewers probe for, all of them ways of asking "loop or project?":

- **Systems thinking** — do you see the funnel as connected stages, or as isolated
  tactics? A loop-thinker fixes the leak with the most leverage; a project-thinker fixes
  whatever was asked for.
- **Measurement** — how did you *know* it worked? A number without a baseline is a story.
  A holdout, a control, a before/after with a comparison group is evidence.
- **Incrementality** — did the change cause the outcome, or ride along with it? This is
  the correlation-versus-causation discipline from Chapters 2 and 5, and it's the single
  most common place candidates over-claim (the seventh piece is about surviving that
  question).

You don't have to have run a loop for years to tell it as one. You ran one in this course:
measure → keep → grow, with holdouts and a plan. Frame it honestly at that scope and it
still reads as loop-thinking, which is the thing being tested.

A clean way to package one story is **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result — a
long-standing behavioural-interview structure that keeps you concrete and stops you
drifting into generalities. Land the Result on a measured outcome and, if you can, the
decision it led to next. Draft yours here:

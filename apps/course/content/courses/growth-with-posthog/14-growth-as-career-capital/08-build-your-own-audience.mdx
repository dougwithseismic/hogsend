Chapter 8 made the case that a business should build an audience it *owns* — an email
list, a community, a direct line to its users — rather than renting attention from a
platform that can change the rules. The same logic applies to your career, and it's a
fitting note to close the course on.

An owned audience for *you* is the people who know what you can do because you've shown
them — through writing, building in public, a newsletter, talks, or just a habit of
posting what you're learning. It's a career flywheel: the work you do generates things
worth sharing, sharing them reaches people, and some of those people bring opportunities —
which generate more work worth sharing. Andrew Chen's own trajectory
([andrewchen.com](https://andrewchen.com)) is a clean example of the pattern — years of
writing about growth became the platform for everything that followed. This isn't a claim
that everyone must become a creator. It's that a small, genuine audience compounds quietly
in the background of a career — and it costs very little to start.

### Why it works

Why it works is the same reason it worked for your product. You **own the relationship** —
it doesn't depend on a recruiter's inbox or a job board's algorithm. It's **compounding** —
a body of writing keeps working while you sleep, the way a retained user keeps paying
back their acquisition cost. And it's **differentiating** — most candidates are a résumé;
someone who's visibly done and explained the work stands out before the interview starts.

You don't need a large following for this to pay off. A handful of the right people —
founders, growth leaders, peers who'd vouch for you — is worth more than a big anonymous
number, which is exactly the owned-audience-over-vanity-metric lesson from earlier in the
course, applied to you. And you already have the raw material: the portfolio from the
second piece, the honest write-ups of what worked and what didn't from the seventh. The
first move is small — publish one thing.

**What to take from it:**

- Robinson's core move is being useful in public: show what's possible, then teach people
  how to build it themselves. Trust compounds over many touches — his skeptical audience,
  developers, tends to adopt around the *tenth* time they hear about something, not the
  first — which is why a small owned audience pays back slowly, then all at once.
- Writing is the compounding asset, and his process makes it repeatable: ideas sit as
  bullets in a notes app until "this needs to be in the world," a rough first draft,
  feedback from people you trust, then a rewrite that moves the conclusion to the top. A
  tweet is the small version that later expands into the post — start smaller than a post.
- Write like you talk: cut jargon and hedging, lead with the bottom line, keep it
  skimmable. The point isn't polish — it's being genuinely useful, which is what earns the
  handful of right people (this atom's owned audience) over a vanity follower count.

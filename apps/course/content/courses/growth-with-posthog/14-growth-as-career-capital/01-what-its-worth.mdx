This course taught you to run a loop: instrument a funnel, find where it leaks, ship a
journey, measure the lift honestly, then decide where the next hour goes. This chapter is
about what that's worth to your career — not as a salary figure this course won't invent,
but as leverage.

### Career capital, not a salary figure

The useful frame is Cal Newport's **career capital**, from *So Good They Can't Ignore
You* ([calnewport.com](https://calnewport.com)): the rare and valuable skills you build
are the capital you trade for the things people actually want from work — autonomy,
interesting problems, a say in what you do next. His argument runs against the usual
advice: mastery tends to come *before* passion, not after it. You don't find a growth
role you love and then get good at it. It's the other way round — you get good at running
a loop, and the control that skill earns you is what starts to make the work feel like
yours.

### Rare because it spans functions

A growth capability is rare *because* it spans functions. Plenty of people can run one
channel well — write ads, send emails, tune a signup page. Far fewer can hold the whole
loop in their head: read the funnel, pick the leak with the most leverage, build the fix
across product and messaging, and prove it moved with a holdout instead of a vanity
chart. That span is exactly what makes the skill hard to hire for, and hard to fake.

### A discipline, not a job title someone invented

It's also a genuine discipline, not a job title someone invented. Andrew Chen's 2012
essay "Growth Hacker is the new VP of Marketing"
([andrewchen.com](https://andrewchen.com)) named the shift: growth sits between product,
engineering, and marketing, and it's driven by data rather than taste. Product-led growth
widened the demand — a product-led company needs people who think in loops and can
instrument them, not just campaigns. Reforge ([reforge.com](https://www.reforge.com))
built a whole curriculum around treating growth as a repeatable system rather than a bag
of tricks, which is the same premise this course runs on.

That capability shows up under three main titles — growth PM, growth marketer, growth
engineer — three doors into the same loop, which the sixth piece decodes. What matters
here is the shape: a founder or growth leader values the person who can run the loop end
to end more than a specialist in any single stage of it, because one person who can do all
of it replaces what would otherwise take a whole committee. The rest of this chapter turns
your course work into the artifacts that prove you're that person.

**What to take from it:**

- Verna's whole list is one argument: the growth "hacks" people chase — copy a competitor's
  onboarding, redesign the homepage, hire a head of growth to fix a slump — reliably fail,
  because growth *amplifies* a working loop rather than manufacturing one. The scarce skill
  is knowing that, which is loop-thinking, not tactic-collecting.
- Her highest-leverage move is building an *earned* channel you own — virality,
  word-of-mouth, user-generated content (Dropbox draws ~50% of acquisition from its sharing
  loop) — instead of renting attention from an algorithm. Seeing and building that loop is
  exactly the cross-functional capability this chapter calls career capital.
- Copying competitors, she says, is "the fastest way to mediocrity" — you can't lead by
  imitating. The operator who can read a funnel and design the fix, rather than reach for
  someone else's playbook, is the one who's hard to hire and hard to replace.

Running a growth loop end to end — instrument a funnel, find where it leaks, ship a fix
across product and messaging, then prove the fix moved the number honestly — is a rare
skill, and rare skills buy you something specific. Not a salary figure this course won't
invent, but leverage: control over what you work on and how. That's what running the loop
is worth to a career.

The useful frame for it is Cal Newport's **career capital**, from *So Good They Can't
Ignore You* ([calnewport.com](https://calnewport.com)): the rare and valuable skills you
build are the capital you trade for the things people actually want from work — autonomy,
interesting problems, a say in what you do next. His argument runs against the usual
advice, that mastery tends to come *before* passion, not after it. You don't find a growth
role you love and then get good at it. It's the other way round — you get good at running
a loop, and the control that skill earns is what starts to make the work feel like yours.

A growth capability is rare *because* it spans functions. Plenty of people can run one
channel well — write ads, send emails, tune a signup page. Far fewer can hold the whole
loop in their head: read the funnel, pick the leak with the most leverage, build the fix
across product and messaging, and prove it moved with a **holdout** — a slice of users
deliberately kept out of the change, so the gap between them and everyone else is the
change's real effect — rather than a vanity chart that only ever shows the line going up.
That span is exactly what makes the skill hard to hire for, and hard to fake.

It's also a genuine discipline, not a job title someone invented. Andrew Chen's 2012 essay
"Growth Hacker is the new VP of Marketing" ([andrewchen.com](https://andrewchen.com))
named the shift: growth sits between product, engineering, and marketing, and it's driven
by data rather than taste. Product-led growth — a way of running a company where the
product and its usage data are the engine of growth — widened the demand, because such a
company needs people who think in loops and can instrument them, not just run campaigns.
Reforge ([reforge.com](https://www.reforge.com)) built a whole curriculum around treating
growth as a repeatable system rather than a bag of tricks, which is the same premise this
course runs on.

That capability shows up under three main titles — growth PM, growth marketer, growth
engineer — three doors into the same loop, which the piece on reading a growth job
description decodes. What matters here is the shape: a founder or growth leader values the
person who can run the loop end to end over a specialist in any single stage of it, because
one person who can do all of it replaces what would otherwise take a whole committee. A
veteran of that work puts it more sharply than a definition can.

**What to take from it:**

- Verna's whole list is one argument: the growth "hacks" people chase — copy a competitor's
  onboarding, redesign the homepage, hire a head of growth to rescue a slump — fail
  reliably, because growth *amplifies* a loop that already works rather than manufacturing
  one. Knowing that is the scarce skill: loop-thinking, not tactic-collecting.
- Her highest-leverage move is building an *earned* channel you own — virality,
  word-of-mouth, user-generated content, the way Dropbox draws ~50% of its acquisition from
  its own sharing loop — instead of renting attention from an algorithm. Seeing and building
  that loop is the cross-functional capability this chapter calls career capital.
- Copying competitors, she says, is "the fastest way to mediocrity": you can't lead by
  imitating. The operator who reads a funnel and designs the fix, rather than reaching for
  someone else's playbook, is the one who's hard to hire and hard to replace.

There's a temptation, when you're selling yourself, to round your impact up. "I grew
activation 3x." "My campaign drove the quarter." Resist it — because the person across the
table has almost certainly run these numbers themselves, and they know how hard
attribution really is. Over-claiming doesn't read as confidence. It reads as someone
who's never had to stand behind a number and defend it.

This is the same honesty the course has argued for throughout, now pointed at your own
story. The talking-about-growth chapter's whole subject is that attribution is hard: multiple things move at
once, seasonality and the base rate move on their own, and a change that *correlates* with
a metric didn't necessarily *cause* it — the correlation-versus-causation discipline from
Chapters 2 and 5. If you internalised that for your product, apply it to your résumé too.

### Pair every claim with how you'd know

The credible move is to pair any claim with **how you'd know**. Compare:

- *Over-claim:* "I increased retention 40%."
- *Honest, and stronger:* "We shipped an onboarding journey and held out 10% of new users.
  30-day retention ran a few points higher in the treated group over two months. I'd want
  a longer window and a repeat before I called it durable, but the holdout gives me
  confidence the journey caused most of it."

The second version is longer and it's *more* persuasive, because everything in it is
checkable. It shows you know what a holdout is for, that you distinguish lift from
activity, and that you know the limits of your own evidence. To a growth leader, that's
the whole job — you're going to be trusted to spend money and report on it, and they're
hiring the person who won't fool them or themselves.

Saying "I don't know for certain, but here's how I'd find out" is not a weakness in a
growth interview. It's the single clearest signal that you've actually done the work,
because only people who've measured things honestly know how much they don't know. The
candidate claiming a clean 10x with no control group is telling you they haven't.

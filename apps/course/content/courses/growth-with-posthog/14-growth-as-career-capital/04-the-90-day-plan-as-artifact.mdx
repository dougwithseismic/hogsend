A large share of growth and product interviews end on the same question, and it's often the
deciding one: *what would your first 90 days here look like?* Sometimes it's a conversation;
sometimes it's a written take-home or a presentation. Either way, the deliverable it wants
is a **30/60/90 plan** — a one-page sketch of what you'd learn in the first thirty days, the
first fix you'd ship by sixty, and the win you'd prove by ninety. This course had you draft
exactly that shape for your own product; the work now is aiming that generic plan at a
specific company.

Why interviewers lean on the question: a 90-day plan reveals in one page how a candidate
thinks. It shows a few things at once — whether you'll spend month one *looking* before you
act, whether you sequence retention before acquisition or reach straight for the acquisition
tap, and whether you set gates you can actually be held to. That's why the plan works as an
interview deliverable almost unchanged — its whole spine is *learn the data first, find
early wins in activation and retention, only then scale acquisition* — activation being the
share of new users who reach first value, not just those who signed up — which is the same
first-90-days shape growth operators run when they land in a new role (Reforge's
first-90-days guide and First Round Review's [founder-led growth
playbook](https://review.firstround.com) both walk this arc).

What turns the generic plan into a *strong* answer is specificity to their business. Do the
homework the plan assumes:

- **Sign up as a real user.** Hit their onboarding, their paywall, their empty states — the
  way the plan's first month is mostly looking before acting. You'll walk in already knowing
  where their funnel probably leaks.
- **Name what you'd instrument first.** You can't promise results on data you haven't seen,
  so frame month one as measurement: "here's the funnel I'd want visible before I commit to
  projects." Restraint reads as maturity, not indecision.
- **Sequence honestly.** Put activation and retention work before paid scaling, and say why —
  the leaky-bucket argument, that filling a bucket faster is pointless while it leaks, is more
  persuasive than a promise to "drive growth."
- **Set gates, not just dates.** "By day 60 I'd want a holdout-measured lift on activation
  before we open the acquisition tap" tells them you measure before you spend — a holdout
  being a slice of new users deliberately left out of the change, so the gap between them
  and everyone else shows what the work actually added.

Say you're interviewing at a scheduling app. You sign up the night before, hit the
empty-calendar first run, and notice there's no nudge to book anything. Now your month-one
answer writes itself: "I'd want the funnel from signup to first booking instrumented, and
I'd look at where new users stall before their first event." It's specific, and it's
grounded in something you actually saw — not a number you promised sight unseen.

The trap to avoid is the confident-stranger plan: bold targets and specific tactics for a
funnel you've never seen. It reads as someone who'll act before they look. A plan that leads
with what you'd *measure* — and admits what you can't know from the outside — consistently
beats one that promises a number on day one.

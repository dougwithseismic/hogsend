---
title: "Build vs buy"
description: "The honest trade-off between renting a hosted lifecycle tool (Customer.io, Braze, Loops) and owning your own on your data and identity. When hosted is the right first move, and when owning it wins."
---

You have a real choice about *where* your lifecycle program lives, and a growth team should
make it deliberately rather than default into it. On one side: **rent** a hosted tool —
Customer.io, Braze, Iterable, Loops, Klaviyo — and run your journeys in someone else's UI.
On the other: **own** it — run the program on your own data and identity graph, versioned
alongside your product code. Both are legitimate. The mistake is picking without seeing
the trade.

**What renting buys you.** A hosted lifecycle tool is, for a lot of teams, the right first
move — and there's no virtue in building for its own sake:

- **A non-engineer can own it.** If a marketer or lifecycle manager runs your messaging, a
  visual tool lets them ship without a code review or a deploy. That alone is often
  decisive.
- **Time-to-first-journey is hours, not a sprint.** Templates, a drag-and-drop builder,
  deliverability, and analytics come in the box. You can have a welcome flow live this
  afternoon.
- **Someone else runs the infrastructure.** No workers, no queues, no on-call for the
  sending pipeline.
- **It's mature.** Segmentation, A/B tools, multi-channel (email/SMS/push), and reporting
  are years deep. Klaviyo on Shopify, or Customer.io for a marketing-led SaaS, will serve
  you well for a long time.

If your flows are simple drips, you need to move this week, and no engineer wants to own
lifecycle — rent. Start there with a clear conscience.

**What renting costs you — and where owning wins.** The bill for convenience comes due
along a few predictable lines, and they're exactly the ones that matter as your program
gets ambitious:

- **Your logic lives in their UI, not your repo.** The program that decides who gets which
  message becomes a set of flowcharts only reachable by logging into a vendor. No diff, no
  code review, no branch, no test, no "git blame this rule." When journeys get genuinely
  conditional — *read this user's whole event history and branch on it* — a canvas becomes
  the bottleneck that code never is.
- **Your identity graph fragments.** A hosted tool keeps its *own* picture of your users.
  Your **identity graph** — the map that ties one person's many devices, emails, and
  sessions to a single identity, the thing a real referral loop is built on — is what lets
  messaging share the same resolution as your product. A separate system holding a separate
  copy of "who is this person" is quietly working against that. Own the messaging engine and
  it reads the same identity you already trust.
- **You rent your own data back.** Behavioural data flows out to the vendor to make the
  tool work, and the interesting questions ("what's the real incremental lift of this
  journey?") get answered in their terms, on their export schedule.
- **Per-contact pricing scales against you.** Hosted tools bill by contacts or sends —
  fine early, a tax later. The pricing wall arrives right when the program is working and
  your list is growing, which is the worst possible time to be penalised for success.

**The honest summary.** Lifecycle messaging is a *durable, stateful, per-user* problem, and
both hosted tools and a purpose-built engine solve the hard parts of it — long waits, exit
conditions, exactly-once delivery — so you're not choosing on raw capability. You're
choosing on **ownership**: whose UI holds your logic, whose system holds your identity
graph, and whose pricing meters your growth. For a product-led team that already codes,
owning the layer — versioned, testable, on your own identity, with no per-contact wall — is
usually worth asking an engineer to own it. For a marketing-led team
shipping simple flows, renting is the honest right answer. Make the call with eyes open;
you can always start rented and bring it home once the program earns the investment. (For
what owning it looks like in practice, see [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).)

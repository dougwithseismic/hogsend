title: "Welcome + onboarding (Activation)"
description: "The highest-leverage pair in the whole program: a welcome the instant someone signs up, then an activation nudge a day or two later only if they haven't hit the aha moment yet. The trigger, the timing, the audience, the content, and how to measure the lift."
---

The welcome-and-onboarding pair is the first journey to build, and the highest-leverage
one you'll ever ship, because it patches the **widest leak in the bucket** — the gap
between someone signing up and someone reaching first value, where most products lose the
users they just paid to acquire. A new user is more engaged the moment after
signup than they will ever be again. Welcome emails post the highest open and click
rates of any automated message — commonly **35–50%** open rates
([Litmus's State of Email reports](https://www.litmus.com/state-of-email-reports) are
the least salesy place to calibrate). Spend that attention on the one action that
predicts they'll stay.

Almost all of the work is done by two messages: a **welcome** the instant they sign up,
and an **activation nudge** a day or two later — sent *only if they haven't reached the
aha moment yet.* Together they plug the biggest hole in your funnel; separately, each
has a job precise enough to write on an index card.

### Message one — the welcome

The welcome is the receipt on their decision to sign up and the single push toward first
value, and it exists because peak intent is perishable: the further a user gets from the
signup click without doing the one thing that makes the product *click for them*, the less
likely they ever will. Its trigger is the signup event itself — the account-created /
`user.created` event your backend emits, not a nightly batch or a **cohort** sync (a cohort
being a group of users defined by a shared trait) — and its timing is *immediate*, within
seconds. This is the one lifecycle email where waiting costs you: the user is still in the
tab, so the welcome is a continuation of the session rather than an interruption of their
day, and the timezone and send-window discipline that governs everything else simply
doesn't apply. Send now.

Everyone who signs up should get it, exactly once. Guard against the double-fire — a user
who submits twice, a retried webhook — with a short suppression window so no one receives
two welcomes, and exit the journey the instant the account is deleted, because a churned or
bounced signup should never get the day-two follow-up. The content is a receipt, not a
brochure: one job, one CTA (call to action), and that CTA is *the single first action from
your activation funnel* — "create your first project," "connect your data," "invite a
teammate" — never a five-feature tour. Add one line setting expectations (what this list
is, how often you'll write) so the next email is welcome rather than a surprise; everything
else is noise competing with the first-value action. Whether it actually worked is a
separate question: opens and clicks only tell you the subject line and CTA are landing, and
the number that matters is downstream — the activation rate of welcomed users against a
small random holdout you deliberately don't email. That holdout is the only honest way to
separate "the email caused activation" from "these users were going to activate anyway,"
because correlation is not causation and a held-out group is how you tell the two apart on
your own program.

### Message two — the activation nudge

The activation nudge is a single, well-aimed follow-up for the users who signed up and then
*stalled* — the ones who never reached the aha moment — and it's where the signup-to-
activation leak actually gets plugged. The welcome catches the users who were always going
to activate; the nudge recovers the ones who wouldn't have. Its trigger isn't a timer
firing blindly but a *check*: a day or two after the welcome, the journey looks at whether
the user has hit the activation event yet and branches on the answer. Reached it, and they
don't need this email (send "here's what to try next," or nothing); didn't, and the nudge
goes out. That's behavioural personalisation — the send is conditioned on what the user
actually did, not on a guess.

Roughly **48 hours** is the common window: long enough that the keen users have already
activated and filtered themselves out, short enough that the stalled ones haven't gone cold.
But *when* on that day matters too — a fixed 48-hour delay after a 2am signup lands the
nudge at 2am, whereas sending at 9am in the user's own timezone, inside a sane send window,
is a small change that makes the whole program feel human rather than machine-timed. The
audience takes care of itself, because the branch reads behaviour before choosing what to
send: only the not-yet-activated get the nudge, everyone who reached the aha moment in the
gap is suppressed automatically, and a deleted account exits before the check ever runs. The
content should remove the *specific* blocker, not a generic "you haven't finished setup" —
if the activation action is "connect your data source," the nudge is a short, concrete
unstick for that exact step, the one link standing between them and value, one job and one
CTA, same as the welcome. To measure its lift, compare the activation rate of the nudged
cohort against a holdout that gets the welcome but not the nudge, which isolates the nudge's
own contribution — and watch the *reason* users stall, because if most are stuck on the same
step, that's a product signal worth more than any email.

### Why this pair is the whole ballgame

Activated users retain roughly **~2× better** than non-activated ones
([Lenny Rachitsky's activation benchmarks](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-a-good-activation-rate)),
and most early churn happens in exactly the window these two emails cover. Doubling
activation can beat doubling signups — at zero acquisition cost. That's why the welcome
pair is built first: it's the cheapest lever you have on the traffic you already paid
for, and it moves the metric (activation) that everything downstream compounds on.

The behavioural branch is what separates this from a drip. A drip would send both
emails to everyone on a timer; this program *reads what happened* and only sends the
nudge to the users who need it — respect and relevance in the same move. When the
mechanics matter (how the trigger, the wait, the behavioural check, and the timezone
scheduling actually get wired), [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover the
implementation; the strategy above is what makes the implementation worth building.

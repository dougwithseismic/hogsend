title: "Welcome + onboarding (Activation)"
description: "The highest-leverage pair in the whole program: a welcome the instant someone signs up, then an activation nudge a day or two later only if they haven't hit the aha moment yet. The trigger, the timing, the audience, the content, and how to measure the lift."
---

This is the first journey to build (Chapter 5, §5.3), and it's the highest-leverage
one you'll ever ship — because it patches the **widest leak in the bucket**: the gap
between signup and activation (Chapter 2). A new user is more engaged the moment after
signup than they will ever be again. Welcome emails post the highest open and click
rates of any automated message — commonly **35–50%** open rates
([Litmus's State of Email reports](https://www.litmus.com/state-of-email-reports) are
the least salesy place to calibrate). Spend that attention on the one action that
predicts they'll stay.

Almost all of the work is done by two messages: a **welcome** the instant they sign up,
and an **activation nudge** a day or two later — sent *only if they haven't reached the
aha moment yet.* Together they plug the biggest hole in your funnel; separately, each
has a job precise enough to write on an index card.

### Message one — the welcome

- **What it is, and why.** The receipt on their decision to sign up, and the single
  push toward first value. It exists because peak intent is perishable: the further a
  user gets from the signup click without doing the one thing that makes the product
  *click for them*, the less likely they ever will.
- **The trigger.** The signup event itself — the account-created / `user.created` event
  from your backend (Chapter 3). Not a nightly batch, not a cohort sync: the moment the
  account exists, the email should be on its way.
- **The timing.** Immediately — within seconds. This is the one lifecycle email where
  waiting costs you. The user is still in the tab; the welcome is a continuation of the
  session, not an interruption of their day. Timezone and send-window discipline (below)
  do *not* apply here — send now.
- **The audience and suppression.** Every new signup, exactly once. Guard against the
  double-fire (a user who submits twice, a retried webhook) with a short suppression
  window so one person never gets two welcomes. And exit the journey the instant the
  account is deleted — a churned or bounced signup should never receive the day-two
  follow-up.
- **The content: receipt, not brochure.** One job, one CTA. The CTA is *the single first
  action from your activation funnel* (Chapter 2 / Chapter 4) — "create your first
  project," "connect your data," "invite a teammate" — not a five-feature tour. Set
  expectations in one line (what this list is, how often you'll write) so the next email
  is welcome rather than a surprise. Everything that isn't the first-value action is
  noise competing with it.
- **How to measure it.** Opens and clicks tell you the subject line and CTA are working;
  they don't tell you the email *did anything.* The number that matters is downstream:
  the activation rate of welcomed users versus a small random holdout you deliberately
  don't email. That holdout is the only honest way to separate "the email caused
  activation" from "these users were going to activate anyway" — the same
  correlation-vs-causation discipline from Chapter 2, applied to your own program.

### Message two — the activation nudge

- **What it is, and why.** A single, well-aimed follow-up for the users who signed up
  and then *stalled* — the ones who never reached the aha moment. This is where the
  signup→activation leak actually gets plugged. The welcome catches the users who were
  always going to activate; the nudge is the one that recovers the ones who wouldn't
  have.
- **The trigger.** Not a fixed timer firing blindly — a *check.* A day or two after the
  welcome, the journey looks at whether the user has hit the activation event yet, and
  branches on the answer. Reached it → they don't need this email (send "here's what to
  try next," or nothing). Didn't → send the nudge. This is behavioural personalisation:
  the send is conditioned on what the user actually did, not on a guess (Chapter 5,
  §5.7).
- **The timing — and timezone as a tactic.** Roughly **48 hours** is the common window:
  long enough that the keen users have already activated (and are filtered out), short
  enough that the stalled ones haven't gone cold. But *when* on that day matters. A
  fixed 48-hour delay after a 2am signup lands the nudge at 2am. Sending "at 9am in the
  user's own timezone" instead — inside a sane send window — is a small change that makes
  the whole program feel human rather than machine-timed. Treat send-time as a
  deliverability and respect lever, not an afterthought.
- **The audience and suppression.** Only the not-yet-activated. Everyone who reached the
  aha moment in the gap is suppressed from the nudge automatically, because the branch
  reads their behaviour before choosing what to send. And, as always, an account that's
  been deleted exits before the check ever runs.
- **The content.** Remove the *specific* blocker, not a generic "you haven't finished
  setup." If the activation action is "connect your data source," the nudge is a short,
  concrete unstick for that exact step — the one link, the one thing standing between
  them and value. One job, one CTA, same as the welcome.
- **How to measure lift.** Compare activation rate for the nudged cohort against a
  holdout that gets the welcome but not the nudge. That isolates the nudge's own
  contribution. Watch the *reason* users stall, too — if most of them are stuck on the
  same step, that's a product signal worth more than any email.

### Why this pair is the whole ballgame

Activated users retain roughly **~2× better** than non-activated ones
([Lenny Rachitsky's activation benchmarks](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-a-good-activation-rate)),
and most early churn happens in exactly the window these two emails cover. Doubling
activation can beat doubling signups — at zero acquisition cost. That's why the welcome
pair is built first: it's the cheapest lever you have on the traffic you already paid
for, and it moves the metric (activation) that everything downstream compounds on.

The behavioural branch is what separates this from a drip. A drip would send both
emails to everyone on a timer; this program *reads what happened* and only sends the
nudge to the users who need it — respect and relevance in the same move. When the
mechanics matter (how the trigger, the wait, the behavioural check, and the timezone
scheduling actually get wired), [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover the
implementation; the strategy above is what makes the implementation worth building.

Every email so far has been good in isolation. But you won't run one — you'll run a dozen, all
firing off different events, and the same user is eligible for several at once. A new signup
who fails a payment is, on paper, in the welcome journey, the activation nudge, *and* dunning.
Without coordination, they get three emails in an afternoon and unsubscribe from all of it. The
difference between a lifecycle *system* and a pile of journeys is four rules.

The first and most important guardrail is a global frequency cap — a hard limit on how many
lifecycle emails one person can receive in a day or a week, enforced *across every journey* so
no combination can pile on. When journeys overlap, the cap decides who waits. It's the thing
that protects the user from your enthusiasm, and it protects your deliverability from the spam
complaints that overwhelmed users generate.

Second comes a priority order. When two emails could fire at the same moment, one has to win,
and the rule of thumb is simple: **money and trust beat promotion.** A failed-payment (dunning) email or a security
notice outranks a feature nudge; a transactional receipt always sends. Decide the order once,
up front, so the cap has something to sort by instead of dropping messages at random.

Third, keep a stage map. Lay every email you send against the funnel — Activation, Conversion,
Retention, Reactivation, Revenue, Referral, Feedback — and the gaps jump out. Most teams
discover they have three activation emails and nothing for reactivation, or a slick referral
ask and no win-back. The map is a five-minute coverage audit that tells you the next journey
worth building.

Finally, one global holdout. Individual holdouts prove one email's lift; a *program* holdout proves
the whole system's worth. Quietly hold a small random slice of users out of **all** lifecycle
email, and compare their retention and revenue to everyone else. That gap is what the lifecycle
program is actually worth — the number your monthly growth review asks for, and the one that
justifies the whole effort to a skeptical cofounder when you have to defend the program out
loud. Keep it running permanently; it's cheap insurance against a program that's quietly
stopped earning its keep.

Build the emails one at a time, in order of impact — but hold the *system* in mind from the
first one, because the second journey is where the coordination starts to matter.

title: "Feeding your journeys and proving lift"

---

A journey is only as good as the events feeding it, and only as trustworthy as your ability
to prove it works. Those are the two sides of the system around every journey you ship:
what *fires* it, and how you *measure* it.

### Where the events come from

Three sources feed your triggers, and most programs use all three:

- **Your product backend.** Your own application emits the events that describe what
  customers do — signed up, activated, hit a milestone, paid, cancelled. This is the
  primary source, and because it's sent server-side from your backend it's immune to
  ad-blockers (Chapter 3) in a way client-side tracking never is. These are events you
  already own; the lifecycle program just listens to them.
- **External webhooks.** Systems you don't control — Stripe, a form provider, a CRM, your
  billing platform — already emit webhooks when something happens. You point those at your
  lifecycle system, map each provider's payload onto your own event names once, and from
  then on a Stripe invoice failure is just another event in the same pipeline as a product
  signup. This is how the dunning trigger arrives (§07): the *billing system* is the source
  of truth, not a scheduled guess. ([The Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover
  wiring up a webhook source.)
- **PostHog cohorts.** Some audiences aren't a single event — they're a *pattern* best
  computed in your analytics tool: "signed up but didn't activate within 7 days," "active
  last month, silent this month." PostHog already maintains these as cohorts. You let
  PostHog decide *who* qualifies and have it hand that verdict to your lifecycle system,
  which then decides *what* to do. Nothing has to poll your database looking for dormancy —
  the analytics layer that's already computing it tells you.

That last source is the clean expression of the whole course's spine — **"PostHog detects,
the lifecycle acts"** (Chapter 0). The thing that's genuinely hard to compute in real time
(30-day dormancy across every user) is a cohort PostHog maintains for free; your program
just receives the verdict and runs. Detection and action stay in the tools that are best at
each.

### Proving lift with a holdout

Shipping a journey is not the same as knowing it worked. Opens and clicks tell you a message
was *seen*; they tell you nothing about whether it *caused* anything — those users might
have converted anyway. The only honest way to know is a **holdout**, the exact discipline
from Chapter 5:

- **Quietly withhold the messages from a small random slice of otherwise-eligible users.**
  They qualify for the journey in every way; they just don't get the emails. Everyone else
  does.
- **Keep the slice stable.** A given user should always land on the same side of the line —
  held out or messaged — not get re-rolled each time. Otherwise you can't cleanly compare
  the groups, and you'll message someone one week and hold them out the next.
- **Make the held-out group visible.** The control group has to be *observable* in your
  analytics — tagged as held-out — or you'll have nothing to compare against. A control you
  can't see isn't a control.
- **Compare outcomes, not opens.** Look at activation, retention, or revenue for the
  messaged group versus the held-out group. The *difference* is the incremental lift you
  can actually credit to the program — the number that survives scrutiny.

A worked read looks like Chapter 5's: messaged users convert at 18%, held-out users at 11%,
so the journey is worth **+7 points** of conversion you can attribute to it — not
correlation, cause. Use a *larger* holdout (20–50%) on low-volume journeys so the
comparison means something, decide your read date in advance so you don't stop the moment
it looks good, and hold out the *whole* experience rather than just some of the messages.

This is the growth-team experiment loop from Chapter 0 running continuously, in production,
on the journey you just shipped. And it's exactly where the answer comes from when Chapter
9's monthly review asks the only question that ultimately matters: **what is the lifecycle
program actually worth?**

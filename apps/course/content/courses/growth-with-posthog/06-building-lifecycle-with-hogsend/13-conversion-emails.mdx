Conversion emails move an activated free user to a paying one. The word that matters there
is *activated*. The most common mistake in the whole funnel is asking someone to pay before
they've felt the value — a trial user who never did the core thing, hit with a "your card
on file?" email, just unsubscribes. Conversion is **earned by activation**; an upgrade
email sent to someone who hasn't reached the aha moment is pushing on a door that was
never opened.

Once they *have* felt the value, three emails do most of the work:

- **The value recap.** Not a feature list — a mirror. Show them what they've already gotten
  out of the product (the reports they ran, the teammates they invited, the hours they saved)
  and, right beside it, what's behind the wall. The recap works because it's anchored in
  *their* usage, so it reads as "here's what you'd lose," which lands harder than "here's what
  you'd gain" — [loss aversion](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/loss-aversion/) is one of the
  more well-attested findings in behavioural work.
- **The trial-ending sequence.** A short run — a few days before, the day it ends, and a
  short grace window after — tied to *real* urgency, not a fake countdown. Manufactured
  scarcity ("only 2 hours left!" that resets on refresh) converts a few extra people once and
  costs you trust with everyone who notices. Honest urgency ("your trial ends Friday and here's
  exactly what changes") converts about as well and doesn't spend credibility.
- **The upgrade nudge, triggered by a wall.** The highest-intent conversion email is the one
  the user summons themselves by bumping into a limit — inviting a fourth teammate on a
  three-seat plan, hitting the export cap, reaching for a paid-only feature. That moment is
  peak willingness to pay, because the need is concrete and immediate. A journey that waits for
  that signal and sends one clean "unlock it" email beats any calendar-based drip.

The tactics under all three are the same as every lifecycle email: fire on the *right signal*
(a limit hit, a power action, a real trial deadline — not a fixed day), personalise on their
actual usage, keep it to one job and one CTA (*upgrade*), and prove it with a holdout so you
know the emails caused the conversions rather than riding on people who'd have paid anyway.

Freemium free-to-paid conversion commonly lands in the low single digits — often quoted around
2–5% *(directional; it varies enormously by product and how "free" is drawn)* — so small,
honest improvements here compound against a large base.

Of every message in your lifecycle program, this is the one that pays for the rest.
Dunning is the sequence that recovers a **failed payment** — and it earns more per email
sent than anything else you'll build, because it's aimed squarely at revenue you've
already won and are about to lose for no good reason.

**Why it's the highest-ROI message you'll send.** Somewhere between 20% and 40% of churn
is *involuntary* (Chapter 5, §5.3): not a customer who decided to leave, but a card that
expired, a bank that declined a charge, a limit that was briefly hit. These people still
want your product — they chose to pay and simply don't know they've stopped. A short,
well-timed nudge recovers a large fraction of them, which makes dunning the closest thing
to free money in the whole funnel. Every point of involuntary churn you claw back drops
straight to retained MRR.

**The trigger: a failed-charge webhook.** The sequence starts when your payment provider
tells you a charge failed — a Stripe `invoice.payment_failed`, or the equivalent from
whoever bills your customers. That webhook becomes an event in your pipeline, and that
event enrolls the customer in the dunning journey. The billing system is the source of
truth here, not a scheduled guess — you react the moment money actually fails to move.
(Wiring an external webhook into a trigger is covered in
[the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).)

**The shape: retry plus reminder.** Your payment processor is already retrying the card on
its own schedule (Stripe's Smart Retries, for example). Your job is the *human* half — the
message that says "your payment didn't go through, here's the one thing to fix it." So the
sequence is short and specific:

- **On failure:** a first message, immediately. Plain subject, plain body — the charge
  didn't go through, here's a one-click link to update the card.
- **A few days later, only if it's still unpaid:** one reminder. Same ask, a touch more
  urgent, and a note about what happens if it isn't fixed (the subscription pauses).
- Spaced across the window your processor gives you before it auto-cancels the
  subscription — typically about two weeks. Two messages, at most three. Dunning is not a
  drip campaign; it's a fix-this-one-thing prompt.

**The single most important rule: exit the instant payment clears.** The moment the retry
succeeds — or the customer updates their card — the journey must stop, cancelling any
pending reminder. Nothing corrodes trust faster than a "your payment failed" email landing
*after* the payment went through: it makes your billing look broken and your company look
asleep. So the journey watches for the payment-succeeded event and exits on it. This is
the same "exit the instant the reason is gone" discipline as win-back stopping when a user
returns — here it's just worth real dollars.

**Content: a receipt, not a brochure.** Dunning email is transactional in tone. State the
amount, name the card if you can ("your Visa ending 4242"), give one action (update
payment method) behind one link, and say what happens next. No product marketing, no
upsell, no "we miss you." The customer isn't lapsed — they're one click from staying. Get
out of their way.

**How you measure it.** Two numbers: **recovery rate** — of the charges that failed, what
fraction were paid within the window — and **dollars recovered per month**. Watch recovery
rate over time; if it sags, your copy or your timing has drifted. And because this journey
deliberately sends the same "update your card" message more than once, it leans harder
than any other on your tooling being *reliable* about repeat sends — which is the next
piece.

**Why the number matters.** Involuntary churn is a slice of your MRR bleeding out every
single month, and a retry-plus-reminder sequence recovers most of it — from customers who
already decided to pay you. Put your own numbers in below. For most paid products this is
the single calculation that justifies the entire afternoon it takes to build the journey —
and often the whole course.

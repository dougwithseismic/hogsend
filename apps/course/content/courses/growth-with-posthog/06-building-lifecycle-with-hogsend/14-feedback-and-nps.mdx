A feedback email asks the user what they think — and a good one is timed, short, and *wired
to do something with the answer*. Most teams get the first two and drop the third, which is
where the value was.

**Time it to a real success, not the calendar.** Ask right after the user felt something go
well — they hit the activation moment, completed a purchase, got a support issue resolved.
Asking a frustrated or idle user "how are we doing?" measures your worst moments and annoys
people; asking just after a win gets a higher response rate and a truer read. The trigger is
an event, like every other lifecycle email.

**Keep it to one question.** The classic is [Net Promoter](https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow)
("how likely are you to recommend us, 0–10?"), but a single open question — "what nearly
stopped you signing up?" — often teaches you more. Either way, one question in the email
itself, answerable in a tap, beats a link to a ten-field form nobody finishes. Treat the NPS
*number* as directional, not gospel — it's a widely used but [heavily critiqued](https://www.medallia.com/blog/net-promoter-score-nps-criticism/)
metric; the free-text *reasons* are where the signal is.

**Close the loop — and route the answer.** This is the part that turns a survey into a
lifecycle system:

- A **detractor** should trigger a save flow — a real human reply, or a "what went wrong?"
  journey — not vanish into a dashboard.
- A **promoter** is a warm referral ask (Chapter 8) or a review request, timed to the moment
  they just told you they love it.
- Every answer should feed *back into the product* — the reasons people give are your roadmap,
  and the patterns are the next thing to fix in onboarding or pricing.

So the feedback email isn't the end of a journey; it's a fork that starts the next one.

The last two atoms were about the *system* — the loop, and the six decisions behind each
message. This one is about the artefact itself: the email that actually lands in the
inbox. A lifecycle email lives under constraints a marketing page never faces. It has to
render in clients whose CSS support froze around 2009, clear the spam filters Chapter 5
described, and do exactly one job the moment someone glances at it. Good lifecycle email
is a craft, and it's worth learning what separates a message that works from one that
gets ignored — or worse, marked as spam.

### Deliverability is the first feature

None of the craft matters if the message lands in spam. Everything in Chapter 5's
deliverability section applies to each individual send: authenticate your domain, keep a
plain-text version so the message clears the filters and stays under Gmail's ~102KB clip
limit, and watch the phrases that drag a spam score up. The best-designed email in the
world scores zero if half of it never reaches an inbox — so treat "does this land?" as the
first question about a template, not the last.

### The rendering reality

Email is not the web. The clients your users read in — old *and* new — still don't support
most of the CSS you'd reach for on a landing page, so an email has to be built as
table-based HTML with inline styles to survive the trip. Modern component-based tooling
(the [React Email](https://react.email/docs/introduction) model) exists precisely to hide
this: you write ordinary components and it emits the cross-client HTML underneath. The
video below is Resend's founder building and sending a template end to end — the clearest
tour of what "author once, render everywhere" actually looks like.

**What to take from it:**

- The problem React Email exists to solve: email clients — old *and* new (Gmail, Yahoo,
  Outlook, Superhuman, Notion Mail) — still don't support SVG, `border-radius`, and most
  modern CSS, so you need a component layer that emits cross-client, table-based HTML.
- The workflow is three steps — *code* the template (unstyled components + Tailwind +
  typed props that fail the build when missing), *test* it (preview across clients, a
  linter that flags broken and 404 links), then *send* it (a `render` util turns the
  component into plain HTML any provider accepts).
- The existing open-source options were stale (one's last commit was seven years ago;
  another's docs were badly outdated), which is exactly why a fresh component-model tool
  was worth building.

### Plain text still matters

A serious lifecycle email ships with a plain-text version, not as an afterthought but as a
real signal. Filters read it as a sign of a well-formed message; screen readers rely on
it; and plenty of people read in a preview pane where the plain text is what they see
first. Skipping it costs you deliverability and accessibility at once — two things a
lifecycle program can't afford to leak.

### Receipt, not brochure

Here is the single most useful mental model for lifecycle email: **a lifecycle email is
closer to a receipt than a brochure.** One job, one call to action, plain and fast. The
best lifecycle emails look almost boring — a sentence of context, the one action you want,
a button, done. That's not a lack of polish; it's the polish. A user who opened your "your
payment failed" email wants a card-update link, not a hero image and three testimonials.
The transactional-email conventions worth internalising —
[Postmark's guide](https://postmarkapp.com/guides/transactional-email-best-practices) is
the standard reference — all point the same way: respect the reader's time, say the one
thing, make the action unmissable.

### Test it before thousands of people see it

A template is about to sit in front of every user a trigger matches, so it's worth
actually looking at it first. Modern tooling makes this cheap: preview every template with
sample data across clients, send a test to yourself against
[test addresses](https://resend.com/docs/dashboard/emails/send-test-emails) (or a capture
sandbox — [Mailtrap's testing guide](https://mailtrap.io/blog/how-to-test-email/) covers
the methodology) before pointing at real users, and run the copy through a spam checker in
preview rather than discovering the problem after a send. The last video covers exactly
this linting-and-compatibility layer.

**What to take from it:**

- React Email 4 adds a spam checker (it runs SpamAssassin, the standard open-source tool)
  that scores your email the way inboxes will and flags the phrases dragging the score
  down — catch deliverability problems in preview, not after a send.
- The linter surfaces accessibility and performance issues — a missing `alt` tag, an
  oversized image that slows the client — and links each finding straight to the offending
  line.
- The compatibility checker (backed by "Can I email") flags CSS that breaks in specific
  clients — `object-fit`, or `height` vs `min-height` diverging between Outlook and Yahoo —
  so the "Outlook breaks this" bug is caught in the preview instead of a customer's inbox.

### Own your emails

There's a strategic decision hiding inside all of this: *where do your emails live?* The
common default is a rented drag-and-drop tool, where each template lives in someone else's
dashboard. It's fast to start, and it costs you three things that compound over time.

- **You can't version or review them.** An email that lives in a dashboard has no history
  you can diff, no pull request where a teammate catches the broken link before it ships,
  and no way to tell which version went out with last quarter's campaign. When you own the
  email, it sits in your codebase next to everything else — reviewable, revertable, and
  diffable like any other change.
- **Personalisation is capped at the tool's merge tags.** A rented editor lets you drop in
  a first name and whatever fields it chose to expose. When the email is yours, it can be
  personalised on *your* data — the same activation events, plan details, and behaviour
  you already track — so the message can say something the merge-tag menu never could.
- **Content and plumbing get tangled.** In the owned model the split stays clean: the
  journey decides *who* gets mail and *when*; the template decides only *what it says*. The
  same separation your product already keeps between logic and view — logic never
  concatenates HTML, and a view never decides who it's for.

None of this argues against a hosted *sending provider* — you still want someone reliable
putting bytes on the wire, and Resend, Postmark, and the like do that job well. The
argument is about where the *email itself* is authored and reviewed: in your repo, under
version control, personalised on your own data — versus locked in a canvas you rent. For
how Hogsend renders these owned templates and hands them to whichever provider you've
configured, see [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).

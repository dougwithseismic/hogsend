---
title: "Reliability: the system has to be trustworthy"
description: "The failure mode that kills a lifecycle program isn't a missed email — it's a double-send. Why exactly-once delivery and safe re-runs are a requirement, not a nicety, and what to expect from your tooling."
---

Everything in this chapter has quietly assumed one thing: that when your program decides to
send a message, it sends it **once** — not zero times, not twice. That assumption is doing
enormous work, and it's the difference between a lifecycle program people trust and one they
quietly route around.

**The failure mode that kills trust isn't a dropped email — it's a double-send.** A missed
message is invisible; nobody complains about an email they never got. A *duplicate* is
loud. Two identical welcomes make you look broken. Two "your payment failed" notices —
especially one that lands after the card already cleared — make you look alarming. A
referral ask that arrives three times makes you look desperate. Users don't file a bug;
they mark you as spam, and internally your own team stops trusting the program enough to
add to it. Reliability isn't a polish item you get to later. It's the foundation the whole
program stands on.

**Why this is genuinely hard — and why you should care that it's handled.** Durable
lifecycle systems survive crashes and deploys by *replaying* a journey from the top and
fast-forwarding through the steps that already happened. That's exactly what lets a
three-day wait survive a Tuesday redeploy. But it also means that if a worker dies at the
wrong instant — *after* the email provider accepted your send but *before* the system
recorded it as done — a naïve replay re-runs the send and the user gets two. At any real
scale, every deploy becomes a small chance of embarrassing some slice of your users. The
mechanism that makes long waits reliable is the same mechanism that, done carelessly,
double-sends.

**So here's what to demand from your tooling, as the growth owner:**

- **Exactly-once delivery by default.** A crash, a redeploy, or a retry mid-journey must
  never turn one intended message into two. This should be the engine's job — automatic,
  with nothing extra for you to remember on the common path — not a discipline you
  hand-maintain send by send.
- **Deliberate repeat sends stay possible.** Some journeys send the *same* message twice on
  purpose — the dunning reminder is the same "update your card" template as the first
  notice. Your tooling has to tell the difference between "the same send, replayed by a
  crash" (collapse it) and "a second send I meant to happen" (deliver it). If getting that
  distinction right needs care, the tool should catch a mistake *loudly in development* —
  not silently drop a message in production.
- **A clear re-enrollment policy.** Separate from crashes, you need a deliberate answer to
  "can this customer enter this journey again?" A welcome should fire **once** — a person
  signs up once. Win-back and dunning should be **once per period** — a customer can
  genuinely lapse again, but shouldn't be re-nudged daily. A few counting journeys (like
  the Discord engagement loop in Chapter 8) are **unlimited** by design. This is a policy
  decision you make per journey; the tool just has to enforce it.
- **Determinism where a choice drives the message.** If something non-deterministic picks
  *what* to send — an LLM choosing a variant, a random A/B split — a replay must not
  re-roll that choice into a different-but-duplicate message. The decision has to be
  recorded once and reused. Same for timestamps: anchor to the event's own time, not "now,"
  so a replay reads the same clock.

You get this kind of correctness *because* the journey runs inside a system purpose-built
for it. Exactly-once delivery across redeploys is brutally hard to get right by hand — it's
one of the subtlest reasons a dedicated engine beats a roll-your-own stack, which is
exactly the trade-off the next piece weighs. For how the engine actually guarantees it —
the idempotency keys, the replay-safe primitives, the loud dev-time errors — see
[the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs). The point for you as the growth owner is
simpler: reliability is a requirement you get to *assume*, and a program you can't trust to
send exactly once is a program no one will let you grow.

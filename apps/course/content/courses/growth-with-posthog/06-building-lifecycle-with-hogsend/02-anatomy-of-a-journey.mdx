---
title: "Anatomy of a lifecycle message"
description: "Every lifecycle email is really six decisions — the trigger, the audience, the timing, the content, the exit, and the measurement. The choices a growth person makes before anyone writes a line of code."
---

Every lifecycle email you'll ever build comes down to six decisions. Get them right and
the implementation is almost mechanical; get them wrong and no amount of clever copy
saves the message. These are the decisions a growth person owns — the tool just carries
them out. Here they are in the order you actually make them.

### 1. The trigger — which event starts it

The trigger is the single behaviour that means *now is the moment*. Choosing it is
choosing when the message fires, so it's the most consequential decision on the list. "A
user signed up" is a trigger. So is "a user hit their fifth project," "a payment failed,"
or "no activity for fourteen days."

Precision here is everything. "Signed up" and "signed up *and is on the free plan*" are
different triggers that produce different programs — the second one never wastes an
upgrade sequence on someone who paid at the door. The tighter you can describe the
moment, the less filtering you have to do later. The events you defined back in Chapter 3
are exactly the raw material; the craft is picking the one that most precisely marks the
moment this message earns its place in the inbox.

### 2. The audience — who's eligible, and who to suppress

A trigger fires for a lot of people. The audience decision narrows it to the people this
message is actually *for*, and — just as importantly — excludes the people it would harm.

Two separate questions live here:

- **Eligibility.** Who qualifies? Free-plan users only. Trial users in their first week.
  Accounts over a certain size. This is a filter on top of the trigger.
- **Suppression and re-enrollment.** Who should *not* get this even though they qualify?
  Someone who's already mid-upgrade shouldn't get the upgrade nudge. Someone who finished
  this sequence last week shouldn't be dropped straight back into it. You decide whether a
  user can enter this journey *once ever*, *once per period*, or *every time the trigger
  fires* — and that re-enrollment policy is one of the most common places lifecycle
  programs go wrong. Set it too loose and you spam; too tight and you miss a legitimate
  second occasion.

This is a correctness decision, not a copy decision. The best-written email sent to the
wrong person is a complaint waiting to happen — and Chapter 5's deliverability math is
unforgiving about complaint rate.

### 3. The timing — the delay, and the time of day

Two sub-decisions, both easy to under-think.

The **delay** is a tactic. Fire the welcome email instantly — intent is never higher than
the second after signup. But the "you've been quiet" nudge needs to wait long enough that
being quiet actually means something, and the "how's it going?" check-in lands better on
day three than day zero. Too soon feels robotic; too late misses the moment.

The **time of day** is the one teams forget. A message that resolves to "send two days
later" will, naively, fire at whatever 3am-in-the-user's-timezone your server clock
happens to hit. The fix is to send in the *user's* local time and inside a sane send
window — a well-timed 9am email beats a perfectly-written 3am one, because nobody reads
the 3am one. Deciding this is a strategy call ("breakfast, their time"); making it happen
reliably is what the engine handles for you — see [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs)
for the timezone and send-window mechanics.

### 4. The content — one job, one CTA

A lifecycle email is closer to a *receipt* than a *brochure*: one job, one call to action,
plain and fast. The content decision is really a decision about *restraint* — what to
leave out. Every lifecycle message should be able to answer "what is the one thing I want
this person to do?" in a single sentence. If it can't, it's two emails wearing a trench
coat. The next atom goes deep on the craft of the email itself; for now, the anatomical
point is that "one job" is a design constraint you set here, before a word is written.

### 5. The exit — the event that stops it

The exit is the mirror image of the trigger, and it's every bit as important. It's the
event that ends the journey early, the instant it happens.

The canonical case: an upgrade sequence should stop the *moment* the user upgrades. If it
doesn't, they get "still thinking about upgrading?" a day after they already paid — which
reads as "we don't even know you're a customer," the most trust-eroding message you can
send. This is Chapter 5's suppression principle made concrete: the exit is how a journey
knows when it has been overtaken by events and should get out of the way.

A journey that's parked mid-wait — say, five days into a check-in delay — has to be able
to abort cleanly when its exit event arrives, so no later step fires after the reason for
it has gone. Deciding *what* stops the message is the growth call; wiring the exit so it
fires even mid-wait is engine territory
([the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover it).

### 6. The measurement — did it lift anything

The last decision closes the loop from the previous atom: how will you know this message
worked? A lifecycle email exists to move a number — activation, recovered revenue,
reactivation. If you can't say which number and can't see it move, you've built a message
you can neither improve nor safely retire.

So every message should report back — emit events on send, open, click, and (ideally) the
downstream action it was trying to cause — so the effect shows up in PostHog next to the
funnel it's meant to help. This is what lets you answer the only question that matters
after launch: *is this email earning its place, or is it quietly costing me sender
reputation for no lift?* A message you can't measure is a message you'll never turn off,
even when it's hurting.

---

Those six — trigger, audience, timing, content, exit, measurement — are the whole anatomy.
Notice that five of the six are pure growth judgment; none of them is a coding problem.
The value you add is in the decisions. The engine's job is to execute them faithfully, in
the right order, for the right user, at the right time — and to keep doing it through
every deploy and restart. For how each decision maps to an actual journey, see
[the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).

Most onboarding grows past a single welcome-and-nudge pair into a **series** — three to
five touches over the first week or two — and the good ones all share one shape:

> **Wait a beat. Check whether the action you wanted actually happened. Send the next
> nudge only if it didn't.**

That single loop — sleep, check, send-only-if-needed — covers most onboarding programs
you'll ever write. It's one principle — branch on behaviour, not on a guess — turned into
a repeatable rhythm.

### Why "send only if needed" beats a fixed drip

A fixed drip fires every message to every signup on a timer, regardless of what they've
done. The behavioural series only reaches the users who actually stalled — and that
difference is both strategic and moral:

- **Relevance.** "You haven't tried the key feature yet" is only a good email *if it's
  true.* A drip sends it to the user who tried the feature an hour ago, and instantly
  reads as "this company doesn't know what I'm doing." The behavioural check makes every
  send true by construction.
- **Respect.** Attention is the scarcest thing a new user gives you. Spending it only
  when you have something the user genuinely needs is how you keep the *next* email
  worth opening. Every irrelevant send is a small withdrawal from that account.
- **Signal quality.** Because each touch is gated on a real event, the series doubles as
  a funnel diagnostic: the step where most users still need nudging is your product's
  worst stall, visible for free.

### How the nudges escalate

Escalation doesn't mean louder — it means *later.* Each touch targets the next blocker
in the activation path, not a more urgent version of the last message:

1. **First nudge** (~day 2): the first-value action from your activation funnel — the
   thing the welcome already asked for, now unblocked more concretely.
2. **Second nudge** (~day 3–4): the *next* step, the one that only makes sense once the
   first is done — deeper setup, the second habit, the integration that makes the
   product sticky.
3. **Later touches**, if any: help, social proof, or a human offer ("reply if you're
   stuck") — used sparingly.

Each message assumes the user completed the previous step, because the behavioural
gate guarantees the ones still receiving mail haven't. The series walks the stalls in
order rather than dumping the whole product on day one.

### When to stop

This is the discipline that separates a series from nagging:

- **Stop the instant they activate.** The moment the user reaches the aha moment, the
  series has done its job — every further nudge now contradicts their behaviour and
  erodes the trust the welcome earned. The whole program should end there, mid-sequence,
  automatically.
- **Cap the number of touches.** Three to five, then stop, even if they never activated.
  A series with no end trains people to tune you out or unsubscribe — and an
  unsubscribe costs you every *future* journey, not just this one.
- **Never nudge a churned or unsubscribed user.** Deleted accounts exit; unsubscribes
  are honoured at send time. Re-checking who still wants to hear from you before a late
  send keeps the program's logic honest.

### How to prove it worked

Hold back a random slice of stalled users from the nudges and compare their activation
rate against the nudged cohort. The gap is the lift the series actually *caused* — not
the correlation you'd get by comparing "users who opened the emails" (who were always
your keenest). When the timing, the behavioural checks, and the exit-on-activation wiring
matter, [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover the implementation; the
strategy here is what decides whether it's worth wiring at all.

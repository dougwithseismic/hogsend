Your happiest customers are your cheapest, highest-converting acquisition channel — a warm
introduction from someone who already loves the product beats any ad you could buy. The
referral email is how you ask for that introduction. Almost everything about this journey
is timing and taste, because a referral ask at the wrong moment doesn't just fail — it
spends goodwill you worked to earn.

**Ask at peak satisfaction.** The rule from Chapter 5 is precise: ask right after the
customer *feels* the value — the aha moment, a shipped result, a milestone hit, a renewal,
a five-star support interaction. That's when they'd actually recommend you to a friend,
because the good feeling is fresh. Ask on a random Tuesday and you're interrupting; ask a
beat after a great outcome and you're riding a wave they're already on.

- **The trigger is a real win, not the calendar.** Fire the journey off a *milestone* or
  success event — the same behavioural events you're already tracking (Chapter 3) — not a
  fixed "day 14" timer. The event *is* the moment of satisfaction.
- **Wait a beat, don't pounce.** Let a little time pass after the win so the ask feels like
  a natural afterglow rather than a popup bolted onto the achievement. A short delay reads
  as thoughtful; an instant ask reads as a script.

**Only ask users who are still active.** Before the send, check that the person has
actually used the product recently — a session, a login, real activity in the last few
weeks. Never ask a disengaged or churning customer to vouch for you: at best it's ignored,
at worst it reminds someone on their way out that they're on their way out. The guard is
simple — no recent activity, no ask — and it's the whole difference between a referral
program and a spray of hopeful emails.

**Make sharing effortless.** The email should carry a personal referral link, pre-written
share copy the customer can send as-is, and a single obvious action. Every extra step —
"log in, find the referrals tab, copy your code" — roughly halves the number of people who
follow through. The easier you make the share, the more shares you get; this is mechanical,
not motivational.

**Reward real referrals.** The ask converts far better when there's something in it — for
the referrer, the friend, or both (double-sided rewards are the classic). But the reward
should fire on a *real* referral: a friend who actually signed up or converted, tracked on
an identity graph you own — not on a click or a good intention.

**How you measure it.** Referral emails sent, invites generated, and — the number that
matters — invites that convert to real signups. Watch the conversion, not the send volume;
a small, well-timed program that converts beats a blast that gets ignored.

That last point — turning the ask into a *tracked, rewarded conversion on an identity graph
you own* — is the referral **loop**, and it's the whole of Chapter 8. This journey is just
the ask: the well-timed tap on the shoulder that starts the loop, and getting the timing
and the audience right here is what makes the loop worth building there. (For wiring the
ask itself, see [the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).)

Chapter 5 made the case for lifecycle messaging as a discipline. This chapter is the
concrete, tactical continuation: how the messages actually get built, and the decisions
behind each one. Everything here rests on a single loop that every lifecycle message —
welcome, dunning, win-back, all eight — runs through:

1. **Something happens.** A real thing a user did: they signed up, hit an activation
   milestone, let their card expire, went quiet for two weeks. This is an *event* — the
   same server-side events you're already sending to PostHog in Chapter 3.
2. **The right message goes out at the right time.** Not to everyone — to *that one user*,
   keyed to what they just did, after whatever delay makes it land well.
3. **You measure whether it worked.** The message reports back — it emits its own events —
   so you can see in PostHog whether it lifted the number you built it to lift.

That's the whole model. A message is worth building only when you can name all three:
the event that starts it, the thing you want to happen, and the way you'll know if it did.

### Why per-user beats a batch blast

This loop is the difference between lifecycle messaging and a newsletter, and it's worth
being precise about why it wins — because it's the same argument Chapter 5 made, now at
the level of a single send.

A **batch blast** is calendar-driven: you pick a Tuesday, write one message, and send it
to everyone on the list at once. It ignores what any individual user is doing. The person
who signed up an hour ago and the person who's been paying for two years get the same
email at the same moment — one of whom it's wrong for.

A **triggered, per-user loop** is behaviour-driven: each user gets the message the moment
their own behaviour earns it. The welcome email fires when *they* sign up, not on the
sender's schedule. The upgrade nudge fires when *they* hit the usage ceiling. Relevance
stops being a thing you guess at and becomes a property of *when* the message is allowed
to fire.

The most underrated consequence of this is what the loop *doesn't* do. A batch blast has
no way to know a user already did the thing — so it emails them anyway. A triggered loop
only reaches the people the event actually describes, and stops the instant they move
past it. As Chapter 5 put it, the power of a lifecycle program is as much in who it
*doesn't* email as who it does. The loop is what makes that restraint automatic rather
than a thing you police by hand.

### One event, two jobs

The events that drive this loop are the same server-side events you're already emitting
for analytics. You don't build a separate tracking layer for lifecycle — the moment your
backend records "this user signed up" for PostHog, that same signal can start a journey.
One emission, two consumers: analytics *measures* it, lifecycle *acts on* it.

Sending these events from your server rather than the browser matters for a second
reason Chapter 3 raised: it's ad-blocker-proof. Roughly a third of users block
client-side scripts, so a lifecycle program that depended on a browser tag would silently
skip a third of the people it was built to reach. Server-side events don't have that
hole — every user who does the thing gets counted, and gets the message.

### The messages have to wait — reliably

There's one property of lifecycle work that shapes every tool built for it: a lifecycle
message is mostly *waiting*. Send, wait two days, check, send, wait five days, check. A
single journey can hold a user in mid-flight for weeks.

That waiting has to survive the real world. Your servers get redeployed, restarted, and
occasionally fall over — and none of that is allowed to strand a user halfway through a
sequence or fire a two-days-later email six days late. Getting this right by hand (a
spreadsheet of "who's due what, when," poked by a nightly cron) is exactly the fragile
plumbing that makes teams give up on lifecycle. It's infrastructure you want handled for
you, so you can spend your attention on the *decisions* — which is what the rest of this
chapter is about. (For how Hogsend makes the waiting durable, see
[the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs).)

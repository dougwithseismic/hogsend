---
title: "Win-back (Reactivation)"
description: "Re-engaging a user who's gone dormant: defining dormancy for your product, giving them a real reason to return, and the one-window-then-stop discipline that keeps win-back feeling human instead of desperate."
---

Win-back re-engages a user who's gone dormant — slipped from active to silent without
formally cancelling. It plugs the **resurrection** leak in AARRR: the pool of users who
already understood the value, already got through onboarding, and then drifted. That's
why it's worth building — reactivating a lapsed user is far cheaper than acquiring a new
one, and you're not persuading a stranger, you're reminding someone who already believed.
Commonly cited reactivation rates land in the **10–30% of lapsed users** band
([Klaviyo's win-back examples](https://www.klaviyo.com/blog/winback-email-campaign-examples)
are e-commerce but the shape transfers) — *directional*, but enough to matter.

### First, define dormancy for *your* product

Everything about win-back depends on one number, and it's not a number you can borrow.
Dormancy is the inactivity window that means "this user is slipping away," and it comes
straight from your product's natural usage rhythm:

- **30 days idle in a daily-use product is churn.** In a tax-filing product, it's a
  Tuesday. Same 30 days, opposite meaning.
- Your threshold comes from **your retention curve** (Chapter 2) — the interval after
  which a silent user almost never comes back on their own. Find the point where the
  curve flattens into "gone" and set the dormancy window just before it, while a nudge
  can still catch them.
- Set it **too short** and you'll pester active users between normal sessions — the
  fastest way to *cause* the churn you were trying to prevent. Set it **too long** and
  you're emailing people who have already emotionally cancelled. The window is a tuning
  dial, not a default.

That threshold becomes the **trigger**: a dormancy event — "no core action in N days" —
that you emit from a scheduled check or a PostHog cohort that fires when a user crosses
the line. The journey subscribes to that event; getting the definition right is 80% of
the work before a single word of copy is written.

### Give them a real reason to return — not "we miss you"

The instinct is to lead with sentiment. Resist it. *"We miss you"* answers no question
the user actually has, and it makes the email about you. Lead with what changed since
they left — the concrete value waiting for them now:

- **What's new / what they're missing.** The feature that shipped, the data that
  accumulated, the thing that's better than when they left. Give them a reason grounded
  in *their* interest, not your loneliness.
- **One job, one CTA.** A win-back is a receipt, not a brochure — the single most
  compelling reason to come back, and one link to act on it. Bury it in a feature roundup
  and you've wasted the one open you were going to get.
- **Personalise on their history where you can.** "Here's what happened in the project
  you started" beats a generic "here's what's new" — the behavioural detail is what
  makes it feel addressed to a person rather than a segment.

### The one-window-then-stop discipline

This is what keeps win-back from tipping into desperation. The whole program is: **send
once, give them a bounded window to come back on their own, send exactly one final nudge
if they don't — then stop.**

- **Bound the comeback window as a tactic.** After the first email, don't fire the
  second on a blind timer. *Wait to see if they return* — give them, say, five days — and
  only send the final nudge if the window elapses with no sign of them. That bounded
  wait is what makes win-back feel human: the program is listening for the user's own
  behaviour, not counting down regardless.
- **Exit the instant they return.** If the user becomes active at any point — even the
  day after the first email — the journey stops immediately. Nothing is more corrosive
  to trust than a "we miss you" email landing the morning after someone logged back in.
  Returning is the goal; the moment it happens, the program has no further business.
- **One window, then leave them alone.** Don't restart the sequence next week. A user who
  ignored a genuine reason to return and a final nudge has told you something; hammering
  them past that just earns unsubscribes and spam complaints, which cost you every future
  journey. Cap re-entry to once per dormancy period.
- **Re-check consent after the wait.** A long wait can straddle an unsubscribe. An exit
  event ends the journey; an unsubscribe is a preference honoured at send time — but
  confirming who still wants to hear from you before the final nudge keeps the program's
  logic honest.

### How to measure lift

Compare the return rate of the win-back cohort against a holdout of dormant users you
deliberately don't email. Some lapsed users drift back on their own; the holdout is the
only way to tell how many you actually *reactivated* versus how many would have returned
anyway. Watch the downstream number too — a user who opens the email but churns again in
a week wasn't really won back. When the dormancy trigger, the bounded wait-for-return,
and the exit-on-activity wiring matter,
[the Hogsend docs](https://hogsend.com/docs) cover the implementation; the strategy above
is what makes it worth building.

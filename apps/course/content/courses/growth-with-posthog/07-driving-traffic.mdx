## 7.1 Where are you starting from?

The rest of this chapter reads differently depending on where you're starting from, so
it's worth pausing on your current mix before the tactics. A team that's all-organic is
really asking whether paid is worth *starting*; a team that's mostly-paid tends to need
the measurement and owned-audience work first, because every tracking gap and every
rented audience is already costing money.

Two facts frame everything that follows:

- **Channels decay.** Andrew Chen's
  [law of shitty clickthroughs](https://andrewchen.com/the-law-of-shitty-clickthroughs/)
  is the empirical observation that every marketing channel's performance degrades as
  it saturates — banner ads went from 78% CTR in 1994 to ~0.05%, and every channel
  since has followed the same curve. Whatever works for you now will work less well
  per dollar next year. The counter isn't channel-hopping; it's capturing what a
  channel brings you into something you own (Chapter 8) while the channel still works.
- **Early traction rarely comes from paid at all.** Paul Graham's
  [Do Things that Don't Scale](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) is the canonical
  argument: almost all startups get their first users manually — recruiting one at a
  time, launching in communities, doing unscalable favours — and the founders who wait
  for ads to do it stall. If you're pre-revenue, this chapter's job is mostly to stop
  you spending money you shouldn't spend yet.

## 7.2 The channel menu — and how to choose

Meta is this chapter's worked example because it's the most common paid channel and its
tracking story teaches the privacy lessons every channel shares. But a growth team picks
the channel that fits its product, and for many readers of this course Meta is *not*
the first choice:

- **Google Ads (Search).** Intent-based — you reach people *already searching* for the
  solution. For products with clear search demand (someone Googling "invoice software"),
  this often converts far better than interrupting a feed. Has its own server-side
  conversion path (Enhanced Conversions, the Google Ads API).
- **TikTok / Reddit / X ads.** TikTok for consumer and younger demographics; Reddit for
  niche/technical communities (it can work surprisingly well for dev tools in the right
  subreddits); X for tech/founder audiences. Each has its own pixel + a server-side
  events API with the same dedup logic as §7.7.
- **LinkedIn Ads.** Expensive per click, but the only platform with reliable job-title/
  company targeting — the B2B choice when your buyer is defined by role.
- **The non-paid channels that often beat paid for this audience:**
  - **Content / SEO** — compounding, owned, and the canonical growth engine for dev
    tools and B2B (it's *why* Supabase, PostHog itself, and others grew). Slow to
    start, nearly free to sustain. Rob Walling's rule of thumb for bootstrapped SaaS
    is that content is a 6–12-month investment before it pays — budget the patience,
    not just the writing.
  - **Launches** — [Show HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/showhn.html) (the actual
    rules are worth reading first: no marketing-speak, the thing must be tryable),
    Product Hunt, and the
    subreddit/newsletter/Discord ecosystem of your niche. Launches are spikes, not
    engines — their real value is seeding the loop: every launch visitor you capture
    into an owned channel (Chapter 8) keeps paying after the spike dies.
  - **Community** — Discord/Reddit/Slack communities, the subject of Chapter 8. For
    community-led and web3 products this *is* the acquisition channel.
  - **Partnerships / integrations / marketplaces** — listing in the ecosystem your
    users already live in (a Shopify app, a Vercel integration, a Raycast extension).
    You inherit the platform's traffic and its trust.

## 7.3 The maths that decides: CAC, LTV, payback

Whatever the channel, the same three numbers decide whether it's working. The
definitions matter — a16z's
[16 Startup Metrics](https://a16z.com/16-startup-metrics/) exists because founders
routinely get them wrong in ways that flatter the business:

- **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** — *fully loaded* spend to acquire one paying
  customer: ad spend plus the content, tools, and people behind it, divided by
  customers acquired. The classic self-deception is reporting CAC on ad spend alone,
  or on signups instead of customers.
- **LTV (lifetime value)** — the **gross-margin** contribution of a customer over their
  lifetime, where lifetime comes from your churn rate (roughly, monthly margin ÷
  monthly churn). Computing LTV on revenue instead of margin is the second classic
  self-deception — a16z calls both out by name.
- **Payback period** — months of gross margin to recover CAC. For a cash-constrained
  startup this is often the number that matters most: an LTV:CAC of 5 is cold comfort
  if payback takes three years and the bank account empties in one.

The folk benchmark you'll hear is **LTV:CAC ≥ 3 and payback under ~12 months** for
SaaS — better treated as a sanity range than a law; what actually protects you is
knowing your numbers and their trend. Two habits follow from that:

- **CAC is most useful per channel, not blended.** A blended CAC averages your free
  organic users into the paid number, which can hide a channel that's quietly
  unprofitable. Blended CAC is a board metric; per-channel CAC is the operating one.
  (Chapter 9's monthly ritual reviews blended CAC precisely to see whether
  owned/referral growth is pulling it down over time.)
- **LTV is worth segmenting by cohort and source.** Users from different channels
  retain differently — paid-social users commonly churn faster than organic (they had
  less intent). If paid cohorts' LTV is half your organic LTV, your real paid LTV:CAC
  may be under water while the blended number looks fine.

## 7.4 The shape of a Meta campaign

From here to §7.9, the paid-social worked example. Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ads have a
fixed three-tier structure, and it's worth getting comfortable with it first because
everything else hangs off it:

- **Campaign** — sets the **objective** and budget strategy. One objective per campaign.
- **Ad Set** — the levers: **audience, placements, the optimisation event, budget,
  schedule**.
- **Ad** — the creative the user sees (image/video, copy, CTA, destination).

The single most consequential choice is the **campaign objective**, because it tells
Meta's delivery algorithm *which kind of person to go find*. Meta consolidated its old
objectives into six (ODAX):

1. **Awareness** — reach/recall, top of funnel.
2. **Traffic** — cheap clicks to a page. *Not* optimised for conversions — a common
   beginner trap is to use Traffic and wonder why clicks don't convert. Traffic buys you
   visitors who clicked; Sales buys you visitors who *act*. They are different people.
3. **Engagement** — interactions, video views, messages.
4. **Leads** — form fills, newsletter signups.
5. **App Promotion** — installs and in-app events.
6. **Sales** — purchases / highest-intent conversions.

**The rule of thumb worth carrying:** optimise for the event closest to revenue that
you can feed Meta *enough of*. The algorithm needs roughly **50 conversions per ad set
per week** to exit its "learning phase" and deliver efficiently. If you can't generate
50 purchases a week yet, a higher-funnel event (lead or signup) as the optimisation
target — with the deeper conversion passed back server-side (§7.7) — keeps the
algorithm fed. The most common early pattern goes the other way: optimising for
`purchase` at five a week starves the algorithm of signal, and it never learns who to
find.

Two practical corollaries of the learning phase:

- **Edits reset learning.** Every significant change (budget, audience, creative) can
  send the ad set back to school. The pattern that hurts — check daily, worry, edit —
  keeps it permanently in learning and permanently expensive. Weekly changes, on the
  cadence from Chapter 9, give the system time to settle.
- **Fewer, bigger ad sets learn faster.** Five ad sets splitting 10 conversions a week
  will all starve; one ad set receiving all 50 can learn. Consolidation is the modern
  default.

## 7.5 The three audiences

Every ad set targets one of three kinds of audience:

- **Broad / interest ("cold").** Demographics and interests, or — increasingly — fully
  broad "Advantage+" audiences where you let Meta's ML find people. The 2024–2026 trend
  is clear: **broad targeting + strong creative + a good conversion signal now beats
  hand-built interest stacks**, because the algorithm does the targeting. Interest
  targeting is more of a hint than a filter now — which shifts the work from audience-
  picking to *creative* and *signal quality* (§7.7).
- **Custom Audiences ("warm", first-party).** Built from *your* data: a customer-list
  upload, site/app visitors, or events sent via the Conversions API. These are the
  highest-signal inputs you can give Meta — and they only exist because you captured
  first-party identity, which is the whole thesis of Chapter 8.
- **Lookalike Audiences ("prospecting").** Meta finds new people resembling a **seed**
  custom audience. **Seed quality dominates the result** — your *best* customers
  (recent purchasers, high-LTV) make a far better seed than "everyone who ever signed
  up." A 1% lookalike is the tightest match; widening to 3–10% trades match for scale.

### The full funnel

A standard structure layers these:

- **Top (cold):** Awareness/Traffic/Video to broad audiences. Cheap short video both
  earns reach *and* manufactures the video-viewer/engager audiences you'll retarget.
- **Middle (warm):** retarget site visitors, video viewers, engagers with
  trust/education content.
- **Bottom (hot):** retarget pricing-page visitors, trial-starters, cart abandoners
  with a direct offer.

> A starting split you'll see quoted is ~50–60% cold / 20–30% warm / 15–20% hot
> *(directional heuristic, not gospel)*. The pattern that catches small founders is
> over-investing in retargeting too early — a tiny warm pool can't scale spend. The
> warm pool grows by capturing more first-party identity, which is Chapter 8.

And a word on the part this course can't do for you: **creative is now the targeting.**
With broad audiences, the ad itself selects the audience — a video about "deploy
previews for your Next.js app" finds developers because only developers watch it. The
practical implication: you'll likely need more creative variants than feels natural
(winners fatigue in weeks), and the first three seconds are where the qualification
happens.

## 7.6 The problem: your conversion tracking is lying to you

Here's the part that trips up everyone who just installs the Meta Pixel and trusts the
numbers.

The **Meta Pixel** is browser-side JavaScript. It fires from the user's browser and
depends on cookies and the request actually completing. A stack of things now breaks
that:

- **iOS App Tracking Transparency** — most iOS users decline cross-app tracking *(opt-in
  is commonly cited around 20–25%, varies a lot by source and region — directional)*.
- **Safari/Firefox/Brave** cap or expire the cookies the Pixel relies on.
- **Ad blockers** drop the Pixel outright for ~10–30% of users (the same blocking you
  met in Chapter 3).
- **Third-party cookie deprecation** — the date keeps slipping, but the direction is
  certain.

The result: the Pixel under-counts conversions, Meta's algorithm optimises on partial
data, and your reported ROAS is wrong. You can't fix bid strategy on numbers that are
quietly missing a third of reality. This is the same lesson as Chapter 3's ad-blocker
section, now with money attached: browser-only measurement under-counts, and the fix is
to move the important events server-side.

The knock-on effect is worse than bad reporting: **the algorithm optimises on what it
sees.** If the Pixel disproportionately loses conversions from privacy-conscious,
ad-blocking users (it does), Meta learns to find *fewer people like them* — a silent
bias in delivery, not just a counting error. Signal quality is targeting quality.

## 7.7 The fix: Pixel *and* Conversions API

The **Conversions API (CAPI)** sends events **server-side** — from your server (or a
tool/CDP) directly to Meta's endpoint, skipping the browser entirely. Because it never
touches the user's browser, it survives ad blockers, cookie expiry, and failed Pixel
loads.

Meta's own guidance is not "CAPI instead of Pixel" — it's **both, deduplicated.** You
send the same event from the browser (Pixel) and the server (CAPI), and Meta merges
them. Redundancy plus better matching.

### What server-side **does** fix

- Recovers web conversions lost to blockers, ITP, and failed Pixel loads.
- **Higher match quality** — your server has the user's hashed email/phone that the
  browser often doesn't.
- Lets you send **back-office conversions the browser never sees**: trial→paid,
  qualified-lead, refunds, LTV, offline events. This is huge — these are usually your
  *most valuable* conversions, and the Pixel can't see any of them.

### What server-side does **not** fix (worth being precise — this is where a lot of advice overpromises)

- **It is not a consent loophole.** You still need a lawful basis to collect, hash, and
  share user data (§7.9). A SHA-256-hashed email is **pseudonymous, not anonymous** —
  same input, same hash, re-identifiable.
- **It does not restore iOS *app-install* attribution.** That's governed by Apple's
  SKAdNetwork plus Meta's Aggregated Event Measurement — aggregated, delayed, modelled,
  *not* user-level. Web CAPI doesn't bring back per-user iOS attribution.
- **It doesn't fix bad inputs.** CAPI with no email/phone/external_id matches poorly and
  barely helps. Garbage in, garbage matched.
- **It doesn't dedupe by magic** — see below.

> ⚠️ **A dated claim you'll still run into: the AEM "8 prioritised events" limit.**
> Meta removed the 8-event cap and manual configuration around **June 2025** and now
> [auto-aggregates eligible events](https://www.conversios.io/blog/meta-aggregated-event-measurement/).
> Older blogs and courses still teach the cap — if a guide mentions it, that's a good
> sign the rest is stale too.

### Deduplication (the thing people get wrong)

When an event goes out from both Pixel and CAPI, both copies need the **same
`event_id`** (and matching `event_name`) — Meta dedupes on `event_id` and counts it
once. Without it, a hybrid setup **double-counts** conversions, corrupting the very
ROAS it was built to measure. This is the single most common CAPI mistake, and it's
silent until you notice the numbers look too good.

### Event Match Quality (EMQ)

Meta scores each event source 0–10 on how well its data matches real Meta users. Aim
for **6 or higher** (8+ is better). The score goes up with more and better identifiers,
roughly weighted:

- **Strongest:** email, the click ID (`fbc`, from the `fbclid` on the landing URL),
  Facebook Login ID.
- **Medium:** phone, the browser ID (`fbp`), and your stable **`external_id`** (your
  user/contact id).
- **Weaker:** name, city, IP, user agent.

All PII must be normalised (trim, lowercase) and **SHA-256 hashed** before sending. The
way to raise EMQ is to **feed CAPI from where you actually have identity** — your
backend/CRM, which holds the email and phone the browser never sees. Which is exactly
where PostHog and Hogsend come in.

## 7.8 Feeding it from PostHog (and Hogsend)

You don't have to hand-build a CAPI integration. PostHog's **Data Pipelines** include a
**Meta Ads conversions destination**:

- You give it your **Pixel/Dataset ID and access token**. PostHog **SHA-256-hashes**
  the email/name, and sends `event_name`, a unique `event_id` (enabling Pixel dedup),
  `event_time`, plus `fbc`/`fbclid` and user agent.
- Use the **same Pixel ID** for the browser Pixel and the PostHog destination so
  deduplication works.
- Filter the destination to **conversion events only** — you don't want every pageview
  going to Meta.

PostHog also auto-captures `fbclid`/`gclid` and UTMs as person properties and stitches
anonymous → identified when the user gives an email, so a later server-side conversion
carries the identifiers Meta needs for a high EMQ.

**Custom audiences without CSVs:** build a PostHog **cohort** (e.g. "closed-won in the
last 180 days") and sync it as a Meta custom audience — for retargeting, or as a
lookalike seed. No manual list upload, and it stays fresh.

**Where Hogsend fits:** Hogsend's events are server-side and keyed to its own identity
graph (email ↔ Discord ↔ Telegram ↔ anonymous). That means Hogsend can fire **enriched,
deep-funnel conversions** — trial→paid, qualified-lead — **server-side, with strong
identity**, which is exactly the high-EMQ, browser-invisible conversion that makes CAPI
worth it. The honest caveat (same as Chapter 3): this makes your *conversions* accurate
and your *journey triggers* ad-blocker-proof; it does **not** fix the user's client-side
PostHog analytics loss, and it does not override consent or ATT.

### Other ways to send server-side conversions

PostHog's destination is the path this course recommends because you're already in
PostHog, but it's not the only one — the field is worth knowing:

- **Stape / server-side Google Tag Manager** — a popular dedicated server container that
  forwards to Meta, Google, TikTok, etc. More control, more setup.
- **Segment / RudderStack** — if you already run a CDP, it has Meta/Google destinations.
- **Direct CAPI integration** — call Meta's endpoint from your backend yourself. Most
  control, most maintenance; only worth it if the managed options can't express what you
  need.

The principle is the same regardless of tool: **send from where you have identity,
dedupe on `event_id`, send only consented conversion events.**

## 7.9 Consent — the part that isn't optional

Server-side does not mean consent-free, and the rules tightened recently. It's worth
knowing exactly where the lines sit:

- As of **January 2025**, Meta requires **explicit user consent** before you upload
  contact info for custom audiences or send PII via CAPI.
- When you upload a list, **you are the data controller** —
  [Meta's custom-audience terms](https://www.facebook.com/legal/terms/customaudience)
  say so explicitly — and need a lawful basis (usually consent). A German regulator has
  ruled list uploads without consent unlawful, and — again — **hashing does not make
  data anonymous** in law.
- Meta's consent signals / data-processing options exist for exactly this — and in
  consent regimes, events for non-consenting users are best not sent at all.
- **Less is safer:** sending only what you need (hashed email/phone/external_id) keeps
  you out of trouble. As of **September 2025**, Meta blocks custom conversions
  containing sensitive (health/financial) data.

The strategic point this sets up: an **owned, consented identity graph** — emails
captured *with opt-in* at signup and verification — is what makes the whole custom-
audience + CAPI + lookalike machine both *effective* and *compliant*. You can only feed
Meta good, legal data if you collected it well in the first place. That's Chapter 8.

## 7.10 Before you move on

A few things worth doing while this chapter is fresh:

1. **Settle whether paid social is even your channel** (§7.2). For a dev tool or
   community product, the honest answer is usually "mostly no" — in which case Chapter
   8 is where your energy goes, with paid kept to small tests.
2. **Write down your per-channel CAC and payback** for whatever you're doing today
   (§7.3), even roughly. A new channel can only be judged against a baseline, and most
   teams discover they don't have one.
3. If Meta is your channel: the **Meta Pixel** on your site (browser side) is step one…
4. …and the **PostHog → Meta Ads** destination with the **same Pixel ID**, filtered to
   conversion events, is step two — that's the dedup working for you.
5. It's worth checking your conversions carry **email + `fbc` + `external_id`** — that's
   what gets EMQ to 6 or higher, and EMQ is targeting quality.
6. **One campaign** is the right amount to start with: a Sales/Leads objective, broad
   audience, strong creative, optimising on the deepest event you can feed 50/week.
7. And wherever you collect email, **opt-in consent** at that moment is what makes the
   whole audience machine in §7.8 legal to run.

With traffic flowing and honestly measured, the last piece is the one that compounds:
turning that traffic into an audience you never have to pay to reach again.

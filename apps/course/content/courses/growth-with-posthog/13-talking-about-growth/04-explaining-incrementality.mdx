Sooner or later someone — an investor, a skeptical cofounder, a numbers-literate
customer — asks the one question that separates a growth team from a marketing team: *how
do you know it worked?* How do you know the welcome journey *caused* the retention bump,
rather than those users being the kind who'd have stuck around anyway?

You already have the honest answer from Chapter 5: a **holdout.** The skill here is saying
it without a statistics lecture, because the moment you reach for "p-values" and
"confidence intervals" you've lost the room. The version that lands is plain and concrete:

> "Of everyone who qualified for the welcome journey, we held back a random slice — say
> 10% — and sent them nothing. Everyone else got the emails. A month later the messaged
> group had upgraded at 18%, the held-back group at 11%. That 7-point gap is what the
> journey added, because the two groups were identical except for the emails." (Numbers
> illustrative.)

**What to take from it:**

- Same idea as this atom's holdout, in a paid-media frame: a *geo holdout* — turn media off
  in a few matched regions, leave it on everywhere else, and the revenue gap is the
  channel's true contribution. It sits at the top of the measurement pyramid precisely
  because it's a controlled experiment, not attribution laid over history.
- Why lift beats activity: platform-reported ROAS and *incremental* ROAS routinely diverge.
  In their example a three-state holdout showed Meta was actually *under*-reporting, while
  Google brand search's incremental value was only ~23% of the reported number — the
  platform taking credit for buyers who'd have converted anyway. Same trap as crediting a
  journey with conversions it didn't cause.
- A detail that defuses the skeptic's fear: the "held-back" spend isn't burned — it's
  reallocated to the live regions, so the test costs almost nothing while buying a causal
  read. The control group is a measurement tool, not lost money.

Three moves make that explanation work:

- **Lead with the control group, not the math.** The intuition people already have is the
  drug-trial one: some patients get the placebo. "We kept a group who got nothing, on
  purpose" is a sentence anyone follows. Reforge and Eppo call this *incrementality*; your
  stakeholder doesn't need the word, they need the placebo.
- **Name the gap as the credit.** Not "the journey has an 18% conversion rate" (that's the
  messaged group alone, and it includes people who'd have converted anyway) but "the
  journey *added* 7 points over doing nothing." The incremental number is smaller and far
  more defensible — [Eppo's incrementality primer](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/marketing-incrementality-101)
  is the fuller version for anyone who wants it.
- **Say what would have changed your mind.** "If the held-back group had converted at the
  same rate, we'd have killed the journey." A stakeholder trusts a measurement far more
  once they hear it *could* have come out against you — that's the tell that you weren't
  just building a case.

Why rehearse this conversation at all? Because it's the whole distance between "we sent
3,400 emails" (activity) and "the program added 7 points of activation, measured against a
control" (proof). The first invites more questions; the second ends the debate — which is
exactly what you want a report to do.

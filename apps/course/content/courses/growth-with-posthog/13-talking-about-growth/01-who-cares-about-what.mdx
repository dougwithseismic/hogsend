The first skill in talking about growth is realising you're not talking to one audience.
Four groups will ask "how's growth going," and each means something different by the
question. Answer them all the same way and you'll bore three of them and mislead the
fourth.

- **Investors and the board.** They operate at the highest altitude: a handful of
  *outcome* metrics that predict whether the company is worth more than last quarter —
  growth rate; retention, and the **net revenue retention** (the revenue you keep and
  expand from existing customers, after churn) underneath it; **CAC payback** and
  **LTV:CAC** — how many months of margin it takes to earn back the **CAC**
  (customer-acquisition cost), and how that cost compares to a customer's **LTV**
  (lifetime value); and burn and runway, how fast the cash goes out and how long it lasts.
  Andreessen Horowitz's [16 startup metrics](https://a16z.com/16-startup-metrics/)
  is the canonical list of what this audience reads, and the thing it repeats is *know
  which number you mean* — **GMV** (gross merchandise value, the total worth of goods sold
  through a marketplace) is not revenue, cumulative is not active. A board update is a
  trend and a few ratios with one narrative, not a tour of your dashboard.
- **Cofounders.** They read at exactly your altitude: they want the whole picture — the
  leak you're working on, the bet you're making, the thing that isn't working yet. This is
  the one audience you can be completely unpolished with, and the one where hiding a
  problem does the most damage, because they'll act on the rosy version too.
- **The team.** They care about the numbers they can actually move *this week* — the
  input metrics: **activation rate** (the share of new users who reach first value),
  journey lift, experiment results, the funnel step someone owns. An outcome like **MRR**
  (monthly recurring revenue) is motivating once a quarter and useless as a weekly target,
  because no one can point to the line of work that moves it directly.
- **Customers.** They do not care about your growth numbers at all. They care about the
  outcome *for them*. "10,000 teams use us" reaches a customer only as a trust signal —
  shorthand for "this is safe to rely on." The same number is doing a completely
  different job than it does on a board slide.

The mistake that quietly costs you credibility is **writing one report for all four.**
Send the board your weekly input metrics and they can't find the signal; give the team
the board's tidy outcome summary and they've nothing to act on; show a customer either
and you've bored them. It's a **dashboard graveyard** in a new form — the right data,
pointed at the wrong audience, changing no one's mind.

The fix is a habit: before you pick a single number, decide the audience's *altitude* and
the *one thing they're trying to learn*. Say your board asks how growth is going — the
altitude is high, and the one thing they want to know is whether the company is worth more
than last quarter, so you lead with the trend and a couple of ratios, not this week's
activation experiments.

The same move — choose the audience, then the one thing that lands for them — is what good
*positioning* does for a customer, and April Dunford has the sharpest version of it.

**What to take from it:**

- Good positioning isn't whether *you* get the pitch — it's whether the right buyer lands
  on it and thinks *of course, I need that*; the same test as framing a number for the
  board versus the team.
- Weak positioning shows up as *misalignment*, founder and sales and product each telling
  the story slightly differently — the mirror image of this atom's warning, where one
  story stretched across four audiences serves none of them.
- "Better" means nothing without "better for whom," so you name the value and the audience
  it's for together — the same pairing that makes each of this chapter's reports land.

Everything the course has built points at one artifact: a **single monthly one-pager** —
the board slide, the investor-update section, the thing you'd screenshot into the team
channel. If you can fill it honestly, the growth system is real. If you can't, it tells
you exactly which part isn't real yet.

It has four blocks and one sentence, in this order:

1. **North Star + trend.** The one metric that reflects value delivered (Chapter 2), shown
   as a *line over 3–6 months*, not a single number. The trend is the story; the point is
   trivia.
2. **Activation + retention.** The proof the bucket holds: activation rate for recent
   cohorts, and the retention curve or its key cohort number. This is the half of the page
   that says whether growth is real or churn-and-replace.
3. **Blended CAC + referral contribution.** The acquisition economics: what a customer
   costs blended across channels, and the share of new users arriving through the owned
   audience and referral loop (Chapter 8). Falling blended CAC as the owned audience grows
   is the flywheel showing up in the finances.
4. **One experiment learning.** Exactly one — stated as what you'll do differently, win or
   loss (§13.5). One real learning beats ten bullet points, because it proves the loop
   turned this month.

Plus the sentence most decks omit: **the one thing you're worried about.** For a board
especially, naming the risk you'd want help on builds more trust than a flawless slide —
it's the difference between reporting *to* your board and working *with* it.

Notice what's *not* on it: total registered users, emails sent, impressions, pageviews. A
one-pager that survives the "so what?" test on every line is the entire course expressed
as a single page someone else can read in two minutes — and it's the report the monthly
cadence in Chapter 9 exists to produce.

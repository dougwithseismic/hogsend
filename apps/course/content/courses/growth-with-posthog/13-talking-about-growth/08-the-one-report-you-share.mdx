Everything the course has built points at one artifact: a **single monthly one-pager** —
the board slide, the investor-update section, the thing you'd screenshot into the team
channel. If you can fill it honestly, the growth system is real. If you can't, it tells
you exactly which part isn't real yet.

It has four blocks and one sentence, in this order:

1. **North Star + trend.** The one metric that reflects value delivered — your North Star —
   shown as a *line over 3–6 months*, not a single number. The trend is the story; the
   point is trivia.
2. **Activation + retention.** The proof the bucket holds: activation rate for recent
   cohorts, and the retention curve or its key cohort number. This is the half of the page
   that says whether growth is real or churn-and-replace.
3. **Blended CAC + referral contribution.** The acquisition economics: **blended CAC** —
   what a customer costs on average across every channel — and the share of new users
   arriving through the owned audience and referral loop. Falling blended CAC as the owned
   audience grows is the flywheel showing up in the finances.
4. **One experiment learning.** Exactly one — stated as what you'll do differently, win or
   loss. One real learning beats ten bullet points, because it proves the loop turned this
   month.

Plus the sentence most decks omit: **the one thing you're worried about.** For a board
especially, naming the risk you'd want help on builds more trust than a flawless slide —
it's the difference between reporting *to* your board and working *with* it.

Notice what's *not* on it: total registered users, emails sent, impressions, pageviews. A
one-pager that survives the "so what?" test on every line is the entire course expressed
as a single page — one someone else can read in two minutes. And it's the report your
monthly cadence exists to produce — the one page the whole loop has been building toward.

**What to take from it:**

- Lead with the trend, not the snapshot — *investors invest in lines, not dots* (Mark
  Suster), so you send updates early and repeatedly and show at least a year of charts,
  exactly this atom's rule that the North Star is a line over 3–6 months, not a point.
- Put every metric next to its month-over-month change and name the lowlights — the
  template insists the lows go in as bullets ("tested a new social strategy, spent $2,500,
  results flat, turned it off"), which is this atom's one learning, win or loss, and its
  one-sentence worry.
- The metrics that carry the update are the economic levers — **CAC** (customer-acquisition
  cost, driven *down*, e.g. by a referral program), **LTV** (lifetime value, driven *up*,
  e.g. by annual billing or packaging), and churn — and the job is widening the gap between
  CAC and LTV, the blended-CAC-and-referral block of the one-pager here.

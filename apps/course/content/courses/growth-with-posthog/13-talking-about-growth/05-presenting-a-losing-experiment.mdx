Most experiments lose. Ronny Kohavi, who ran experimentation at Microsoft, reports that
only about **a third** of well-designed experiments there improved the metric they were
built to move; many of the rest did nothing, and some made it worse. (Well-attested, from
Kohavi's work at Bing and Microsoft.) At other top experimentation teams the win rate is
often quoted lower still. (Directional.) If most tests fail *at the companies best at
testing*, a losing result on your team is not a failure of the team — it's the normal
output of a working experiment loop.

That reframe is the whole conversation: a loss is a **learning you already paid for,** and
the payment is non-refundable. The only way to waste it is to hide it. So the way you
present a loss matters more than the loss:

- **State the hypothesis first, then the result.** "We believed a second onboarding email
  would lift activation; it didn't move — flat within noise." The hypothesis makes the
  loss legible: you tested a belief, and now you know something. A result with no stated
  hypothesis just looks like something broke.
- **Say what it ruled out.** A flat result narrows the search: "so the activation leak
  isn't email volume — it's earlier, in the first-run experience." That sentence is the
  asset the experiment bought. Chapter 9's rule — *write the losers down too* — exists
  because a learning that lives in one head gets re-paid-for six months later.
- **Show your tempo, not just this test.** One loss in isolation looks like a miss; the
  same loss as "test 7 of the quarter, here's what the last six taught us" looks like a
  machine working. Sean Ellis's case for high-tempo testing is exactly this: velocity
  compounds *because* individual tests fail, not despite it.

What you must never do: bury the loss, quietly stop reporting a journey that isn't
working, or go hunting through the data for the one sub-segment where it "won" so you have
a positive to show. That last one — p-hacking a win out of a loss — is the fastest way to
lose a numbers-literate stakeholder for good, because they *can* smell it, and once
they've smelled it once, every future win you present gets discounted too.

The counter-intuitive payoff: a leader who stands up and presents their own flat result —
what Andrew Capland calls dogfooding failure — buys more credibility than a clean win
would. It's proof the numbers aren't being managed for the audience, and that proof is the
only thing that makes the wins believable.

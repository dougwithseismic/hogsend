Most experiments lose. Ronny Kohavi, who ran experimentation at Microsoft and Bing,
reports that only about **a third** of well-designed experiments there improved the metric
they were built to move; many of the rest did nothing, and some made it worse. At other
top experimentation teams the quoted win rate runs lower still. If most tests fail *at the
companies best at testing*, a losing result on your team is not a failure of the team —
it's the normal output of a working experiment loop.

That reframe is the whole conversation: a loss is a **learning you already paid for.**
You've spent the time and the traffic either way — the only way to waste that spend is to
hide what it taught you. So how you present a loss matters as much as the result itself:

- **State the hypothesis first, then the result.** "We believed a second onboarding email
  would lift activation; it didn't move — flat within noise." The hypothesis makes the
  loss legible: you tested a belief, and now you know something. A result with no stated
  hypothesis just looks like something broke.
- **Say what it ruled out.** A flat result narrows the search: "so the activation leak
  isn't email volume — it's earlier, in the first-run experience." That sentence is the
  asset the experiment bought. The rule *write the losers down too* exists because a
  learning that lives in one head gets re-paid-for six months later.
- **Show your tempo, not just this test.** One loss in isolation looks like a miss; the
  same loss as "test 7 of the quarter, here's what the last six taught us" looks like a
  machine working. Sean Ellis's case for high-tempo testing is exactly this: velocity
  compounds *because* individual tests fail, not despite it.

**What to take from it:**

- Capland makes failure safe *structurally*, not with vibes: the team's values go into an
  operating manual, and the first one is an explicit "we experiment to find out what works
  and what doesn't" — so a loss is the process working, not a person failing.
- You make it real by launching it like a feature — to execs, then the team, then the
  company — and reinforcing it weekly, in planning meetings and 1:1s that ask "what did you
  get to experiment on this week?" until reporting a loss becomes a routine answer.
- The accelerant is the leader dogfooding failure: standing up in front of the company with
  an experiment where their *own* hypothesis was dead wrong — the move that gives everyone
  else permission to present a flat result.

What you must never do: bury the loss, quietly stop reporting a journey that isn't
working, or go hunting through the data for the one sub-segment where it "won" so you have
a positive to show. That last one — p-hacking a win out of a loss — is the fastest way to
lose a numbers-literate stakeholder for good. They *can* smell it, and once they have,
every future win you present gets discounted too.

The counter-intuitive payoff sits one level above any single result: a leader who presents
their own flat test buys more credibility than a clean win would. It's proof the numbers
aren't being managed for the audience — and that proof is the only thing that makes the
wins believable.

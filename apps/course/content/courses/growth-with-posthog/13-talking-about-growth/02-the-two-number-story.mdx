A single number is easy to argue with, because on its own it can't show cause.
"Activation is 44%" invites *so what?* — up or down from what, and did anything you did
move it? The unit that actually persuades is a **pair**: a *leading* metric you control,
and the *lagging* outcome it's supposed to move.

- A **leading** metric is an input — something a person on the team can act on this week.
  Onboarding completion, welcome-journey lift, invites sent per new user. It moves
  quickly, and it's yours to change.
- A **lagging** metric is an outcome — retention, revenue, the North Star. It moves
  slowly, it's what the business is actually made of, and it isn't directly movable: you
  reach it *through* the inputs. (This is the North Star property from Chapter 2 — it
  reflects value and you work on 3–5 input metrics beneath it — pointed at how you *talk*
  about it.)

The story lives in the gap between them. "We shipped the onboarding checklist;
completion went from 51% to 68% (leading, controllable), and week-4 retention on that
cohort is +3 points (lagging, the outcome we care about)." (Numbers illustrative.) That
sentence persuades because it names the thing you did, the input it moved, and the
outcome that followed — a mechanism, not a coincidence. One number can't carry a
mechanism; two, with the causal link stated between them, can.

It also protects you both ways. A leading metric that moved with no lagging follow-through
yet is an honest "not proven yet" — you did the work, the outcome hasn't landed, keep
watching. A lagging metric that moved with no leading metric behind it is a *warning*: you
can't explain why, so you can't repeat it, and you shouldn't take credit. a16z's rule for
the board version is [know exactly which metric you mean](https://a16z.com/16-startup-metrics/).
The same discipline sits underneath the two-number story: the pair is only honest if each
half is defined precisely enough to check.

The habit worth dropping is presenting the outcome alone as proof of your work.
"Revenue is up" is true and unattributable. "We did X, the input moved, revenue followed"
is a claim you can stand behind — or be proven wrong on, which is exactly the point of
making it.

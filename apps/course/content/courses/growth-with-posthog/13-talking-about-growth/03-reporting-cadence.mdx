The audiences from the last piece map to two standing reports, on two rhythms. Keeping
them separate is what stops the weekly meeting from ballooning and the board update from
drowning in detail.

**The weekly team review (operational).** Thirty minutes, a fixed agenda, input metrics
and live work — the growth ritual from Chapter 9. Its job is *decisions and learnings
this week*, not status. Brian Balfour's [weekly growth meeting](https://brianbalfour.com/essays/growth-meeting),
run at HubSpot and Reforge, is the template worth compressing: anomalies get an owner
rather than a live group debugging session, because group debugging is how a 30-minute
meeting becomes a two-hour one.

<Checklist
  id="ch13-weekly-review"
  title="Weekly team review — skeleton"
  items={[
    "Metrics read (10 min): North Star vs last week, the retention curve, the one funnel step you're working — is growth real or churn-and-replace?",
    "Journeys and spend (10 min): each live journey's volume and holdout lift; cost per conversion — kill-or-scale decisions made out loud",
    "Experiments (10 min): what finished (result + one-sentence learning), what launches next from the top of the backlog, what's blocked",
    "Every anomaly gets an owner and a next step — not a live debugging session",
    "One learning from the week, written down somewhere permanent",
  ]}
/>

**The monthly or board update (outcomes).** An hour to write, once a month (quarterly for
the board) — the zoom-out from Chapter 9. It carries outcomes and trend, not this week's
inputs: the North Star trend, retention, incremental lift from the lifecycle program (the
global holdout), blended CAC and the referral loop's contribution, and exactly one
learning worth the reader's attention. The rule of thumb: a board update is a **trend and
a handful of ratios with one narrative**, never a data dump. (Rule of thumb.)

<Checklist
  id="ch13-monthly-update"
  title="Monthly / board update — skeleton"
  items={[
    "North Star metric: this month's number, and the trend over the last 3–6 months (a line, not a point)",
    "Retention: the curve or the key cohort number — the proof the bucket holds",
    "Lifecycle lift: incremental lift from the global holdout — the number that justifies the program",
    "Acquisition economics: blended CAC, and the share of growth from the owned audience / referral loop",
    "One learning: a single experiment or insight, stated as what you'll do differently — not a list",
    "The one thing you're worried about: for a board, naming a real risk builds more trust than a clean slide",
  ]}
/>

The two reports share almost no rows, on purpose. If your board update reads like your
weekly review, you've handed the highest-altitude audience the lowest-altitude data — and
asked them to find the signal you were supposed to find for them. The next pieces are how
to make the few numbers that survive to the board update honest ones.

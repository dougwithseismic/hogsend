---
title: "Growth theatre and over-claiming"
description: "The other failure mode: performing growth instead of doing it, and claiming more credit than the data supports. Why last-click hero stories cost you more than they buy."
workbook: "Take one attribution claim — 'X drove Y signups' — and rewrite it at the level of honesty the data actually supports. Saves to your workbook."
---

Vanity metrics mislead by *choosing* the flattering number. A close relative misleads the
other way — by *overstating* what a real number proves. It shows up in two forms.

**Growth theatre.** Activity performed because it *looks* like growth work — the referral
program launched for the announcement, the "growth hack" copied from a thread, the
dashboard built to be shown rather than read. It's the **tactic collector** in a suit —
someone forever chasing the next growth hack with no stated leak to plug: constant motion,
nothing measured against a control. The tell is the same one — ask *which leak does this
plug?* and there's no answer, only *it's what growth teams do.*

**Over-claiming attribution.** The more damaging form, because it looks rigorous. "Our
Product Hunt launch drove 10,000 signups." "The tweet that went viral got us
500 customers." These are **last-click hero stories** — they credit the final touch,
ignore everything that primed it, and almost always present a *correlational* number as if
it were *causal*. The tool that exposes this is the holdout: what a channel *reports* and
what it actually *added* over doing nothing are different numbers, usually by a lot. A
channel can post a **ROAS** (return on ad spend — revenue divided by what you spent to earn
it) of 2.31 while its *incremental* ROAS, the return it truly added, is 2.78 — or half
that. Sometimes reality is higher, often it's lower; it is never automatically the reported
figure.

Why under-claiming is the better long-run strategy:

- **Over-claiming has a compounding cost.** The first time a stakeholder catches a hero
  story that doesn't survive a holdout, *every* future number you present gets a mental
  discount. Credibility is the currency a report runs on, and it's priced by your least
  defensible claim, not your best one.
- **Attribution honesty is a differentiator.** "We think the launch helped, but we can't
  cleanly separate it from the press that week — here's what we *can* measure" sounds
  weaker and lands stronger. It signals you know the difference between correlation and
  cause, which is precisely what makes your causal claims — the holdout ones — believable.
- **Hold the hero stories to the holdout bar.** Not every number needs an experiment, but
  any number you're using to *justify spending more* does. If you're about to triple a
  budget on the strength of "it drove 10k signups," that is exactly the claim to run
  incrementality on first.

The through-line with vanity metrics: both are ways of talking about growth that feel good
in the room and cost you later. The antidote is the same one — say the smaller, defensible
number, and say plainly what you can't yet prove.

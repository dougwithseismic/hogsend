The most common way to talk about growth *badly* is the **vanity metric** — a number
that reliably goes up, feels like progress, and tells you nothing you can act on. Eric
Ries named the category in *The Lean Startup*, splitting metrics into *vanity* and
*actionable*, and the test he proposed is the one to keep: when this number moves, does
it tell me what to do differently? If not, it's vanity.

The usual suspects share a shape:

- **Cumulative totals.** Total registered users, signups ever, **GMV** (gross merchandise
  value) to date. A running total can only go up — it literally cannot report a bad month —
  so it can't inform a single decision. a16z's [16 startup metrics](https://a16z.com/16-startup-metrics/) makes
  the point bluntly: show *active* users, not cumulative registered ones; the gap between
  the two is where the truth lives.
- **Raw reach.** Impressions, pageviews, follower counts. Big numbers that impress in a
  deck and disconnect entirely from value delivered — [Amplitude's definition](https://amplitude.com/blog/vanity-metrics)
  is that a vanity metric is one that can rise while the business goes nowhere.
- **Registered, not active.** "50,000 users" that's really 50,000 signups and 800
  weekly-actives. The number is true; the impression it creates is false.

Two tests catch all of them:

- **The "so what?" test.** After any number, ask: *what would I do differently if this were
  half, or double?* If there's no answer, it's context at best and theatre at worst.
- **Ratio and cohort over cumulative count.** A vanity metric becomes actionable the
  moment you make it a *rate* (activation %, week-4 retention, paid-conversion %) or split
  it by *cohort* (how does the group that joined in May retain, vs April?). Rates can go
  *down*, which is exactly what makes them useful; cohorts show whether the product is
  getting better or you're just adding more people to a leaky bucket.

The trap is that vanity metrics aren't lies — "10,000 signups" is true. They're *true
numbers that point away from the decision,* which is why they survive: they're defensible,
they flatter, and everyone nods along. The discipline is to lead with the ratio and keep
the big cumulative number where it belongs — as a trust signal for customers, never as
evidence for the team. It's the same honesty you'd apply to reading your own dashboard,
now turned on what you show other people.

> **In this chapter:** who cares about which number and at what altitude, why a single
> number never persuades and the two-number story that does, the two reporting cadences
> (weekly team review vs monthly board update), how to explain a holdout to a skeptic
> without a stats lecture, how to present a losing experiment as the learning you already
> paid for, how to spot the vanity metrics and growth-theatre that mislead, and how to
> assemble the one monthly report the whole course has been building toward. This is the
> human skill the loop depends on — the stage that decides whether anyone acts on what
> you found.

**How this chapter works.** It's broken into nine short pieces — most are a one- to
three-minute read, each with a prompt or a checklist that turns the idea into something
in your workbook. Work through them in order, or jump to what you need:

1. [Who cares about what](./01-who-cares-about-what.mdx) — four audiences, four altitudes, and the one-report-for-all mistake.
2. [The two-number story](./02-the-two-number-story.mdx) — pair a leading metric with its lagging outcome; one number never persuades.
3. [Two cadences, two reports](./03-reporting-cadence.mdx) — the weekly team review vs the monthly board update, with a skeleton for each.
4. [How do you know it worked?](./04-explaining-incrementality.mdx) — explaining a holdout to a skeptic, without a stats lecture.
5. [Presenting a loss](./05-presenting-a-losing-experiment.mdx) — a losing experiment is a learning you already paid for.
6. [Vanity metrics](./06-vanity-metrics.mdx) — the numbers that impress and mislead, and the "so what?" test that catches them.
7. [Growth theatre and over-claiming](./07-growth-theatre-and-over-claiming.mdx) — performing growth, last-click hero stories, and the credibility cost.
8. [The one report you share](./08-the-one-report-you-share.mdx) — assemble the single monthly one-pager the whole course points at.
9. [Before you move on](./09-before-you-move-on.mdx) — recap, then a scenario quiz on the whole chapter.

## 2.1 AARRR: the five metrics that matter

In 2007 Dave McClure (then at PayPal, later founder of 500 Startups) gave a five-minute
talk called *Startup Metrics for Pirates*. His argument: a startup drowns in metrics,
but only five categories actually matter. They spell **AARRR** — which sounds like a
pirate, hence "pirate metrics."

Eighteen years later it's still the cleanest map of a business there is, and it's the
thing that turns "growth" from a vague ambition into five owned, measurable numbers — the
shared language a growth team (Chapter 0) actually runs on. The five stages, each as a
question, what you'd measure, and a concrete example:

### Acquisition — *how do people find you?*
The top of the funnel: people arriving and signing up.
**Measure:** visitors by channel, signups, signup conversion rate, and **CAC** (cost
to acquire a customer) by channel.
**Example:** 4,000 visitors this month — 2,200 from organic search, 1,000 from a
launch post, 800 from ads — converting to 260 signups (6.5%). Already you can see which
channel is cheap and which is vanity.

### Activation — *do new users reach the value?*
The first successful experience — the moment the product clicks. This is *not* the
same as acquisition: acquisition is that they showed up; activation is that they had a
good first visit. McClure's original words were "turn the drive-by visitors into users
who are somehow enrolled."
**Measure:** activation rate (% who complete the key first action), time-to-value,
onboarding completion.
**Example:** of those 260 signups, 95 create their first project in week one (37%). That
37% is the single most important number on this page, and §2.3 is about why.

### Retention — *do they come back?*
Activated users returning and keeping the habit. This is the engine of compounding
growth, and the rest of the framework exists to feed it.
**Measure:** N-day retention (D1/D7/D30), the DAU/MAU ratio (stickiness), churn rate,
and where the retention curve flattens.
**Example:** of the 95 activated users, 60 are still active at day 30 — and crucially the
curve *flattens* there rather than sliding to zero, which means you have a real product
(§2.2).

### Referral — *do they bring others?*
Users liking the product enough to invite people.
**Measure:** invites sent per user, invite acceptance rate, the viral coefficient
(**K-factor**), and what fraction of new users came from referral.
**Example:** 60 retained users send 48 invites, 18 of which convert — a K-factor of 0.3.
Below 1 it's a discount on CAC, not viral growth; above 1 it compounds on its own.

### Revenue — *do they pay, and how much?*
The business capturing value.
**Measure:** MRR/ARR, conversion to paid, **LTV** (lifetime value), and the
**LTV:CAC ratio** — the single number that tells you if growth is profitable.
**Example:** 22 of the retained users upgrade at $29/mo; with a CAC of $40 and an LTV of
$220, the 3:1+ LTV:CAC says growth here is worth scaling — *once* the bucket holds.

> A small note that confuses people: some sources list the last two as Revenue then
> Referral. McClure's own slides put **Referral before Revenue**, but it genuinely
> doesn't matter — the acronym is identical (R-R) either way. Don't spend energy on it.

AARRR is best read as **"the five categories of number to watch,"** not as a strict
chronological pipeline every user marches through. A user can refer a friend before they
pay; a user can pay before they're really retained. That distinction is what the next
section is about.

## 2.2 The leaky bucket: why order matters

Here is the analogy this course returns to over and over.

**Your business is a bucket.** Water poured in at the top is newly acquired customers.
Water leaking out of holes in the bottom is customers who churn. The water level is
your active customer base.

There are only two ways to raise the level: **pour faster** (acquire more) or **plug
the holes** (retain more).

Almost everyone reaches for the tap first. It feels like progress — the signup number
goes up, the chart is up and to the right, the launch looks like a success. But if the
bucket is full of holes, pouring faster mostly reveals the problem: you spend more, the
level barely moves, and you've simply *bought churn*. As one framing puts it, "if the
product cannot retain, paid acquisition becomes a way of purchasing churn."

The mobile world makes this brutal. Data popularised by Andrew Chen found the average
app **loses about 77% of its daily users within three days, and about 90% within 30
days.** Run the math on that: acquire 100 users at \$5 each, lose 77 of them in three
days, and you've effectively set \$385 on fire — immediately, before you've done
anything wrong except scale a leaky bucket. *(Directional: the 77%/90% figures come
from one widely-cited dataset; treat them as illustrative of the shape, not gospel.)*

**This is why the course fixes things in this order: measure → keep → grow.** Plug the
biggest holes (activation and retention) *first*, because retention is the multiplier
on every acquisition dollar. A user who stays is worth more, refers more, and pays
back their acquisition cost. Only once the bucket holds water do you open the tap. This
is the same "retention before acquisition" line you got the team to nod to in Chapter 0 —
§2.2 is the argument behind the nod.

### RARRA: the same point, sharpened

Around 2017, Thomas Petit and Gabor Papp reordered the letters to **RARRA** —
Retention, Activation, Referral, Revenue, Acquisition — specifically to break the
habit of starting with acquisition. Their blunt version: *"If you don't have
retention, you have nothing."* AARRR is the map of a user's journey; RARRA is the order
of *priorities* — what to fix first for the most leverage.

### The honest counterpoint

So you don't over-rotate: the leaky-bucket idea has a credible critique. The
Ehrenberg-Bass Institute argues it's over-applied — for many categories, acquiring new
and light buyers drives growth *more* than plugging churn, and obsessing only over
retention can cap your ceiling. The balanced reading: **retention-first is the right
default for an early-stage product**, but "retention is the only thing that matters"
is an oversimplification. Fix the bucket, then absolutely turn on the tap (Chapters
7–8). The skill is knowing which phase you're in — and the retention curve (§2.5) is how
you tell.

## 2.3 Activation and the "aha moment"

Activation is the highest-leverage stage for most early products, so it's worth going
deep. The **aha moment** is the point a new user first feels the product's value. The
**activation metric** is a measurable stand-in for it: a specific action, or a count of
actions in a time window, that *predicts* whether someone sticks around.

The famous examples:

| Company | The "magic number" |
|---|---|
| **Facebook** | 7 friends in 10 days |
| **Twitter** | follow ~30 accounts |
| **Slack** | a team sends 2,000 messages |
| **Dropbox** | put one file in one folder |

These are real and they're useful — but they are widely misunderstood, so read the
next part carefully.

### The magic number is a rally cry, not a law

It is tempting to copy "7 friends" and chase it. Don't. As Mixpanel put it, *"magic
numbers are an illusion — a very useful illusion, but an illusion nevertheless."*
Andrew Chen, talking about Facebook's number, said it *"could have been '10 friends in
12 days' or even 'five friends in one day'"* — it was a **round number picked from the
middle of a range**. Facebook weren't trying to make it precise.

Three things to understand about these numbers:

- **They describe an average, not an individual.** One user is hooked after 3 friends;
  another churns after 12. The number is a population-level signal.
- **Their real job is organisational.** A magic number is a mantra that gets every team
  pointed at the same goal. That alignment is most of the value — and it's exactly the
  "shared language" a growth team needs (Chapter 0).
- **Some are shakier than others.** Slack's 2,000 is a *team*-level number, not
  per-user. Dropbox's "one file in one folder" is repeated everywhere but hard to
  trace to a primary source — Dropbox's *solid, documented* story is its referral
  program (Chapter 8), not its activation number.

### How to find *your* activation metric (the method that matters)

Copying someone else's number is the mistake. Finding your own is a three-step method
(from Lenny Rachitsky):

1. **Brainstorm candidate aha moments** from your product-usage data — actions that
   plausibly mark "they got it."
2. **Run a correlation/regression analysis** to see whether retention jumps for users
   who hit each candidate. This surfaces patterns.
3. **Run an experiment** — deliberately push more new users to hit the candidate
   moment, and see if retention actually rises.

Step 3 is the one teams skip, and it's the one that counts. The crucial caveat: *"a
good activation metric is causal for your retention, not just correlative."* Steps 1–2
only show correlation — maybe engaged users do X *because* they were already going to
stick. Only an experiment proves that *causing* more X *causes* more retention. Without
it, you can spend months optimising a number users hit on their way out the door.

### Worked example: finding it for a real-ish product

Say you run a design-collaboration tool. You walk the method:

1. **Candidates** from your data: *invited a teammate*, *created 3+ files*, *left a
   comment*, *connected Figma*. All plausible "aha"s.
2. **Correlation:** in PostHog, build a retention insight split by each candidate. Users
   who *invited a teammate in week one* retain at 64% to day 30; everyone else at 19%.
   That's a huge gap — but it might just be that engaged teams invite *and* retain.
3. **Experiment:** you put a "+ invite a teammate" prompt in onboarding for half of new
   signups (a feature flag). If the prompted half retains meaningfully better, you've
   shown *causing* the invite *causes* retention. Now "invite a teammate in week one" is
   your activation metric, and onboarding's whole job is to drive it.

That's the work, and it's the same correlation-vs-causation discipline you'll use again
for lifecycle holdouts in Chapter 5. Twitter's team is the model: they found "follow 30
accounts" correlated with retention — then dug further and found a *better*, more causal
signal: what fraction of those 30 **followed back**. They refined a correlation into
something that actually moves the outcome.

The shape of an activation metric is almost always: **[quantity] of [key action]
within [time window]**, for new users. Your job in the next chapter is to make sure
the "[key action]" is an event you actually capture.

### What "activation" means for your kind of startup

The metric is yours to find, but the *shape* is predictable by business type:

- **Self-serve SaaS** — the first time the core job gets done (project created, report
  shipped, integration connected).
- **Marketplace** — the first *successful match or transaction*; for a two-sided
  marketplace you have two activation metrics (supply and demand), and liquidity is the
  real game.
- **Consumer / social app** — a habit signal: sessions-per-week, or the network action
  (a follow, a post) that predicts the user comes back.
- **Dev tool** — the first successful API call / first green build / first deploy. The
  "aha" is "it worked in my environment."
- **E-commerce** — first purchase, then second purchase (the repeat is the real
  activation; one-time buyers are barely retained).
- **Community / web3** — the link-and-verify-for-a-perk action (Chapter 8) is both the
  activation *and* the start of the identity graph.

## 2.4 The North Star Metric

AARRR gives you five categories. A **North Star Metric (NSM)** gives you the *one*
number on top — the single measure that best captures the value your product delivers,
that the whole team can rally around. It's the metric a growth team puts at the top of
the dashboard (Chapter 4) and reads first.

A good North Star (criteria from Amplitude, the framework's main steward):

- **Reflects customer value**, not company vanity. It should rise when users get more
  value, not just when more users exist.
- **Expresses your strategy** — someone should be able to read it and roughly
  understand what your product is for.
- **Is a leading indicator** — an early signal of impact, not a lagging financial
  result.
- **Is not directly movable.** If you can move it with one button, it's not a North
  Star. You move it *through* inputs.

Bad North Stars are vanity or lagging metrics: registered users, downloads, page
views, and — importantly — **revenue itself** (ARR/MRR). Revenue is the score at the
end of the quarter; by the time it moves, the game is already played. The North Star
is the leading thing that *causes* the revenue.

Examples that capture value:

| Product | North Star |
|---|---|
| Airbnb | nights booked |
| Spotify | time spent listening |
| WhatsApp | messages sent |
| PostHog (their own) | "Discoveries" — a user viewing an insight/replay for 10+ seconds |

PostHog's own choice is instructive: they picked "Discoveries" because it predicted
retention 2.3× better than the alternatives. They chose an *activation-quality* signal
over a vanity count — exactly the lesson from §2.3.

**How you operate it:** the NSM sits too high for any one team to move directly, so you
break it into **3–5 input metrics** that teams *can* move — and those inputs usually
map straight onto AARRR stages (acquisition volume, activation rate, frequency,
referral rate). When the inputs work, the North Star rises, and that's your early
warning *before* revenue reacts. One headline number on top, a handful of input
metrics beneath it, each owned by someone. That ownership — "you own activation rate,
you own weekly frequency" — is how a small team distributes the growth work without a
growth department.

## 2.5 Other frameworks, and when to reach for them

AARRR is the default, not the only lens. A growth team should know what else exists so it
picks the right tool for the question:

- **Growth loops (Reforge).** The sharpest critique of AARRR: a funnel is *linear* —
  people enter at the top and fall out the bottom, and you're always refilling the top. A
  **loop** is a system where the output of one cycle becomes the input of the next: a new
  user creates content → that content gets indexed/shared → it brings the next user.
  Loops compound; funnels leak. The funnel is the right lens for *seeing* leaks; the loop
  is the right lens for *designing* growth that doesn't need constant refilling. The
  owned-audience flywheel in Chapter 8 is a growth loop. **Use loops when** you're
  designing how growth sustains itself, not just measuring conversion.
- **The Sean Ellis PMF survey.** Before you optimise activation, it's worth knowing if
  you have product-market fit at all. Ellis's test: ask users "how would you feel if you
  could no longer use this product?" If **40%+ say "very disappointed,"** you likely have
  PMF and should pour into growth; below it, fix the product first. A cheap, fast gut-check
  that complements the retention curve.
- **HEART (Google).** Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task-success — a UX-
  quality framework, more granular than AARRR for *product* health. **Use it when** the
  question is "is this feature good," not "is the business growing."
- **The Pirate-metrics critique itself.** Some teams find AARRR too acquisition-shaped
  and prefer to lead with retention (RARRA, §2.2) or with loops. There's no prize for
  orthodoxy — the frameworks are lenses, and the honest move is to use AARRR to find the
  leak, loops to design the fix, and the PMF survey to know which phase you're even in.

The point is not to collect frameworks. It's that a growth team reaches for the lens that
answers the question in front of it, instead of forcing every question through one.

## 2.6 From framework to instrument

Here's how the theory in this chapter becomes the charts in Chapter 4:

| AARRR stage | What you build in PostHog |
|---|---|
| Acquisition | A **funnel** from first-touch → signup, broken down by channel/UTM; CAC per channel |
| Activation | The **activation funnel** (signup → aha threshold); regression to find the aha; experiments to prove it |
| Retention | The **retention curve** (does it flatten above zero?) and **cohort** tables |
| Referral | A funnel of invites sent → accepted → activated; the K-factor |
| Revenue | Free→paid conversion funnel; LTV:CAC by cohort |
| North Star | One headline number pinned to a dashboard, with its input metrics beneath |

The single most important chart in that list is the **retention curve**. If it decays
to zero, you have a leaky bucket and no amount of acquisition will save you. If it
flattens above zero — some cohort of users sticks forever — you have something real to
pour into. It's also how you answer the only strategic question that matters right now:
*am I in the "fix the bucket" phase or the "open the tap" phase?*

You now know what to measure and why. Next, you'll wire PostHog up to measure it.

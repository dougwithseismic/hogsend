## 8.1 Owned beats rented

Every ad audience is **rented.** You pay to reach those people the first time, and you
pay again every time you want to reach them after that. The moment you stop paying, the
channel goes dark. Social followings are rented too, just with a subtler landlord: the
platform's algorithm decides what fraction of "your" followers see any given post, and
that fraction has fallen for a decade across every major feed — reach you built in 2020
is not reach you have now (Chapter 7's
[law of shitty clickthroughs](https://andrewchen.com/the-law-of-shitty-clickthroughs/),
applied to organic).

An **owned** channel — a verified email address, a linked Discord or Telegram account —
is the opposite. Once someone is in it:

- **There's no algorithm tax.** A Discord post or Telegram message reaches your members
  directly; an email lands in the inbox. No boost, no bid, no feed gatekeeper deciding
  who sees it.
- **Reactivation is nearly free.** Bringing a lapsed user back through an owned channel
  costs a fraction of re-buying the ad impression.
- **It's portable.** An email list exports. A Discord guild's members are linked to
  *your* identity graph, not just to Discord. Platform policy changes hurt; they don't
  zero you.

The exact multiples you'll see quoted — "acquiring costs 5–25× more than retaining,"
"owned reactivation is 5–10× cheaper than paid" — are **directional folklore** with
loose sourcing, better treated as direction than decimals. But the **mechanism is
undeniable** and it's the load-bearing argument of this chapter: *a one-time ad spend
that captures someone into an owned channel converts a rented impression into a
reusable asset.* You pay once to acquire the contact, then reach them as often as you
like for free.

This reframes what an ad is *for*. A click that bounces is money spent and gone. A click
that ends in a verified email or a linked Discord is money spent on an asset you keep —
the same dollar, but one version evaporates and the other compounds. This is why a
lifecycle tool is the natural place for acquisition to *land*: ads bring people,
lifecycle keeps them, and the bridge between the two is the moment you capture their
identity into a channel you own.

The scale you need is smaller than you think. Kevin Kelly's
[1,000 True Fans](https://kk.org/thetechnium/1000-true-fans/) is the foundational
version of the argument: a creator (or a niche product) doesn't need millions of
renters — it needs a thousand people it can *reach directly* who care enough to buy.
An owned audience of a few thousand engaged, reachable people is a real business
foundation; a hundred thousand rented impressions is a receipt.

## 8.2 The capture mechanic: a perk for linking

People don't hand over their email or link their Discord out of goodwill. They do it for
a **perk.** The pattern, across every community that does this well:

> **"Link your account / verify your email → get [a role, a perk, access, credits, a
> whitelist spot]."**

The perk is the conversion event, and the conversion *is* the identity capture. Examples
worth modelling:

- **Web3 / token-gating:** Collab.Land and Guild.xyz let a member connect a wallet, the
  bot verifies an on-chain holding, and a role unlocks gated channels or whitelist
  spots. Guild's primitive is literally **Requirements → Roles → Rewards**.
- **Quest platforms** (Zealy, Galxe, Layer3): daily actions earn XP, roles, and airdrop
  eligibility — a structured engagement loop.
- **Email-verify-for-role** (the non-web3 analog, and Hogsend's pattern): the bot links
  to a verification page; the user confirms their **email**; they get a "Verified" role
  and its perks. This is the move that produces an **email ↔ Discord** link you own —
  and several tools market it openly as list-building.

The reward works best when it maps to your product's value and lives *in the owned
channel*, so claiming it deepens the relationship rather than ending it. A perk the
user collects and walks away from buys you one email; a perk they collect *inside*
your community pulls them one step further in.

Two design considerations keep the mechanic honest:

- **The perk must be worth the link, and no more.** Over-reward and you harvest
  perk-hunters who churn the moment the reward lands (§8.7 returns to this with
  referral fraud). The best perks are things a genuine user wanted anyway: access to
  the community channels, credits in the product, early features.
- **Ask at the moment of motivation.** The link prompt converts best right after the
  user got value — the same peak-engagement timing as the referral ask (Chapter 6,
  §6.8). A cold DM asking to "verify your email for a role" is spam; the same ask as
  the doorway into a community they just decided to join is onboarding.

## 8.3 Linking Discord, with email verification

Here's the mechanic in Hogsend terms. The Discord cold-connect flow lets a member run
`/link you@example.com`; the engine emails a single-use confirmation link; clicking it
**proves inbox ownership**, binds `discord ↔ email`, and emits a `discord.linked` event
you can build on.

The important design principle — and the one that makes the captured email *trustworthy*
— is that **the typed address is never trusted until the email is clicked.** The
delivered-and-clicked email *is* the proof. That's what makes the resulting email both
real and consented (which matters for Chapter 7's ad uploads — an unverified typed
address is worthless as a custom-audience input and possibly unlawful to upload).

Once `discord.linked` fires, a journey welcomes them and grants the role:

```ts
import { defineJourney, sendConnectorAction, sendEmail } from "@hogsend/engine";
import { Events, Templates } from "./constants/index.js";

export const discordLinked = defineJourney({
  meta: {
    id: "discord-linked",
    name: "Discord — Linked & Verified",
    enabled: true,
    trigger: { event: Events.DISCORD_LINKED }, // fires after the email is confirmed
    entryLimit: "once",
  },
  run: async (user) => {
    const discordId = String(user.properties.discordId ?? "");
    const guildId = String(user.properties.guildId ?? "");

    // Grant the "Verified / Community" role — the perk that prompted the link.
    await sendConnectorAction({
      connectorId: "discord",
      action: "grantRole",
      args: { guildId, member: discordId, roleId: process.env.DISCORD_ROLE_COMMUNITY ?? "" },
    });

    await sendConnectorAction({
      connectorId: "discord",
      action: "dmMember",
      args: { member: discordId, content: "✅ Verified! You've unlocked the community channels." },
    });

    // Now they're reachable by BOTH email and Discord — an owned, multi-channel contact.
    await sendEmail({
      to: user.email, userId: user.id, journeyStateId: user.stateId,
      template: Templates.ACTIVATION_COMMUNITY,
      subject: "You're in — here's how to get the most from the community",
      journeyName: user.journeyName,
    });
  },
});
```

The same `sendConnectorAction` primitive drives **gamification** — granting roles for
community engagement, which both rewards members and densifies the graph. Hogsend's
real Discord journeys do exactly this: grant a "Hello world" role on a first message, a
"Resonator" role when a member's post gets reactions from five different people, a "Hype
hog" role for reacting to five different people's posts. Each engagement is an event;
each role is a `grantRole` action; each is another edge in the identity graph. This is
the same behavioural-journey machinery from Chapter 6 — the only new thing is that the
*channel* is Discord instead of email, and the trigger is a reaction instead of a signup.

## 8.4 The part the tooling can't do: a community worth joining

A warning before the next code block, because this is where most "build a Discord"
advice quietly fails: **the linking mechanic captures identity; it does not create a
community.** A server with a verification gate, twenty channels, and no conversation is
a list with extra steps — and everyone who joins can *see* it's dead, which a mailing
list at least hides.

The practitioners' consensus (Jono Bacon, who wrote the standard books on this, is the
clearest voice) compresses to:

- **Start with a mission, not a server.** People join a movement or a topic, not your
  product's support channel. Frame the community around the thing your users are trying
  to *become* — better at growth, better at shipping — with your product as the venue.
- **Fewer channels than feels right.** Three active channels beat twenty empty ones.
  Conversation density is the product; split channels only when volume forces it.
- **Rituals create the habit.** A weekly show-and-tell, a changelog thread, an office
  hour — recurring, hosted moments give members a reason to come back on a schedule
  (the community version of Chapter 9's operating cadence).
- **The first 100 members are hand-recruited.** Like Chapter 7's do-things-that-don't-
  scale: personally invite, personally welcome, personally introduce members to each
  other until conversation is self-sustaining.

## 8.5 Linking Telegram

Telegram has no native email field — identity is phone-based — so linking uses a bot
deep-link plus an email-confirm flow, the same proof-by-clicking pattern as Discord. A
member runs `/link you@example.com`; the journey emails a single-use confirm link:

```ts
export const telegramLinkRequest = defineJourney({
  meta: {
    id: "telegram-link-request",
    name: "Telegram — Link Request (/link)",
    enabled: true,
    trigger: { event: TelegramEvents.LINK_REQUESTED },
    entryLimit: "unlimited",
  },
  run: async (user) => {
    const chatId = String(user.properties.chatId ?? "");
    const fromId = String(user.properties.fromId ?? "");
    const email = String(user.properties.email ?? "");

    const reply = (text) =>
      sendConnectorAction({ connectorId: "telegram", action: "sendMessage", args: { chatId, text } });

    if (!email) {
      await reply("To connect your email, send:\n\n/link you@example.com");
      return;
    }

    // Mint a server-sealed, single-use confirm token (rate-limited, fail-closed).
    const minted = await telegramColdConnect.mintConfirm({ platformUserId: fromId, email });
    if (!minted.ok) {
      await reply("Linking is briefly unavailable — please try again shortly.");
      return;
    }

    const url = telegramColdConnect.confirmUrl({ apiPublicUrl: process.env.API_PUBLIC_URL, token: minted.token });
    await getEmailService().send({
      template: "transactional/magic-link",
      props: { magicLinkUrl: url, expiresIn: "15 minutes" },
      to: email, userId: email, userEmail: email,
      subject: "Confirm your Telegram connection",
      category: "transactional",
      skipPreferenceCheck: true,
    });
    await reply(`📧 I've emailed a confirmation link to ${email}. Open it to finish — expires in 15 minutes.`);
  },
});
```

Clicking the link binds `telegram ↔ email` and stitches the analytics identity from the
client (so you get the real geo/IP). Referrals on Telegram ride the same deep-link
mechanism: a unique `t.me/<bot>?start=<referrerId>` link lets the bot record who brought
whom — which is the attribution primitive the referral loop (§8.7) is built on.

## 8.6 The identity graph (why this compounds)

Every link event writes an **edge** between two ways of reaching the same person:

```
email ↔ Discord ID ↔ Telegram ID ↔ wallet ↔ anonymous web id ↔ PostHog person
```

Collect enough edges and you have an **identity graph**: one unified profile that spans
devices, sessions, and channels. The payoffs stack:

- **Retroactive stitching.** When an anonymous visitor finally gives an email or links
  Discord, all their prior touchpoints attach to the now-known profile — so attribution
  works on data *you own*, independent of cookies.
- **Channel choice on reactivation.** A lapsed user isn't reachable only by an ad you
  must re-buy — you can email them, ping them in Discord, or DM them on Telegram. The
  denser the graph, the more ways back in, and the lower your cost to re-reach anyone.
- **Better ads, legally.** Those verified, consented emails are exactly the high-EMQ
  custom-audience and lookalike-seed inputs from Chapter 7. The graph you build here is
  the fuel the ad machine there runs on.

There's a strategic layer on top of the operational one. Chris Dixon's
[come for the tool, stay for the network](https://cdixon.org/2015/01/31/come-for-the-tool-stay-for-the-network/)
describes the classic ladder: a single-player tool earns the user, and the network they
join is what keeps them (and keeps competitors out). A linked community sits exactly on
that ladder — the product is the tool; the community and its graph are the network. And
a16z's [Hidden Networks](https://a16z.com/hidden-networks-network-effects-that-dont-look-like-network-effects/)
makes the subtler point that defensible networks often don't look like social networks —
an identity graph connecting your users' channels, reachable by you and no competitor,
is precisely such a hidden network. Features can be copied in a quarter; a dense,
consented identity graph has to be earned edge by edge.

Hogsend builds this graph on its **own** identity model (it stitches email ↔ channel ↔
anonymous), and its connectors emit the engagement events — `discord.linked`,
`reaction_added`, `member.joined`, `link.clicked` — that both trigger journeys *and* add
edges. The graph is the hub of the flywheel; every channel you link spins it faster. And
because it's *yours* — not a rented list inside someone else's platform — it's the
compounding asset a growth team is actually building over time. Features come and go;
the identity graph only gets denser.

## 8.7 The referral loop

Referrals are where the owned graph pays off most, because **a referral program only
works if you can attribute it** — and attribution is exactly what the graph gives you.
The prize is real:
[Lenny Rachitsky's survey of referral programs](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/this-week-16-building-a-referrals)
found they commonly drive **15–50% of new-user growth** at companies that get them
right — and, in the same breath, that most programs see **under 10% of users ever
refer anyone**. Both numbers matter: the upside is large, and the median program
underdelivers because it was bolted on rather than designed.

### Incentive structures that work

- **Double-sided (give-get).** Reward *both* the referrer and the new user. It removes
  the "I'm asking a favour" friction. **Dropbox** is the canonical case: 500 MB of
  storage to *each side* per referral, capped at 16 GB. The reward was the product's own
  core value, the ask was the last step of onboarding, and it drove **~3,900% growth in
  15 months.** (The backstory: paid ads were costing Dropbox far more than a customer was
  worth, so a product-value referral was dramatically cheaper.)
- **Milestone / tiered.** An escalating ladder of rewards — **Morning Brew**'s newsletter
  referral program (stickers → shirt → bigger swag → trip to HQ) is the model, with a
  leaderboard for status. Near-zero marginal cost per reward; great for community.
- **Status / access.** For pre-launch and web3/games, the reward is *earlier access*, a
  *whitelist spot*, or an *exclusive role* — scarcity instead of cash. **Robinhood**'s
  skip-the-line waitlist is the classic.

The design principles that run across all of them: sharing that costs nothing
(prewritten copy, a one-click link), rewards on both sides, a reward aligned to product
value, visible progress (a dashboard or leaderboard), and **an ask timed to peak
engagement** — right after the aha moment, which is the referral journey from Chapter
6, §6.8.

### The honest failure mode: referred users can be your worst users

Before settling on a reward, it's worth sitting with Andrew Chen's
[counterpoint](https://andrewchen.com/worst-users-referral-trials-coupons-gamification/):
users acquired through heavy incentives — referral bounties, coupons, gamified
rewards — are often the *lowest-quality* cohort you acquire, because the incentive
(not the product) did the selecting. The symptoms: referred users who never activate,
reward-farmers inviting throwaway accounts, and a referral "channel" whose cohort
retention embarrasses every other source.

The defences are all things this course already gave you:

- **Cohorting the referred users** (Chapter 4) and comparing their retention against
  other channels — a referral program is judged on retained users, not invites sent.
- **Rewarding verified behaviour, not signups.** When the referee has to verify email,
  link an account, or hit a real activation milestone before the reward fires, farmers
  lose interest. This is exactly the anti-fraud power the identity graph gives you
  (below).
- **A reward aligned to product value** (Dropbox's storage, not cash), so the people
  the incentive attracts are people who wanted the product anyway.

### Why the owned graph makes attribution work

Cookie-based referral tracking breaks on the exact journey referrals take: a friend
shares a link, the recipient clicks on their phone, then signs up on a laptop weeks
later. Last-click misses it; the referrer goes uncredited; the program looks like it
doesn't work and gets cut.

An owned identity graph fixes this:

- First-party referral codes/links are tracked **independent of third-party cookies**,
  so you capture the conversion even as browsers tighten.
- The graph **resolves the new user across devices** and **back-credits the referrer**
  when the anonymous visitor finally identifies (gives email / links Discord).
- You can enforce **anti-fraud rules** — the referee must verify their email or link an
  account or hit a real milestone before the reward fires — because the *whole graph is
  yours*. A Discord-linked referral resolves to a real verified person, not a cookie.

And the natural community fit closes the loop: **"X verified referrals → unlock a Discord
role"** puts the reward *in the owned channel*, which densifies the graph further. The
reward for growing your audience is itself a deeper hook into your audience.

Hogsend ships a referral v1 on exactly this shape: `visited → converted → credited`, two
journeys plus a Discord role granted at a milestone — all on its own identity graph, no
cookies required.

### Build vs buy: the referral tooling landscape

You don't have to build referral attribution from scratch, and a growth team should know
the off-the-shelf options:

- **Rewardful / GrowSurf / Referral Rock / Viral Loops** — hosted referral SaaS, fast to
  stand up, with dashboards and reward logic out of the box. **Buy when** you want a
  program live this week and a simple link-based attribution is enough.
- **PartnerStack / FirstPromoter** — affiliate/partner-program flavoured, for paid
  referrers and influencers rather than user-to-user.
- **Build it on your own graph (Hogsend's approach)** — **worth it when** cross-device
  and cross-channel attribution matters, when the reward lives in your product or
  community (a role, credits, access), and when you want anti-fraud tied to real verified
  identity. The hosted tools mostly rely on cookies/links and can't resolve the
  phone-click → laptop-signup gap; your graph can.

The honest split: a hosted tool is the right first move for a simple cash/credit referral
on a single channel. The owned-graph build wins precisely when referrals are
cross-device, community-rewarded, or fraud-prone — which is most of the interesting cases
for the products this course is written for.

## 8.8 Other ways to own an audience

Discord/Telegram-on-your-own-infra is this course's emphasis because it builds the graph
you own outright. But "owned audience" is a spectrum, and the alternatives are worth
naming:

- **Newsletter platforms (beehiiv, ConvertKit, Substack).** A hosted email list is a
  genuinely owned audience in the sense that matters most — you can export it and reach it
  without an algorithm. beehiiv even has a built-in referral program. **Use one when**
  email is your whole channel and you don't need the behavioural-journey or identity-graph
  depth. The trade-off: your list and its data live in their platform, and you don't get
  the cross-channel graph.
- **Community platforms (Circle, Slack, Discord, Geneva).** Circle and Slack are
  higher-polish, more B2B-feeling; Discord is the default for consumer/gaming/web3 and the
  one with the richest bot ecosystem for the link-for-perk mechanic. Choose by where your
  audience already is.
- **The fully-hosted CRM/audience suites (HubSpot, Customer.io audiences).** Convenient,
  and they own the data model. Fine if you've decided to rent the infrastructure.

The principle that ranks them: **the more of the identity graph you own outright, the more
the audience compounds and the less you're exposed to a platform's pricing or policy
changes.** A hosted newsletter is a fine, real owned audience; an identity graph on your
own infrastructure is the maximal version. The rung worth picking is the one that
matches how central owned growth is to your business.

### For your kind of startup

- **Consumer / gaming / web3** — Discord is the owned channel; the link-for-a-role
  mechanic is both activation and acquisition. This is your main chapter.
- **Dev tool** — a mix: a high-quality newsletter (changelog/deep-dives) plus a Discord
  or Slack for support and community. The docs-to-Discord path is your loop.
- **B2B SaaS** — email list + maybe a Slack community for customers; referrals are often
  partner/affiliate-shaped (PartnerStack territory) rather than viral.
- **E-commerce** — email + SMS list is the owned audience; post-purchase referral
  (give-get discount) is the canonical loop, and a hosted tool is usually enough.

## 8.9 The full funnel, assembled

Putting Chapters 7 and 8 together, this is the complete path — and every stage captures
an identity edge:

| Stage | Goal | What's captured |
|---|---|---|
| **Ad / content** | Cold reach | `fbclid` / UTM on the landing page |
| **Landing** | Intent | anon id stitched to the click |
| **Email capture + verify** | Own the contact | **email** (consented) |
| **Discord/Telegram link (perk)** | Densify the graph | **email ↔ Discord/Telegram** |
| **Lifecycle journeys** | Activate & retain | engagement events on your own spine |
| **Referral loop** | Compound | referrer ↔ referee edge, often a Discord role |

The feedback loops that make it a flywheel rather than a funnel — the
[growth-loops model](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-loops) from Chapter 2, finally
built:

- Verified emails → **custom audiences + lookalike seeds** → cheaper, better-matched ads
  (Chapter 7).
- Server-side deep conversions → **smarter Meta optimisation** (Chapter 7).
- Owned channels → **near-free reactivation** (this chapter).
- Referrals → **growth that lowers blended CAC over time** (this chapter).

The identity graph sits at the hub. Every turn of the loop adds edges, and every edge
makes the next turn cheaper. That's the difference between a funnel you keep refilling and
a loop that compounds.

## 8.10 Before you move on

A few things worth doing while this chapter is fresh:

1. **Choose the perk** that's worth linking for (a role, access, credits, a whitelist).
   The sweet spot: valuable enough to convert, not so valuable it attracts farmers —
   §8.2's two design considerations are the test.
2. **Stand up the Discord and/or Telegram connector** with the `/link` email-verify
   flow — this is the moment your list starts becoming a graph.
3. **The `discord-linked` / `telegram-linked` journey** grants the perk and welcomes
   people across both channels; it's the code from §8.3, nearly verbatim.
4. **Give the community a reason to exist beyond the perk**: a mission statement, three
   channels, one weekly ritual (§8.4). The first members are worth recruiting by hand —
   conversation density is what everyone who joins next will judge you on.
5. **Place the referral ask at your aha milestone** (Chapter 6, §6.8) with verified
   referrals rewarded by a role — and it's worth putting "referred-cohort retention" on
   the dashboard before celebrating the invite count.
6. **Sync your verified, consented emails** as a Meta custom audience and lookalike
   seed (Chapter 7) — this is where the two chapters' machines connect.

That's the whole machine. Chapter 9 turns running it into a weekly rhythm, and Chapter
10 sequences everything you've built into a 30/60/90/180-day plan.

title: "Stop logging in: alerts and subscriptions"
description: "The goal isn't to remember to check — it's for the important charts to come to you. Alerts fire on a threshold, subscriptions deliver a scheduled snapshot, and tuning matters more than setting."
---

The goal is not to *remember* to check these. The goal is for the important ones to
come to you, and for you to only open PostHog when something's worth investigating.

- **Alerts** fire when an insight crosses a threshold or trips anomaly detection, and
  notify you in-app, by email, on Slack, or via webhook. One on each critical chart
  covers it: "signups dropped below X today," "errors spiked." You choose how often
  each checks (hourly/daily/weekly).
- **Subscriptions** send a scheduled snapshot of an insight or dashboard to email or
  Slack on a cadence. A weekly snapshot of your North Star dashboard, delivered Monday
  morning, keeps the team aligned with zero effort.

Tuning matters more than setting. An alert that fires daily is furniture within a
week — nobody clicks it, and then nobody clicks the one that mattered. Two rules keep
alerts honest: **every alert that fires gets acted on or re-thresholded** (no third
option), and **prefer few loud alerts to many quiet ones** — three alerts someone
investigates beat fifteen that scroll by. If you're deleting an alert because it never
mattered, that's the system working.

Set the alerts once and your daily ritual shrinks to: read the Slack alerts, and open
the dashboard only when one fires. That two-minute daily glance is the first rung of a
full operating cadence — daily notice, weekly review, monthly re-examination — that the
operating-cadence chapter lays out in full.

There's a deeper seam here, and it's exactly the one Hogsend extends. A PostHog alert
tells *you* something changed; a Hogsend **journey** — an automated sequence that
messages the user when an event fires — tells *the user* something instead, without you
in the loop. Notice for you, action for them: turning that second loop on is what the
lifecycle chapters are about.

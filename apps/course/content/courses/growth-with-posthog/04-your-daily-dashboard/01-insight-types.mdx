PostHog has seven ways to look at your events, and you'll lean on four of them. All
seven are still worth knowing, because picking the wrong insight type is the most
common reason a founder ends up staring at a chart that can't answer their question.

- **Trends** — a metric over time (counts, unique users, formulas), with breakdowns by
  any property. The workhorse. *Read it for direction and breakdown:* "signups per day,
  by channel — which channel is growing?" *Its failure mode:* counting events when you
  mean people. Ten pageviews from one user and one pageview from ten users look identical
  until you switch the series to unique users.
- **Funnels** — step-by-step conversion
  ([docs](https://posthog.com/docs/product-analytics/funnels)). Shows the drop-off at
  each step and the time to convert. *Read it for the worst step:* the biggest drop is
  your biggest leak. Click a step to see — or replay — the users who fell out. Two
  settings people miss: the **conversion window** (a user who converts on day 15 of a
  7-day window counts as dropped) and **exclusion steps** (remove users who, say, hit an
  error page between steps). *Its failure mode:* steps in an order users don't actually
  follow — check User Paths first if conversion looks impossibly low.
- **Retention** — a cohort grid ([docs](https://posthog.com/docs/product-analytics/retention)):
  pick a start event ("signed up") and a return event ("did the core action"). Each row
  is a cohort, each column a later period. *Read it for the shape* (the retention piece
  walks through it) — this is the leaky-bucket test made visual.
- **Lifecycle** — splits your active users each period into **New**, **Returning**,
  **Resurrecting**, and **Dormant**. *Read it for whether growth is real:* a big Dormant
  band under modest New growth means churn-and-replace, not growth.
- **Stickiness** — *how many times* users did something in a period (depth), as opposed
  to retention's *whether they came back*. *Read it to find your power users* and what
  separates them: if users who fire your core action 5+ days a week all created three
  or more projects in week one, "three projects" is a candidate activation bar worth
  testing (Chapter 2's method).
- **User Paths** — a flow diagram of how people move through the product. *Read it to
  discover* unexpected routes and dead ends you didn't think to build a funnel for —
  the classic find is a settings or pricing page appearing in the middle of what you
  assumed was a straight onboarding line.
- **SQL** — write SQL (HogQL) directly when the UI can't express your question.
  PostHog's AI assistant (Max) can write these for you, which makes SQL approachable even
  if you don't know HogQL.

The four you'll read daily are **Trends, Funnels, Retention, and Lifecycle.**

**What to take from it:**

- A funnel is just ordered steps; rename each step so the chart reads clearly, then break
  it down by a property (channel, continent) to see which segment actually converts.
- Two levers change what a funnel *means*: strict vs sequential step order (strict counts
  only direct step-to-step, and conversion usually drops), and exclusion steps that remove
  users who took a detour — say, hit the support page — between steps.
- Filtering to one traffic source shows fewer users but often a higher conversion — segment
  before you judge the number.

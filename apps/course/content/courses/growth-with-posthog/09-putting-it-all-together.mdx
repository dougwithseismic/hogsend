## 9.1 The whole thing, in one loop

Everything in this course is one loop with the **identity graph** at its hub:

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │                                                         │
   drive traffic (Ch 7)                                           │
   ads · content · community                                      │
        │                                                         │
        ▼                                                         │
   capture identity (Ch 8)          measure everything (Ch 1–4)   │
   email verify · link Discord  ───► PostHog: funnels, retention  │
   /Telegram · perks for linking     the daily dashboard          │
        │                                  │                      │
        ▼                                  ▼                      │
   ┌─────────── IDENTITY GRAPH ───────────┐                       │
   │ email ↔ Discord ↔ Telegram ↔ anon    │                       │
   └──────────────────┬───────────────────┘                      │
                      │                                           │
                      ▼                                           │
   keep them (Ch 5–6)                                             │
   lifecycle journeys in Hogsend ──► fire conversions + audiences ┘
   welcome · nudge · win-back ·       back to the ads (Ch 7)
   dunning · referral                 → referrals lower CAC (Ch 8)
```

Read it as a sentence: **you measure so you can see the leaks; you keep users with
lifecycle journeys so the bucket holds; then you drive traffic and capture every visitor
into an audience you own; that audience feeds better ads and a referral loop, which
lowers your cost of growth — and the whole thing is measurable because every step fires
an event.**

The reason the order is *measure → keep → grow* and not the reverse is the leaky bucket
(Chapter 2). Acquisition is the last lever because it's the one that wastes the most
money when the bucket leaks and pays back the most when it doesn't. That single ordering
decision, agreed by the team back in Chapter 0, is the most valuable thing in the course.

## 9.2 Who does what

A quick reference for the division of labour the course is built on:

| Job | Tool | Chapters |
|---|---|---|
| Decide what to measure (AARRR, North Star) and own the order | You | 0, 2 |
| Collect events, identify people | PostHog | 1, 3 |
| See the funnel, retention, leaks | PostHog | 2, 4 |
| Run lifecycle journeys (email, Discord, Telegram) | Hogsend | 5, 6, 8 |
| Drive paid traffic | Meta ads (or the right channel) | 7 |
| Track conversions accurately | Pixel + CAPI, via PostHog | 7 |
| Capture & link identity, referrals | Hogsend connectors | 8 |

**PostHog detects. Hogsend acts. You decide.** Keep that split clear and the system
stays simple no matter how much you add.

## 9.3 The build order at a glance

The sequence is the course's spine, compressed to four gates:

1. **Day 0 — commit.** Name the growth owner, write the activation sentence, agree
   retention-before-acquisition out loud (Chapter 0).
2. **Days 1–30 — Measure.** PostHog live, tracking plan, activation event, the daily
   dashboard. Exit when you can say where the funnel leaks, with numbers (Chapters 1–4).
3. **Days 31–60 — Keep.** Welcome, dunning, win-back journeys — with a holdout. Exit
   when lift is measured, not vibed (Chapters 5–6).
4. **Days 61–180 — Grow, then compound.** Open the tap, capture identity into owned
   channels, then turn the whole thing into an experimentation program (Chapters 7–8,
   and §9.5 below).

That's the summary; **Chapter 10 is the full plan** — each phase with its interactive
checklist, exit criteria, realistic targets, and the anti-patterns that blow it up.
This chapter is about what keeps the machine alive *after* it's built: the cadence and
the experimentation culture.

## 9.4 The operating cadence

Once it's built, it runs on a rhythm, not on heroics. This is the ritual that makes you
a growth *team* rather than a team that did a growth project once (Chapter 0). The
principle — [Reforge calls it your operating
cadence](https://www.reforge.com/blog/operating-cadence) — is that the *rhythm* of
looking is itself a designed system: decide in advance what gets read daily, weekly,
and monthly, and by whom, so attention stops depending on anyone's memory.

**Daily (2 minutes).** Read the Slack alerts. Open the dashboard only if one fired.
Glance at the activation funnel and active-users trend. That's it — the daily job is
*noticing*, not analysing. If the daily glance regularly takes more than five minutes,
your alerts are badly tuned, not your discipline.

**Weekly (30 minutes, the growth review).** The one meeting this course asks you to
hold, and it has a fixed agenda — a compressed version of the weekly growth meeting
[Brian Balfour has documented](https://brianbalfour.com/essays/growth-meeting) from
running growth at HubSpot and Reforge:

1. **Metrics read (10 min).** Retention curve and lifecycle chart — is growth real or
   churn-and-replace? North Star vs last week. No debugging in the meeting; note
   anomalies and assign an owner.
2. **Journeys and spend (10 min).** Each live journey's send volume and holdout lift.
   Ad performance: cost per conversion, EMQ, which audiences work. Kill or scale
   decisions get made here, out loud.
3. **Experiments (10 min).** What finished (result + learning, in one sentence each),
   what launches next from the top of the backlog (§9.5), what's blocked.

The discipline that makes the meeting work: it reviews **learnings and decisions**, not
status. "The welcome-nudge holdout shows +4 points activation, we're keeping it and
removing the day-5 email" is a growth review; "we sent 3,400 emails this week" is
theatre.

Two habits close the weekly loop. **Watch 3–5 session replays** of the week's biggest
drop-off — the qualitative *why* behind the quantitative *where* (Chapter 4). And
**write the week's one learning down** somewhere permanent. A learning that lives in
one person's head is a learning the team will pay to re-discover in six months.

**Monthly (an hour).** Zoom out to the questions that are too slow to see weekly:

- Re-examine the **activation metric** — is it still the best predictor of retention?
  Run Chapter 2's correlation-then-experiment method if you're unsure.
- Report **incremental lift** from the lifecycle program (global holdout) — the number
  that justifies the whole Keep phase to anyone who asks.
- Review **blended CAC** and the referral loop's contribution. Is growth getting
  cheaper as the owned audience grows? That's Chapter 8's flywheel, showing up in the
  finances.
- **Prune.** Kill journeys with no lift, events no one queries, ad sets that don't
  convert. A growth system accretes cruft exactly like a codebase.

The cadence is the point. A growth system that's checked on a rhythm survives a busy
quarter; one that depends on someone remembering to look does not.

## 9.5 The experimentation program

The cadence keeps the machine observed; experiments are how it improves. The difference
between a team that occasionally A/B tests and a team with an experimentation *program*
is three artifacts:

**A scored backlog.** Every growth idea — a new journey, a pricing page change, an
onboarding step removed — goes into one list as a **hypothesis**, not a task: *"if we
[change], [metric] will move [direction] because [reasoning]."* Score each on
**[ICE](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/ice-scoring-model)** — impact, confidence,
ease, each 1–10 — and let the scores order the list.
([PIE](https://conversion.com/framework/pie-framework/) — potential, importance, ease —
is the same idea with different letters; pick one and stop debating frameworks.) The
score's job isn't precision, it's forcing the *"this would be huge"* claim to survive
contact with *"how confident are we, actually?"* — and giving the weekly meeting a top
of the list to pull from, instead of the loudest idea in the room.

**A steady tempo.** Sean Ellis — the man who coined both "growth hacking" and the
North Star framing this course uses — argues the compounding asset is your
**velocity of tests**: more shots on goal means faster learning, and the learning is
what compounds even when individual tests fail.

Reforge's [experiment management
system](https://www.reforge.com/blog/growth-experiment-management-system) puts a
number on the same idea — a standard template plus a visible backlog roughly tripled
one team's testing velocity — and [First Round's growth-sprint
process](https://review.firstround.com/the-secret-to-running-effective-growth-sprints-follow-this-process-to-learn-faster/)
wraps it in a sprint so every experiment ends in a written learning. For a startup,
"high tempo" might mean one experiment a week. That's fine. One a week, every week,
with results written down, beats a burst of ten followed by a quarter of nothing.

**Honest rules.** Tempo without discipline manufactures false wins, so the program
needs three:

- **Not everything deserves an experiment.** Obvious fixes, reversible changes, and
  changes too small to measure should just ship —
  [Lenny's guide to when *not* to test](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-running-an-experiment)
  is the checklist. Experiments are for decisions that are expensive to get wrong.
- **Respect sample size.** At startup traffic, most tests can't detect small effects.
  Prefer big swings (whole journeys, whole onboarding flows) over button colours, and
  treat "no detectable difference" as the honest, common result it is.
  [Kohavi and Lenny's A/B testing guide](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/the-ultimate-guide-to-ab-testing)
  covers the failure modes — peeking, underpowered wins, p-hacking by another name.
- **Write down the losers.** A result only counts if it's recorded — hypothesis,
  what happened, what you'd do differently. The write-up is thirty seconds; the
  re-run of a forgotten failed experiment is a month.

Where does the *content* of the backlog come from? The machine you built:
the funnel's biggest drop-off (Chapter 4), the journey with no lift (Chapter 5), the
channel with decent volume but poor activation (Chapter 7), the replay where users rage-
click (Chapter 4). If the backlog feels empty, the dashboard is being read too politely.
[YC's growth curriculum](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6k-growth-for-startups)
makes the same loop explicit at whole-company scale: pick the one metric, find the
constraint, test against it, repeat.

## 9.6 The mistakes to not make

A short list, each tied to where the course warned you:

- **Scaling acquisition into a leaky bucket** (Ch 2). The most expensive mistake there
  is. Fix retention first.
- **Chasing someone else's magic number** (Ch 2). Find *your* activation metric with the
  correlation-then-experiment method. The famous numbers are rally cries.
- **Building event names dynamically** (Ch 3). One ruined analytics setup. One fixed
  event, variables in properties.
- **Installing analytics and never looking** (Ch 0, Ch 4). The dashboard graveyard.
  Measurement without a ritual and without action is theatre.
- **Experiment theatre** (§9.5). Running underpowered tests to "validate" decisions
  already made, celebrating wins nobody could reproduce, never writing down a loser.
  Tempo counts learnings, not launches.
- **Trusting the Pixel alone** (Ch 7). It under-counts by a third or more. Pixel *and*
  CAPI, deduped, fed from where you have identity.
- **Treating server-side as a consent loophole** (Ch 7). It isn't. Hashed email is
  pseudonymous, not anonymous. Capture consent.
- **Measuring opens instead of lift** (Ch 5). Opens don't prove causation. Use holdouts.
- **Renting your whole audience** (Ch 8). Capture identity into owned channels so you
  never have to pay to reach the same person twice.

## 9.7 You're a growth team now

Come back to where the course started. The tools were never the hard part — you could
install PostHog, Hogsend, and a pixel in an afternoon. The thing that actually changed,
if you did the work, is how your team *operates*:

- You **measure honestly** — decisions cite the funnel, not the loudest opinion, and
  "activated" means a specific event, not a vibe.
- You **act automatically** — the messages that pull users back to value are journeys in
  your repo, triggered by behaviour, code-reviewed like the rest of the product, and
  proven with holdouts.
- You **own your audience** — every dollar of acquisition lands in a channel you can reach
  again for free, on an identity graph that only gets denser.
- You **learn on a rhythm** — a weekly cadence, a scored backlog, a steady tempo of
  experiments whose results get written down, winners and losers alike.

That's the whole definition of a product-led growth team from Chapter 0, and you'll
notice none of it required a growth department. It required one owner, one loop, and a
rhythm. A founder who can read a retention curve and ship a journey *is* the growth team —
and the system you've built is what lets a team that small operate like one many times its
size.

It also doesn't matter what your startup is. The tactics flexed by type all the way
through — activation means a first match for a marketplace, a first deploy for a dev tool,
a second purchase for e-commerce, a linked Discord for a community — but the operating
model never changed: instrument, measure, act, own, repeat. That portability is the whole
point. Growth stops being a thing you hope works and becomes a system you run.

## 4.1 The insight types, and how to read them

PostHog has seven ways to look at your events. You'll lean on four. Know all seven so
you reach for the right one — picking the wrong insight type is the most common reason a
founder stares at a chart that can't answer their question.

- **Trends** — a metric over time (counts, unique users, formulas), with breakdowns by
  any property. The workhorse. *Read it for direction and breakdown:* "signups per day,
  by channel — which channel is growing?" *Its failure mode:* counting events when you
  mean people. Ten pageviews from one user and one pageview from ten users look identical
  until you switch the series to unique users.
- **Funnels** — step-by-step conversion
  ([docs](https://posthog.com/docs/product-analytics/funnels)). Shows the drop-off at
  each step and the time to convert. *Read it for the worst step:* the biggest drop is
  your biggest leak. Click a step to see — or replay — the users who fell out. Two
  settings people miss: the **conversion window** (a user who converts on day 15 of a
  7-day window counts as dropped) and **exclusion steps** (remove users who, say, hit an
  error page between steps). *Its failure mode:* steps in an order users don't actually
  follow — check User Paths first if conversion looks impossibly low.
- **Retention** — a cohort grid ([docs](https://posthog.com/docs/product-analytics/retention)):
  pick a start event ("signed up") and a return event ("did the core action"). Each row
  is a cohort, each column a later period. *Read it for the shape* (§4.3) — this is the
  leaky-bucket test made visual.
- **Lifecycle** — splits your active users each period into **New**, **Returning**,
  **Resurrecting**, and **Dormant**. *Read it for whether growth is real:* a big Dormant
  band under modest New growth means churn-and-replace, not growth.
- **Stickiness** — *how many times* users did something in a period (depth), as opposed
  to retention's *whether they came back*. *Read it to find your power users* and what
  separates them: if users who fire your core action 5+ days a week all created three
  or more projects in week one, "three projects" is a candidate activation bar worth
  testing (Chapter 2's method).
- **User Paths** — a flow diagram of how people move through the product. *Read it to
  discover* unexpected routes and dead ends you didn't think to build a funnel for —
  the classic find is a settings or pricing page appearing in the middle of what you
  assumed was a straight onboarding line.
- **SQL** — write SQL (HogQL) directly when the UI can't express your question.
  PostHog's AI assistant (Max) can write these for you, which makes SQL approachable even
  if you don't know HogQL.

The four you'll read daily are **Trends, Funnels, Retention, and Lifecycle.**

## 4.2 The daily checklist

You don't need to stare at twenty charts. You need a short, fixed list you read the
same way every morning. Here it is, with what each one means *and what to do when it
looks wrong* — because a chart you don't act on is the "dashboard graveyard" failure mode
from Chapter 0.

Before the list, be honest about the baseline you're starting from:

There's no wrong answer — but the gap between "when something feels off" and "daily"
is precisely what this chapter closes, and by §4.5 the daily version costs two minutes.

### 1. Active users (Trends — unique users on your core action)
**The pulse.** If this is flat or falling week-over-week, nothing else matters yet.
Watch the *trend*, not the absolute number — early on, direction is the signal. Define
"active" as your **core action**, not "opened the app": a user who logs in and does
nothing is not active in any sense that pays you.
*If it's falling:* check the lifecycle chart (#6) — is it fewer New, or more Dormant?

### 2. New signups (Trends — count of `user_signed_up`, server-side)
**Is acquisition working?** A useful number, but a vanity one on its own: a signup
spike with no matching activation means you attracted the wrong people or overpromised.
Always read it next to chart 3, and always with the **channel breakdown** on — a flat
total can hide one channel doubling while another collapses.
*If it spikes but activation doesn't:* you bought the wrong traffic — check the channel
breakdown.

### 3. Activation funnel (Funnel — `signup → your aha event`)
**The most actionable chart you have.** The drop-off between steps shows you *exactly*
where onboarding leaks. Click the worst step and pull the list of users who fell out —
or jump straight to their session replays (chart 7) to see why. A useful discipline:
know your funnel's *baseline* by heart (say, "62% reach step 2, 31% activate") so a
change registers as a change and not a guess.
*If the drop is at one step:* that step is your week's work.

### 4. Signup conversion rate (Web Analytics goal, or a funnel)
**Is the top of the funnel smooth?** Visitors → signup. A low number here points at
friction on the landing page or in the signup flow itself.
*If it's low:* watch replays of the landing page; it's usually copy or a form, not the
product.

### 5. Weekly retention curve (Retention — start: signup; return: core action)
**The single best product-market-fit signal.** Read the *shape* (the full how-to is
§4.3): a curve that decays to zero is a leaky bucket; one that flattens above zero is a
product with real pull.

### 6. Lifecycle (Lifecycle insight on your core action)
**Is your growth real?** A healthy chart shows New and Returning growing. A big red
**Dormant** band underneath modest New growth means you're refilling a leaking bucket —
the same users churning and being replaced. This is the leaky bucket, drawn for you.
*If Dormant dominates:* stop thinking about acquisition; this is a Chapter 5–6 problem.

### 7. Session replays of drop-offs and errors
**The "why" behind the numbers.** Filter replays to users who hit a funnel drop-off, a
rage-click, or an error, and watch a few. Five minutes of watching real people struggle
will teach you more than an hour of staring at a chart. The workflow that makes this
cheap: never browse replays cold — arrive from a chart (a funnel step, an error, a
rage-click filter) so every replay you watch is pre-selected to show you a problem
([replay docs](https://posthog.com/docs/session-replay)).
*What you're looking for:* the moment of hesitation. Cursor circling, tab-switching,
a form field cleared and retyped — the qualitative signature of the leak your funnel
found quantitatively.

### 8. Errors / exceptions (Error Tracking)
**Production health.** New or spiking issues. Best piped to Slack (see §4.5) so you
don't have to remember to look. *A spike in errors often explains a dip in activation —
check this before blaming your onboarding copy.*

### 9. Paid conversion (free → paid %)
**The strongest value signal there is.** People paying is the hardest proof that the
problem you solve is worth solving. Track the conversion rate, not just the count.

That's the list. The first time, it'll take you twenty minutes. Once it's a dashboard,
it's a two-minute glance.

### What a real read looks like

To make the ritual concrete, here's a Monday morning read of the nine charts, as it
actually goes for a small SaaS:

*Active users: flat, fine. Signups: up 18% — nice, but the breakdown says it's all one
Reddit thread, so don't celebrate a trend. Activation funnel: baseline is 31%, today
it reads 24% — that's outside normal bounce. Worst step is still "create first
project," but the drop moved to the step before it: the workspace-naming screen.
Errors: a new exception, `WorkspaceNameValidation`, started Friday. There's the story.
Replays: three watched, all three show users typing a workspace name with a space,
getting a red field with no message, and leaving. Lifecycle and retention: unchanged,
they move on week timescales. Paid conversion: unchanged.*

One decision comes out of it — fix the validation message today, then confirm tomorrow
that the funnel step recovers to baseline. Total time: eight minutes, because the
charts were pre-built and the errors alert had already flagged Friday's exception.

Notice what the read did *not* involve: no exploring, no new charts, no debate about
what "active" means. The nine tiles answered "where's the leak?" and the replays
answered "why?" — and every future morning starts from the same fixed list, so a
deviation registers instantly. That's the entire skill. It's pattern-matching against
a baseline you know by heart, and it only works because the list doesn't change.

## 4.3 How to read a retention curve (the one chart that matters most)

Most founders glance at retention and move on. Read it properly and it answers your
single most important strategic question — *am I in the "fix the bucket" or "open the
tap" phase?* Three things to look at:

- **The shape, not the height.** Three archetypes: a curve that **slides to zero** (no
  PMF — every cohort eventually leaves), a curve that **declines then flattens** (the
  "smile" or plateau — some cohort sticks forever, the sign of real product-market fit),
  and the rare **smiling curve that ticks back up** (resurrection exceeds churn — usually
  a network-effect or content product). You want, at minimum, a flattening above zero.
- **Where it flattens.** A curve flattening at 40% means four in ten users become
  permanent; flattening at 5% means you have a trickle. The flattening *level* is roughly
  your ceiling without intervention — and lifecycle messaging (Chapter 5) is how you lift
  it.
- **Cohort over cohort.** Are *newer* cohorts retaining better than older ones? That's
  the real signal your product changes are working — each week's improvements should show
  up as a higher curve for the cohorts that joined after them. PostHog's cohort grid lets
  you compare rows directly.

Three technicalities that change what the same grid means — worth knowing before you
panic or celebrate ([Amplitude's retention guide](https://amplitude.com/blog/retention-analysis)
covers all three in depth):

- **N-day vs unbounded retention.** N-day asks "did they return *on* day 7?";
  unbounded asks "did they return on day 7 *or any day after*?" N-day is stricter and
  lower; unbounded suits products used sporadically. Know which your grid shows before
  comparing anything to anything.
- **The right-hand columns are younger.** In a weekly grid, the newest cohort has only
  had one week to prove itself — the bottom-right of the grid is always sparse and
  noisy. Read the *oldest few cohorts* for shape, the newest few for early trend.
- **Small cohorts lie.** A 12-user cohort swinging from 40% to 25% is three people.
  Don't re-plan the roadmap on it; wait for the trend across several cohorts.

A practical benchmark, held loosely: for many B2B SaaS products a flattening D30 in the
*tens of percent* is healthy; consumer social needs higher; e-commerce thinks in repeat-
purchase rate instead. [Lenny Rachitsky's benchmark survey of growth
leaders](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-good-retention-issue-29) puts real
numbers on "good" and "great" by category if you want to calibrate — but your own
cohort-over-cohort trend is a better guide than anyone's benchmark, and retention is
still [the single best PMF signal you
have](https://posthog.com/founders/measure-product-market-fit).

## 4.4 Build it as one dashboard

Don't navigate to nine insights every day. Save each as an insight, add them to a
single **dashboard**, and **pin that dashboard to your project homepage** so it's the
first thing you see when you log in
([dashboards docs](https://posthog.com/docs/product-analytics/dashboards)).

- Put your **North Star metric** (Chapter 2) as the top tile, with its input metrics
  beneath it — the layout itself should say what matters most
  ([PostHog's founder guide to North Star metrics](https://posthog.com/founders/north-star-metrics)
  makes the case that the metric on top shapes what the team optimises).
- Use dashboard-level date and property filters so you can switch the whole board to
  "last 7 days" or "paid users only" at once.
- PostHog ships dashboard templates (web metrics, product health) you can start from
  instead of building blank.
- Give every non-obvious tile a one-line description ("D7 retention, weekly cohorts,
  core action = project_created"). The tile that needs a tribal elder to interpret is
  the tile that stops being read.

This dashboard is a growth team's shared artifact. When everyone opens the same board and
reads the same nine charts the same way, "how are we doing" stops being a meeting and
becomes a glance. That's the operating model from Chapter 0 made concrete — and this
board is exactly what the weekly growth review (Chapter 9, §9.4) opens with.

## 4.5 Stop logging in: alerts and subscriptions

The goal is not to *remember* to check these. The goal is for the important ones to
come to you, and for you to only open PostHog when something's worth investigating.

- **Alerts** fire when an insight crosses a threshold or trips anomaly detection, and
  notify you in-app, by email, on Slack, or via webhook. Set one on each critical
  chart: "signups dropped below X today," "errors spiked." Choose how often it checks
  (hourly/daily/weekly).
- **Subscriptions** send a scheduled snapshot of an insight or dashboard to email or
  Slack on a cadence. A weekly snapshot of your North Star dashboard, delivered Monday
  morning, keeps the team aligned with zero effort.

Tuning matters more than setting. An alert that fires daily is furniture within a
week — nobody clicks it, and then nobody clicks the one that mattered. Two rules keep
alerts honest: **every alert that fires gets acted on or re-thresholded** (no third
option), and **prefer few loud alerts to many quiet ones** — three alerts someone
investigates beat fifteen that scroll by. If you're deleting an alert because it never
mattered, that's the system working.

Set the alerts once and your daily ritual shrinks to: read the Slack alerts, and open
the dashboard only when one fires. That two-minute daily glance is the first rung of
the full operating cadence in Chapter 9 — daily notice, weekly review, monthly
re-examination.

This is also exactly the seam Hogsend extends. PostHog alerts tell *you* something
changed; Hogsend journeys tell *the user* something — automatically, when an event
fires. The next chapter is about that second loop.

## 4.6 Other ways teams stay on top of the numbers

The pinned dashboard plus alerts is the default. Some alternatives and when they fit:

- **The weekly metrics email/Slack post.** A human (or a subscription) posts the five
  headline numbers with one line of commentary every Monday. Lower-tech than a dashboard,
  higher-signal for a team — the commentary forces interpretation, not just observation.
- **Warehouse BI (Metabase, Looker, Lightdash).** If you've outgrown PostHog's insights
  or need to join heavy financial data, a BI tool on your warehouse is more flexible.
  It's also more setup and another tool to maintain — reach for it when PostHog genuinely
  can't express the question, not before.
- **PostHog's home + "Max" AI.** You can ask Max a plain-language question ("what's our
  D7 retention this month vs last?") instead of building an insight. Good for ad-hoc
  questions that don't deserve a permanent tile.

The anti-pattern to avoid: **dashboard sprawl.** A team with forty dashboards has none —
nobody knows which is canonical. One pinned board everyone reads beats a wall of charts
nobody trusts.

### For your kind of startup

- **Marketplace** — your daily pulse is *liquidity*: track both sides (supply added,
  demand served) and the match/transaction rate between them. A healthy one side and a
  starved other is the failure mode.
- **Consumer app** — DAU/MAU stickiness and the retention curve are the whole game; paid
  conversion may not even apply.
- **E-commerce** — repeat-purchase rate and time-between-orders replace "retention curve";
  the activation chart becomes first → second purchase.
- **Dev tool** — the activation funnel (docs → first successful call) is your #1 chart;
  signups without a first call are noise.

## 4.7 A note on reading numbers honestly

Three failure modes to avoid as you build the habit:

- **Watching vanity metrics.** Signups, pageviews, and registered-user counts feel
  good and move easily — [Amplitude's definition](https://amplitude.com/blog/vanity-metrics)
  is blunt: a vanity metric is one that can go up while the business goes nowhere.
  Anchor on the metrics that reflect *value delivered* — activation, retention, paid
  conversion — and treat the vanity numbers as context.
- **Reacting to noise.** Day-to-day numbers bounce. Look at trends over weeks before
  concluding something changed. An alert is for "investigate," not "panic." A single bad
  day is weather; a two-week trend is climate.
- **Reading for confirmation.** The subtle one. If you only dig into charts that look
  wrong when you expected them to look wrong, the dashboard becomes a mirror. The
  discipline is the fixed list read the same way every time (§4.2) — it forces you past
  the charts you'd rather skip. This honesty habit is also what Chapter 9's
  experimentation program depends on: a team that reads charts for confirmation will
  run experiments for confirmation too.

## 4.8 Do this now

1. Create the nine insights from §4.2.
2. Add them to one dashboard; pin it to your homepage; put your North Star on top.
3. Set alerts on signups, activation, and errors → Slack — few and loud.
4. Schedule a weekly subscription of the dashboard to yourself/your team.
5. Read your **retention curve** with §4.3 in hand and say out loud which phase you're
   in — fix the bucket, or open the tap.
6. Watch three session replays of your worst funnel step's drop-outs, arriving from the
   chart, not browsing cold.

You can now *see* your funnel and your leaks. The rest of the course is about acting on
them — first by keeping the users you have, then by going to get more.

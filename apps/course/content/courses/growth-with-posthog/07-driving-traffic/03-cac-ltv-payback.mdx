Whatever the channel, the same three numbers decide whether it's working. The
definitions matter — a16z's
[16 Startup Metrics](https://a16z.com/16-startup-metrics/) exists because founders
routinely get them wrong in ways that flatter the business:

- **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** — *fully loaded* spend to acquire one paying
  customer: ad spend plus the content, tools, and people behind it, divided by
  customers acquired. The classic self-deception is reporting CAC on ad spend alone,
  or on signups instead of customers.
- **LTV (lifetime value)** — the **gross-margin** contribution of a customer over their
  lifetime, where lifetime comes from your churn rate (roughly, monthly margin ÷
  monthly churn). Computing LTV on revenue instead of margin is the second classic
  self-deception — a16z calls both out by name.
- **Payback period** — months of gross margin to recover CAC. For a cash-constrained
  startup this is often the number that matters most: an LTV:CAC of 5 is cold comfort
  if payback takes three years and the bank account empties in one.

The folk benchmark you'll hear is **LTV:CAC ≥ 3 and payback under ~12 months** for
SaaS — better treated as a sanity range than a law; what actually protects you is
knowing your numbers and their trend. Two habits follow from that:

- **CAC is most useful per channel, not blended.** A blended CAC averages your free
  organic users into the paid number, which can hide a channel that's quietly
  unprofitable. Blended CAC is a board metric; per-channel CAC is the operating one.
  Watching the blended figure fall over time is a useful signal in its own right — it
  usually means owned and referral growth is pulling your average cost down.
- **LTV is worth segmenting by cohort and source.** A **cohort** here is a group of
  customers bucketed by when they signed up or where they came from, and users from
  different sources retain differently — paid-social users commonly churn faster than
  organic, having arrived with less intent. If paid cohorts' LTV is half your organic
  LTV, your real paid LTV:CAC may be under water while the blended number looks fine.

**What to take from it:**

- LTV falls out of ARPA and churn: average revenue per account ÷ churn rate, taken down
  to margin — a $100 ARPA at 10% monthly churn is a $1,000 lifetime value, the ceiling on
  what you can spend to acquire that customer.
- Track **gross** revenue retention (churn and contraction only), not just the flashier
  net revenue retention, which can exceed 100% on expansion and quietly hide the bad news
  gross retention would show you.
- MRR (monthly recurring revenue) is not cash flow — a $1,200 annual deal is $100 of MRR —
  and segmenting churn and CAC by customer size or source tells you far more than any
  blended number.

Where do the numbers come from? The machine you already built: revenue events captured
server-side in PostHog (the instrumentation chapter sets that up), and source plus UTM
tags — the `utm_source`/`utm_medium` parameters on a campaign link — landed as person
properties (the PostHog-feeding piece later shows how). So "LTV by acquisition source" is
a PostHog insight, not a spreadsheet safari.

Benchmarks are worth less than your own numbers, so put them in. Watch the payback figure
most when cash is tight — a healthy LTV:CAC with a payback longer than your runway still
sinks you.

Where your users come from today decides which half of this chapter is urgent. A team
growing almost entirely through word of mouth and launches is really asking one question:
is paid worth *starting* at all? A team already spending heavily on ads is asking a
different one: where is that spend leaking, and how much of the audience am I renting
instead of owning? Same tactics, opposite priorities.

The one thing true for both is that paid acquisition only compounds once the product
already keeps the users it wins. Pour traffic into something that can't retain and you're
just *buying churn* — the acquired users leak straight back out, and the ad spend hides
the real problem instead of solving it. So before any of the tactics, it's worth being
honest about your current mix, because that's what tells you whether this chapter is about
starting paid carefully or measuring the paid you already run.

Two facts frame everything that follows:

- **Channels decay.** Andrew Chen's
  [law of shitty clickthroughs](https://andrewchen.com/the-law-of-shitty-clickthroughs/)
  is the empirical observation that every marketing channel's performance degrades as
  it saturates — banner ads went from a 78% click-through rate (CTR — the share of people
  who saw one and clicked) in 1994 to ~0.05%, and every channel since has followed the
  same curve. Whatever works for you now will work less well per dollar next year. The
  counter isn't channel-hopping; it's capturing what a channel brings you into something
  you own, while the channel still works.
- **Early traction rarely comes from paid at all.** Paul Graham's
  [Do Things that Don't Scale](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) is the canonical
  argument: almost all startups get their first users manually — recruiting one at a
  time, launching in communities, doing unscalable favours — and the founders who wait
  for ads to do it stall. If you're pre-revenue, most of this chapter's job is to stop
  you spending money you shouldn't spend yet.

Both facts point the same way. A channel is a lease, not a deed — so the real game isn't
finding the perfect one, it's converting whatever any channel rents you into an audience
you own outright. The next piece is the menu of channels themselves, and why, for a lot
of readers here, paid social isn't the first one.

A gentle reminder on order before anything else: this chapter is deliberately seventh.
If your retention curve (Chapter 4) still decays to zero, this chapter will serve you
much better after Chapters 5–6 have done their work — paid acquisition into a leaky
bucket is, as Chapter 2 put it, buying churn. Teams that prove the bucket holds first
get dramatically more out of every pound they spend here.

The rest of this chapter reads differently depending on where you're starting from, so
it's worth pausing on your current mix before the tactics. A team that's all-organic is
really asking whether paid is worth *starting*; a team that's mostly-paid tends to need
the measurement and owned-audience work first, because every tracking gap and every
rented audience is already costing money.

Two facts frame everything that follows:

- **Channels decay.** Andrew Chen's
  [law of shitty clickthroughs](https://andrewchen.com/the-law-of-shitty-clickthroughs/)
  is the empirical observation that every marketing channel's performance degrades as
  it saturates — banner ads went from 78% CTR in 1994 to ~0.05%, and every channel
  since has followed the same curve. Whatever works for you now will work less well
  per dollar next year. The counter isn't channel-hopping; it's capturing what a
  channel brings you into something you own (Chapter 8) while the channel still works.
- **Early traction rarely comes from paid at all.** Paul Graham's
  [Do Things that Don't Scale](https://paulgraham.com/ds.html) is the canonical
  argument: almost all startups get their first users manually — recruiting one at a
  time, launching in communities, doing unscalable favours — and the founders who wait
  for ads to do it stall. If you're pre-revenue, this chapter's job is mostly to stop
  you spending money you shouldn't spend yet.

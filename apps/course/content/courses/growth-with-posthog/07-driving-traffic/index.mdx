> **In this chapter:** how to choose an acquisition channel deliberately (and why paid
> social is the wrong first channel for many readers of this course), the CAC/LTV maths
> that decides whether a channel is working, the structure of a Meta campaign, the three
> audience types, why Pixel-and-Conversions-API (not Pixel alone), what server-side
> tracking does and doesn't fix, and how PostHog/Hogsend feed conversions and audiences
> back to the ad platform. Accuracy matters here — the privacy landscape is full of
> half-truths.

**How this chapter works.** It's broken into ten short pieces — most are a one- to
three-minute read. Where a piece has a video, the idea is the same each time: read the
one-line reason it's worth your time, watch it (or read the transcript underneath), then
read the short digest so the point sticks. Work through them in order, or jump to what you
need:

1. [Where are you starting from?](./01-where-you-start) — your acquisition mix changes how the rest reads; and channels decay.
2. [The channel menu](./02-channel-menu) — pick the channel that fits your product, and why dev tools skip paid social.
3. [The maths: CAC, LTV, payback](./03-cac-ltv-payback) — the three numbers that decide whether a channel works.
4. [The shape of a Meta campaign](./04-meta-campaign-shape) — campaign/ad-set/ad, and the objective that matters most.
5. [The three audiences](./05-three-audiences) — cold, warm, and lookalike, and why seed quality wins.
6. [Your conversion tracking is lying](./06-tracking-is-lying) — why the browser Pixel under-counts.
7. [The fix: Pixel *and* Conversions API](./07-pixel-and-capi) — server-side events, deduplicated, at high match quality.
8. [Feeding it from PostHog](./08-feeding-from-posthog) — the managed CAPI path, and where Hogsend fits.
9. [Consent](./09-consent) — the part that isn't optional.
10. [Before you move on](./10-before-you-move-on) — a short checklist while it's fresh, then a quiz to check yourself.

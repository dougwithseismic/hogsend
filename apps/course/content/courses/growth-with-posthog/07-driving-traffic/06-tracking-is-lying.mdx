---
title: "Your conversion tracking is lying to you"
description: "The Meta Pixel is browser-side JavaScript, and App Tracking Transparency, Safari and Firefox cookie caps, ad blockers and third-party-cookie death all break it — so it under-counts, and Meta's algorithm optimises on the partial data it can still see."
---

The **Meta Pixel** is browser-side JavaScript — a snippet that fires from the user's
browser and depends on cookies and the request actually completing — and that single fact
is why the conversion numbers it reports are always an under-count. A stack of things now
breaks it:

- **iOS App Tracking Transparency (ATT)** — Apple's prompt that lets users refuse
  cross-app tracking, and most iOS users decline it *(opt-in is commonly cited around
  20–25%, though it varies a lot by source and region — directional)*.
- **Safari / Firefox / Brave** cap or expire the cookies the Pixel relies on.
- **Ad blockers** drop the Pixel outright for ~10–30% of users — the same blocking that
  already eats into your client-side analytics.
- **Third-party cookie deprecation** — the date keeps slipping, but the direction is
  certain.

The result: the Pixel under-counts conversions, Meta's algorithm optimises on partial
data, and your reported ROAS (return on ad spend — revenue divided by what you spent to
get it) is wrong. You can't fix bid strategy on numbers that are quietly missing a third
of reality. It's the same lesson as client-side analytics loss, now with money attached:
browser-only measurement under-counts, and the fix is to move the important events
server-side.

The knock-on effect is worse than bad reporting: **the algorithm optimises on what it
sees.** If the Pixel disproportionately loses conversions from privacy-conscious,
ad-blocking users (it does), Meta learns to find *fewer people like them* — a silent
bias in delivery, not just a counting error. Signal quality is targeting quality.

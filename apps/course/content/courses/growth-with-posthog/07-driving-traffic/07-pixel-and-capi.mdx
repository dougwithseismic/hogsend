title: "The fix: Pixel and Conversions API"
description: "The Conversions API sends events server-side, surviving ad blockers and cookie expiry — but Meta's guidance is both, deduplicated on a shared event_id. What server-side genuinely fixes, what it doesn't, and how EMQ scores your match quality."
---

The **Conversions API (CAPI)** sends events **server-side** — from your server (or a tool
such as a CDP, a customer data platform that collects and routes your events) directly to
Meta's endpoint, skipping the browser entirely. Because it never touches the user's
browser, it survives ad blockers, cookie expiry, and failed Pixel loads.

Meta's own guidance is not "CAPI instead of Pixel" — it's **both, deduplicated.** You
send the same event from the browser (Pixel) and the server (CAPI), and Meta merges them.
Redundancy plus better matching.

Server-side genuinely fixes three things. It **recovers web conversions** lost to ad
blockers, to Safari's Intelligent Tracking Prevention (ITP — its system for capping and
expiring tracking cookies), and to failed Pixel loads. It raises **match quality**,
because your server holds the user's hashed email or phone that the browser often doesn't.
And it lets you send **back-office conversions the browser never sees** — trial→paid,
qualified-lead, refunds, LTV, offline events. That last one is the real prize: these are
usually your *most valuable* conversions, and no pixel can see a single one of them.

It's worth being precise about what server-side does *not* fix, because this is where a
lot of advice overpromises. **It is not a consent loophole** — you still need a lawful
basis to collect, hash and share user data (the consent piece is next), and a
SHA-256-hashed email is **pseudonymous, not anonymous**: same input, same hash,
re-identifiable. **It does not restore iOS *app-install* attribution** — that's governed
by Apple's SKAdNetwork plus Meta's Aggregated Event Measurement, which is aggregated,
delayed and modelled, *not* user-level, and web CAPI doesn't bring per-user iOS
attribution back. **It doesn't fix bad inputs** — CAPI with no email, phone or
external_id matches poorly and barely helps; garbage in, garbage matched. And it doesn't
dedupe by magic, which is the next thing to get right.

> ⚠️ **A dated claim you'll still run into: the AEM "8 prioritised events" limit.**
> Meta removed the 8-event cap and manual configuration around **June 2025** and now
> [auto-aggregates eligible events](https://www.conversios.io/blog/meta-aggregated-event-measurement/).
> Older blogs and courses still teach the cap — if a guide mentions it, that's a good
> sign the rest is stale too.

**Deduplication is the thing people get wrong.** When an event goes out from both Pixel
and CAPI, both copies need the **same `event_id`** (and a matching `event_name`) — Meta
dedupes on `event_id` and counts it once. Without it, a hybrid setup **double-counts**
conversions, corrupting the very ROAS it was built to measure. It's the single most
common CAPI mistake, and it stays silent until you notice the numbers look too good.

The measure of how well all this lands is **Event Match Quality (EMQ)** — Meta's 0–10
score for how well each event source's data matches real Meta users. Aim for **6 or
higher** (8+ is better). The score climbs with more and better identifiers, roughly
weighted:

- **Strongest:** email, the click ID (`fbc`, from the `fbclid` on the landing URL),
  Facebook Login ID.
- **Medium:** phone, the browser ID (`fbp`), and your stable **`external_id`** (your
  user/contact id).
- **Weaker:** name, city, IP, user agent.

All PII (personally identifiable information — email, phone, name) must be normalised
(trim, lowercase) and **SHA-256 hashed** before sending. The way to raise EMQ is to
**feed CAPI from where you actually have identity** — your backend or CRM, which holds
the email and phone the browser never sees. Which is exactly where PostHog and Hogsend
come in.

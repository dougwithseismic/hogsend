Meta is this chapter's worked example because it's the most common paid channel and its
tracking story teaches the privacy lessons every channel shares. But a growth team picks
the channel that fits its product, and for many readers of this course Meta is *not*
the first choice:

- **Google Ads (Search).** Intent-based — you reach people *already searching* for the
  solution. For products with clear search demand (someone Googling "invoice software"),
  this often converts far better than interrupting a feed. Has its own server-side
  conversion path (Enhanced Conversions, the Google Ads API).
- **TikTok / Reddit / X ads.** TikTok for consumer and younger demographics; Reddit for
  niche/technical communities (it can work surprisingly well for dev tools in the right
  subreddits); X for tech/founder audiences. Each has its own pixel + a server-side
  events API with the same dedup logic as §7.7.
- **LinkedIn Ads.** Expensive per click, but the only platform with reliable job-title/
  company targeting — the B2B choice when your buyer is defined by role.
- **The non-paid channels that often beat paid for this audience:**
  - **Content / SEO** — compounding, owned, and the canonical growth engine for dev
    tools and B2B (it's *why* Supabase, PostHog itself, and others grew). Slow to
    start, nearly free to sustain. Rob Walling's rule of thumb for bootstrapped SaaS
    is that content is a 6–12-month investment before it pays — budget the patience,
    not just the writing.
  - **Launches** — [Show HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/showhn.html) (the actual
    rules are worth reading first: no marketing-speak, the thing must be tryable),
    Product Hunt, and the
    subreddit/newsletter/Discord ecosystem of your niche. Launches are spikes, not
    engines — their real value is seeding the loop: every launch visitor you capture
    into an owned channel (Chapter 8) keeps paying after the spike dies.
  - **Community** — Discord/Reddit/Slack communities, the subject of Chapter 8. For
    community-led and web3 products this *is* the acquisition channel.
  - **Partnerships / integrations / marketplaces** — listing in the ecosystem your
    users already live in (a Shopify app, a Vercel integration, a Raycast extension).
    You inherit the platform's traffic and its trust.

**What to take from it:**

- Content marketing fails without a framework for *what to write first*: Walling's own
  early spend flopped because every piece was generic top-of-funnel ("email marketing
  myths") with no plan for sequencing.
- Map content to the five stages of customer awareness (unaware → problem-aware →
  solution-aware → product-aware → most-aware) and **start at the bottom** — versus
  pages, alternative-to pages, case studies, feature docs — because that content closes
  sales *and* works its way up the funnel.
- Match content to search intent: informational/commercial/transactional keywords line
  up with the stages, and tools like Semrush and Similarweb tag that intent so you can
  prioritise which terms to chase.

### A note for dev-tool, community, and web3 founders

For **developer tools and B2B, paid social is weak** — content, community, and product-
led growth usually out-perform it. Developers install ad blockers at the highest rate
of any audience, distrust interruption marketing, and discover tools through
[GitHub, Hacker News, docs, and each other](https://www.heavybit.com/library/article/developer-marketing-guide/)
rather than feeds. Heavybit's
[developer-first go-to-market material](https://www.heavybit.com/devguild/developer-first-go-to-market)
and First Round's
[Retool case study](https://review.firstround.com/retools-path-to-product-market-fit-lessons-for-getting-to-100-happy-customers-faster/)
both land on the same shape: bottom-up, user-to-user distribution, seeded by content
and community, with paid as (at most) an amplifier.

**What to take from it:**

- Developers want a free tier to try before they adopt — they're skeptics who go
  straight to the *docs*, not the marketing pages, and for a developer tool the product
  itself *is* the developer experience.
- Developer marketing is showing the art of the possible, then teaching them to build it
  themselves; devs are late-adopter skeptics who often need ~10 touches before they try
  a tool, so trust compounds over repeated exposure.
- The reliable loop is talk to developers → ask "five whys" → fix what frustrates them
  *fast* → and critically **tell them** you fixed it — the step most teams skip.

And **web3/crypto ads face policy restrictions** on Meta and Google. For both segments,
treat paid as *fuel for the loop*, not the loop itself — which makes the owned channels
in Chapter 8 disproportionately important, and means the accuracy work in the rest of
this chapter applies mostly to whatever *small* paid spend you do run. **If you're a
dev tool, the useful way to read this chapter isn't "now go buy Meta ads" — it's
"here's how paid works and how to measure it honestly when you do use it," with most
of your energy pointed at Chapter 8.**

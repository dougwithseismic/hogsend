The right channel is the one that fits how your product is actually discovered, which is
rarely the channel with the loudest marketing around it. Meta (Facebook and Instagram) is
this chapter's worked example — chosen because it's the most common paid channel and its
tracking story teaches the privacy lessons every channel now shares — but for many readers
here it's the wrong place to start. The paid options each answer a different kind of
demand:

- **Google Ads (Search).** Intent-based — you reach people *already searching* for the
  solution. For products with clear search demand (someone Googling "invoice software"),
  this often converts far better than interrupting a feed. It has its own server-side
  conversion path (Enhanced Conversions, the Google Ads API).
- **TikTok / Reddit / X ads.** TikTok for consumer and younger demographics; Reddit for
  niche and technical communities (it can work surprisingly well for dev tools in the
  right subreddits); X for tech and founder audiences. Each has its own pixel plus a
  server-side events API on the same deduplication logic the Pixel-and-CAPI pieces cover
  later.
- **LinkedIn Ads.** Expensive per click, but the only platform with reliable job-title
  and company targeting — the B2B choice when your buyer is defined by role.

For this course's audience — developer tools, B2B, community and web3 products — the
channels that most often beat paid are the ones you own outright:

- **Content / SEO** — compounding, owned, and the canonical growth engine for dev
  tools and B2B; it's *why* Supabase, PostHog itself, and others grew. Slow to start,
  nearly free to sustain. Rob Walling's rule of thumb for bootstrapped SaaS is that
  content is a 6–12-month investment before it pays — budget the patience, not just the
  writing.
- **Launches** — [Show HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/showhn.html) (whose actual rules
  are worth reading first: no marketing-speak, the thing must be tryable), Product Hunt,
  and the subreddit/newsletter/Discord ecosystem of your niche. Launches are spikes, not
  engines — their real value is seeding the loop, because every launch visitor you capture
  into a channel you own keeps paying after the spike dies.
- **Community** — Discord, Reddit and Slack communities, and the subject of the
  owned-audience chapter that follows. For community-led and web3 products this *is* the
  acquisition channel.
- **Partnerships / integrations / marketplaces** — listing in the ecosystem your users
  already live in (a Shopify app, a Vercel integration, a Raycast extension). You inherit
  the platform's traffic and its trust.

**What to take from it:**

- Content marketing fails without a plan for *what to write first* — Walling's own early
  spend flopped because every piece was generic top-of-funnel with no sequencing behind it.
- Start at the bottom of the funnel: comparison and alternative-to pages, case studies,
  and feature docs close sales *and* work their way up to the broader terms over time.
- Match each piece to search intent, because commercial and transactional keywords convert
  while purely informational ones mostly build awareness.

### A note for dev-tool, community, and web3 founders

For developer tools and B2B, paid social is weak — content, community, and product-led
growth usually out-perform it. Developers install ad blockers at the highest rate of any
audience, distrust interruption marketing, and discover tools through
[GitHub, Hacker News, docs, and each other](https://www.heavybit.com/library/article/developer-marketing-guide/)
rather than feeds. Heavybit's
[developer-first go-to-market material](https://www.heavybit.com/devguild/developer-first-go-to-market)
and First Round's
[Retool case study](https://review.firstround.com/retools-path-to-product-market-fit-lessons-for-getting-to-100-happy-customers-faster/)
both land on the same shape: bottom-up, user-to-user distribution, seeded by content and
community, with paid as (at most) an amplifier.

**What to take from it:**

- Developers judge a tool by its docs and free tier, not its marketing pages — for a
  developer tool, the product itself *is* the marketing.
- Trust compounds slowly: devs are late-adopter sceptics who often need around ten touches
  before they try something, so repeated useful exposure beats one big push.
- The loop that works is to talk to developers, fix what frustrates them fast, and — the
  step most teams skip — *tell them* you fixed it.

Web3 and crypto ads carry the same weakness for a different reason: both Meta and Google
restrict them by policy. For dev tools, communities and crypto alike, the honest
conclusion is the same — treat paid as *fuel for the loop, not the loop itself*. The
accuracy work in the rest of this chapter still matters, but it applies to whatever small
paid spend you run while your real leverage compounds in the audience you own. So if
you're a dev tool, read what follows as *how paid works and how to measure it honestly* —
not as marching orders to go buy ads.

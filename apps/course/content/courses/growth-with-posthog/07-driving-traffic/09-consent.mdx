---
title: "Consent — the part that isn't optional"
description: "Server-side does not mean consent-free, and the rules tightened recently: explicit consent before uploading contact info or sending personal data, you as the data controller on list uploads, and hashing that does not make data anonymous in law."
---

Server-side tracking is not a way around consent, and over 2025 the law moved against the
loophole rather than towards it. Since **January 2025**, Meta itself requires **explicit
user consent** before you upload contact information for a custom audience or send PII
(personally identifiable information — an email, a phone number) through CAPI. The server
is a different pipe, not a different rulebook.

The obligations sit with you, not Meta. When you upload a list you are the **data
controller** — [Meta's own custom-audience terms](https://www.facebook.com/legal/terms/customaudience)
say so explicitly — which means you need a lawful basis, usually consent. A German
regulator has already ruled that list uploads without consent are unlawful, and hashing
doesn't rescue you: a SHA-256-hashed email is pseudonymous, not anonymous, so in law it
is still personal data.

Meta gives you the controls to do this properly — consent signals and data-processing
options exist for exactly this purpose — and in consent regimes the safe default is
simply not to send events for users who haven't opted in. Less is safer as a rule: send
only what you need (a hashed email, phone or external_id) and you stay clear of the
sharpest edges. As of **September 2025**, Meta blocks custom conversions that carry
sensitive health or financial data outright.

The strategic point underneath all of it: an **owned, consented identity graph** — emails
captured *with opt-in* at signup and verification — is what makes the whole custom-
audience, CAPI and lookalike machine both *effective* and *compliant*. You can only feed
Meta good, legal data if you collected it well in the first place. That is what the next
chapter is about.

## 5.1 Lifecycle messaging is how you plug the bucket

Chapter 2 said: fix retention before scaling acquisition. But "fix retention" is not a
thing you do in the product code alone — a lot of it is *reaching the user at the right
moment* and pulling them back toward value. That's lifecycle messaging: the right
message, triggered by the right behaviour, at the right time.

It's worth tying this directly to the leaky bucket, because each hole in the bucket
has a message that plugs it:

- A user signs up but never reaches value → an **onboarding** sequence pulls them to
  the aha moment.
- An active user goes quiet → a **win-back** message brings them back before they're
  gone for good.
- A card fails → a **dunning** sequence recovers revenue you already earned.

Lifecycle messaging isn't "marketing email." It's the operational layer that moves
people between AARRR stages — and the cheapest retention lever you have, because the
users are already yours. For a growth team it's also the *action* half of the operating
model (Chapter 0): measurement (Chapters 1–4) tells you where the leak is; lifecycle
messaging is how the team acts on it automatically, every time, without anyone
remembering to.

One more framing before the mechanics, because it changes how you'll design every
message: lifecycle messaging is a *service to the user*, not a broadcast at them. Every
good lifecycle message answers a question the user has at that moment — "what do I do
first?", "why did my payment fail?", "what changed since I left?" A message with no
answer behind it is noise, and users bill you for noise in unsubscribes and spam
complaints (§5.6). The whole discipline of this chapter — triggers, cadence, exit
conditions, holdouts — exists to keep the program on the right side of that line.

## 5.2 The stages, mapped to AARRR

Every lifecycle program is some subset of these. Here's each one, when it fires, and
which AARRR stage it serves:

| Program | AARRR stage | Fires when | Typical trigger event |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Welcome** | Acquisition → Activation | Right after signup | `user_signed_up` |
| **Onboarding / activation** | Activation | New user hasn't hit the aha yet | signup + "not yet activated" |
| **Engagement / feature adoption** | Retention | A valuable feature goes unused | `feature_x_never_used` after N days |
| **Retention / habit** | Retention | On the product's natural usage rhythm | usage cadence, streaks, digests |
| **Resurrection / win-back** | Retention | A user goes dormant | inactivity 30/60/90 days |
| **Referral** | Referral | At peak satisfaction | activation milestone hit |
| **Expansion / upsell** | Revenue | Approaching a plan limit / power usage | `approaching_limit` |
| **Dunning / failed payment** | Revenue | A charge fails | `payment_failed` |

A useful mental model (from Reforge): **retention is an output, not a stage you push
on directly.** Its inputs are *activation*, *engagement*, and *resurrection* — and
lifecycle messaging is how you move each of those inputs.
[Customer.io's lifecycle guide](https://customer.io/learn/lifecycle-marketing/automation-and-personalization-guide)
frames the same program as a five-step arc — capture, activate, retain, win back,
expand — which is a decent sanity check that your table above covers the whole
relationship, not just the first week of it.

The best programs reinforce the product's *existing* habit loop rather than inventing
new reasons to log in. A fitness app's habit is the workout; the message that says
"your Tuesday session is waiting" works because it strengthens a loop that already
exists. A message that invents a reason ("check out our blog!") fights the user's
attention instead of riding it. Before writing any sequence, it's worth naming the
habit loop you're reinforcing — teams that can't name one usually discover, a few
months in, that they've built a newsletter and called it lifecycle.

## 5.3 The order to build them — and why

You can't build all eight at once — and you don't need to. Here's the order most
programs converge on, and the reasoning behind it, so you can adapt it rather than
follow it blindly:

### 1. Welcome + onboarding — first, and most important

It's hard to justify starting anywhere else, for three reasons:

- **Peak intent.** A new user is more engaged the moment after signup than they will
  ever be again. Welcome and onboarding emails consistently post the highest open and
  click rates of any automated message — welcome-email open rates commonly land around
  **35–50%** *(well-attested across benchmark reports; the high end is SaaS-blog
  territory — [Litmus's State of Email reports](https://www.litmus.com/state-of-email-reports)
  are the least salesy place to calibrate)*.
- **It plugs the biggest hole.** Most early churn happens in the gap between signup and
  activation. That's the widest leak in the bucket, and onboarding is the patch.
- **It's the highest-leverage growth lever you have.** Activated users retain roughly
  ~2× better than non-activated ones
  ([Lenny Rachitsky's activation benchmarks](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-a-good-activation-rate)).
  Doubling activation can beat doubling signups — at zero acquisition cost. Onboarding
  email is the cheapest way to move activation.

> ⚠️ You'll see "poor onboarding causes 40–60% of early churn" repeated everywhere.
> It's directional folklore with no single solid study behind it — believe the
> direction (onboarding matters enormously), not the decimal.

What a good welcome sequence actually contains, since "send a welcome series" hides all
the craft: **message one** delivers the single next action (not five features — the
first action from your activation funnel, Chapter 4); **message two**, a day or two
later, either celebrates progress or unblocks the stall, branching on behaviour
(Chapter 6 shows the code); **message three** introduces the second habit only once the
first stuck. Every message earns the next; none of them fires if the user has already
moved past it. That last property — *not sending* — is what separates lifecycle from a
drip.

### 2. Dunning / failed-payment recovery — early, if you charge

Most lifecycle guides bury this because they're written for e-commerce. For a
subscription product it belongs near the top, because it's often the **highest
dollar-ROI** journey you'll ever build: it recovers revenue you already earned.

- **Involuntary churn** (failed cards, expirations) is **20–40% of total SaaS churn**
  *(well-attested — consistent across many independent sources, including
  [Baremetrics' dunning writeup](https://baremetrics.com/blog/dunning-management))*.
- Well-run dunning recovers a large share of it.
  [Stripe's Smart Retries documentation](https://docs.stripe.com/billing/revenue-recovery/smart-retries)
  is the primary source worth reading: machine-timed retries plus reminder emails are
  the whole mechanism, and Stripe publishes how its retry timing is chosen. Vendor
  case studies claim recovery rates well above half of failed payments — treat the
  precise percentages as marketing, the mechanism as real.

A handful of retry-and-remind emails can recover a meaningful slice of your MRR. The
reason it's so high-ROI: these users *want* to keep paying — they're not churning by
choice, their card just expired. You're removing friction, not persuading — which is
why this one rewards being built early. The sequence itself is short: an immediate "your payment didn't go through" with
a one-click card-update link, a reminder a few days later if the retry hasn't cleared,
and a final "your account is about to pause" — all of it exiting the instant a payment
succeeds (Chapter 6, §6.7 builds exactly this).

### 3. Win-back / resurrection — next

Reactivating a lapsed user is cheaper than acquiring a new one, and they already
understand the value. Reactivation rates of **10–30% of lapsed users** are the commonly
cited band *(directional)*. The structure that works —
[Klaviyo's win-back examples](https://www.klaviyo.com/blog/winback-email-campaign-examples)
are e-commerce but the shape transfers — is a gentle "here's what's new" first touch,
a concrete-value second touch, and only then an incentive if you use one at all.
Leading with what changed since they left works because "we miss you" answers no
question the user has. Dormancy is also worth segmenting honestly: 30 days idle in a
daily-use product is churn; in a tax product it's Tuesday. Your dormancy threshold
comes from *your* usage interval (Chapter 2's retention curve), not from a template.

### 4. Engagement + retention/habit — once the above are stable

Feature-adoption nudges and habit reinforcement, tuned to your product's natural usage
rhythm. These are the journeys that most reward the behavioural branching of §5.7 —
"you've never tried X" is only a good email if it's true.

### 5. Referral — after you have a reliable activation milestone

You need a real "aha" to ask from — the ask only lands once the user has felt the
value, which is why this waits for a reliable activation milestone (more in Chapter 8).

### 6. Expansion / upsell — as monetisation matures

> One thing to **drop**, not build: rigid, time-based lead-nurture drips that ignore
> what the user is actually doing. They underperform behavioural triggers
> ([MarTech's lifecycle-programs piece](https://martech.org/5-email-lifecycle-programs-you-need-and-one-to-drop/)
> makes the same call), and a plain newsletter does the relationship-building job
> better.

## 5.4 Triggered beats batch-and-blast

The core argument for the whole approach: a message triggered by behaviour reaches a
person at a moment of intent. A batch send hits everyone regardless of where they are
in their journey. The performance gap is large and consistent:

- Automated/triggered flows are a tiny fraction of send volume but drive a hugely
  disproportionate share of revenue — a common benchmark is **flows ≈ 5% of sends but
  ~40% of email revenue** *(vendor benchmark, directional — the direction is rock
  solid even if the exact split varies)*.
- Triggered emails show materially higher open and click rates than batch campaigns —
  often roughly **70–120% higher opens and several times the click rate**. Treat these
  as a *range*, not a precise multiple; the numbers vary wildly by source.

> Avoid the fake-precise stats floating around win-back blogs ("2,361% higher
> conversion"). They're uncited SEO bait. The honest version: multi-touch behavioural
> sequences materially outperform single batch sends. You don't need invented decimals
> to make the case.

Why the gap exists is worth internalising, because it predicts where triggered wins
biggest. A batch send's relevance is the *average* relevance across your whole list —
necessarily mediocre. A triggered send's relevance is pinned to a moment: the user
*just* signed up, *just* hit a milestone, *just* had a card fail. The bigger the spread
in your users' states, the bigger triggered's advantage — which is why it dominates in
SaaS onboarding (users are wildly spread) and matters less for a weekly content digest
(everyone's in the same state: it's Thursday).

### How a product event becomes a message

The mechanism, which Chapter 6 implements:

1. You instrument a behaviour as an **event** with properties (Chapter 3) —
   `trial_started`, `project_created`, `payment_failed { amount }`.
2. A journey is **subscribed** to that event. When the event fires, the journey starts
   for that user.
3. The journey runs its steps — wait, check a condition, send, branch — and an **exit
   condition** stops it the instant the goal is met (e.g. stop the "upgrade" sequence
   the moment the user upgrades).

A few reliable trigger → message pairs to model yours on:

| Behaviour | Message |
|---|---|
| Signed up | Welcome #1 — one clear CTA to the first action |
| Trial started | Onboarding sequence begins |
| Key action not done in 24–72h | Activation nudge |
| Milestone hit | Celebration + (timed) referral ask |
| Inactive 30 days | Re-engagement #1 |
| Inactive 60–90 days | Win-back with an incentive |
| Payment failed | Dunning: retry + update-card CTA |
| Subscription cancelled | Win-back / save offer |

Note what's *absent* from the table: "it's been 7 days since email #2." Time appears
only as a window on behaviour ("not done in 24–72h"), never as the reason to send. If the
trigger column starts filling with durations instead of behaviours, that's usually the
drip you were escaping, quietly creeping back in.

## 5.5 Why touch-points matter (and how many)

A single email is a single chance to catch someone at the right moment — and most of
the time you'll miss it. Sequences work because they account for missed opens and rising
intent over days. Multi-touch beats single-touch. The defensible framing: **a 3–5
message sequence materially outperforms a one-shot send.**

But messaging is a dial you can turn too far in either direction:

- **Under-messaging** is the silent failure. No onboarding push means the bucket just
  leaks. There's also a deliverability cost: senders who email irregularly see roughly
  *double* the unsubscribe rate of those who send consistently — inbox reputation is
  built by showing up predictably.
- **Over-messaging** burns the relationship: fatigue, unsubscribes, spam complaints,
  and damaged sender reputation. *(One survey claims ~56% of people unsubscribe after
  4 marketing emails in 30 days — directional, but the effect is real.)* The spam
  complaint is the expensive one — it doesn't just lose that user, it degrades
  delivery to everyone else (§5.6).

A workable default cadence:

- **Welcome series:** every 2–3 days for the first few touches, while intent is high.
- **Active users:** 1–3 per week, depending on your product's rhythm.
- **Dormant users:** slow and value-led — a user who's already drifting is the last
  person a barrage will win back.

The signal that tells you the dial has gone too far: **engagement per send drops with
each added message.** Opens, clicks, or revenue-per-email falling as you add sends is
the program telling you it's reached the useful maximum.

Two practices become load-bearing the moment you run more than one journey:

- **Frequency capping** — a ceiling on total messages per user across all journeys and
  channels, so two journeys don't both fire on the same bad day. The failure mode it
  prevents is real and common: a user's card fails the same week they go dormant and
  hit a plan limit, and three well-intentioned journeys each send twice. Every message
  was individually justified; the pile-up is what gets you marked as spam.
- **Suppression** — stop a sequence the instant its goal is met, and respect an opt-out
  on *every* channel, not just the one they unsubscribed from.

## 5.6 Deliverability: none of this works if you don't land

Everything above assumes the message reaches the inbox. That's earned, not given —
and since early 2024 it's also *specified*. Gmail and Yahoo's bulk-sender rules
([Google's sender guidelines](https://support.google.com/mail/answer/81126) are the
primary source;
[Resend's summary](https://resend.com/blog/gmail-and-yahoo-bulk-sending-requirements-for-2024)
is the readable version) made the previously-soft best practices hard requirements:

- **Authenticate everything: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC.** These are DNS records proving
  your mail actually comes from your domain. No auth, no inbox — it's that binary now
  for senders above trivial volume.
- **One-click unsubscribe** (the `List-Unsubscribe` header), honoured within two days.
  Making people hunt for the link doesn't retain them; it converts unsubscribes into
  spam complaints.
- **Spam complaint rate below 0.3%** — and realistically well below it. This is the
  metric the cadence discipline in §5.5 protects.

Beyond the hard rules, three habits keep a young domain healthy: **send from a
subdomain** (e.g. `mail.yourapp.com`) so lifecycle reputation is insulated from your
transactional mail; **warm up volume gradually** rather than blasting a cold list on
day one; and **prune non-engagers** — continuing to mail people who never open is how
good lists rot into spam-trap territory. None of this is busywork. A lifecycle program
with a damaged sender domain is a program that silently stopped existing; you'll keep
"sending" for months before anyone notices nothing lands.

## 5.7 Personalise on behaviour, not just name

`Hi {{first_name}}` is not personalisation. Real lifecycle personalisation branches on
**what the user did or didn't do in the product** — their plan, which features they've
touched, how many projects they've created, whether they hit the aha moment. Behavioural
triggers reportedly drive several times the engagement of time-based sends
*(directional —
[Userlist's trigger catalogue](https://userlist.com/blog/behavior-email-campaign-triggers/)
is a good idea-bank)*, and it's the thing a code-first engine does well: your journey
can read event history and person properties and take a different path for each user.
Chapter 6 shows exactly that.

The test for whether personalisation is real: **could this exact message have been sent
to a user in a different state?** "You created 3 projects but haven't invited a
teammate" passes. "Here are five tips for getting started," sent to someone who
finished setup a week ago, fails — and quietly teaches the user your emails aren't
worth opening. The damage of fake personalisation is cumulative and invisible in any
single send's metrics.

## 5.8 Multi-channel: email is the workhorse, not the only horse

Different channels do different jobs at different stages:

- **Email** — highest reach, best for storytelling and education. The workhorse for
  welcome, onboarding, and win-back.
- **In-app** — highest *relevance*, because the user is already in the product. Best
  for activation nudges and, crucially, **referral asks at the aha moment** —
  in-product asks convert better than email asks because you're catching them while
  they're holding the value.
- **SMS / push / Discord / Telegram** — urgency and community. Discord/Telegram
  linking (Chapter 8) lets community engagement feed the *same* journey graph as your
  emails.

The principle: **one trigger can branch across channels**, on unified data, evaluated in
real time — with frequency capping and cross-channel suppression applied across all of
them. The mistake to avoid is running channels as separate programs with separate
tools and separate opt-out lists: that's how a user who muted your emails gets the
same campaign by push, and how nobody on the team can answer "how many times did we
contact this person this week?"

## 5.9 The tools you could use (and when no-code is the right call)

This course builds journeys in Hogsend, but a growth team should know the landscape and
choose deliberately. The honest map:

- **Customer.io** — the closest "serious" behavioural-messaging tool: event-triggered,
  multi-channel, strong segmentation. A genuinely good choice, especially for a
  marketing-owned program. Trade-off: your lifecycle logic lives in their UI, not your
  repo.
- **Loops.so** — clean, developer-friendly transactional + lifecycle email; lovely DX
  for simple flows. Trade-off: less powerful when your branching gets complex.
- **Klaviyo** — the e-commerce default; superb for cart/browse/post-purchase flows on
  Shopify. If you're commerce, start here. Weaker fit for product-event-driven SaaS.
- **Braze / Iterable** — enterprise-grade multi-channel orchestration. Powerful,
  expensive, and overkill until you're at real scale with a marketing team.
- **Intercom** — strong when messaging and support live together (in-app + email +
  inbox). You pay for the suite.
- **Resend Broadcasts / Mailchimp / ConvertKit** — broadcast-first. Fine for a
  newsletter; not built for behavioural, per-user journeys.
- **PostHog Workflows** — no-code, lives next to your data, good for batch + simple
  drips (Chapter 1, §1.3).

**When a no-code tool is the right call:** if a non-engineer owns messaging, if your
flows are simple drips, or if you need to ship something this week without touching the
codebase — Customer.io or Loops will serve you well, and there's no virtue in code for
its own sake.

**Why this course is code-first (Hogsend):** because for a *product-led growth team*
(Chapter 0) the messaging layer is part of the product, and it benefits from the same
things your product code does — version control, code review, real branching logic,
testing, and exactly-once guarantees. When your journey needs to *read* arbitrary event
history and *branch* on it, a flowchart UI becomes the bottleneck; code doesn't. And the
identity graph that makes Chapter 8 work is much easier when the messaging engine is
yours. The trade-off is real and you should make it with eyes open: code-first asks an
engineer to own lifecycle. For the audience of this course, that's a feature.

## 5.10 Prove it works: holdouts

This is the discipline that separates a real lifecycle program from cargo-culting. Open
and click rates do **not** tell you whether a message *caused* anything — maybe those
users would have converted anyway. To measure real impact, you measure **incrementality**
with a **holdout**:

- Randomly withhold the message(s) from a **control group**, and compare the outcome —
  activation, retention, revenue — between the users who got messaged and those who
  didn't.
- The difference is the *incremental lift* you can actually attribute to the messaging.

Two kinds:

- **Global holdout** — a small % of users excluded from *all* lifecycle messaging.
  Measures the whole program's lift. This is the number that justifies the program's
  existence in the monthly review (Chapter 9).
- **Campaign holdout** — users excluded from one specific journey. Measures that
  journey's lift, and powers the kill-or-keep decision per journey.

A worked example: messaged group upgrades at 18%, held-out group at 11% → **+7
points** of lift you can credit to the journey. A standing holdout on every important
flow, with lift reported monthly, is the habit that keeps the whole program honest.
This is the same correlation-vs-causation lesson
from Chapter 2's activation section — only an experiment proves cause, and it's the same
discipline that made your activation metric trustworthy.
[Eppo's incrementality primer](https://www.geteppo.com/blog/marketing-incrementality-101)
covers the standard designs (user-level holdout, geo holdout, PSA tests) if you want
the fuller statistical picture, and
[Reforge's lock-down incrementality method](https://www.reforge.com/guides/method-run-a-lock-down-incrementality-test-on-meta-ads)
shows the same logic applied to a paid channel — worth reading now because Chapter 7
will ask you to apply it there too.

Common holdout mistakes, so you don't relearn them expensively: **too-small holdouts**
on low-volume journeys (a 5% holdout of 200 users tells you nothing — use 20–50% until
volume grows); **peeking early** and stopping the moment the numbers look good
(decide the read date in advance); and **quietly excluding the holdout from only some
messages** (the comparison is only clean if the held-out group's *entire* experience
differs by exactly the thing you're measuring).

A code-first engine makes holdouts natural: randomly skip enrollment for a held-out
cohort and compare their downstream events. Chapter 6 shows where that hook lives
([Blueshift's holdout writeup](https://blueshift.com/blog/product-spotlight-understand-the-true-impact-of-your-campaigns-with-holdout-testing/)
shows the same idea in a no-code tool, if that's your stack). This is also where
lifecycle messaging stops being "marketing" and becomes the growth-team experiment
loop from Chapter 0 — every important journey is a standing A/B test with a measured
result, not an act of faith.

## 5.11 Which journeys first, for your kind of startup

The order in §5.3 is the default; here's how it shifts by type:

- **Self-serve SaaS** — welcome/onboarding → dunning → win-back, exactly as written.
- **E-commerce** — abandoned-cart and browse-abandonment first (highest-ROI flows in
  commerce), then post-purchase and win-back. Klaviyo territory.
- **Consumer app** — habit/streak and resurrection dominate; onboarding is about
  forming the habit, not "setup."
- **Marketplace** — onboarding has *two* versions (supply and demand), and the key
  retention message is "your side of the market is active" (a new listing, a new buyer).
- **Community / web3** — the link-and-verify and gamification journeys (Chapter 8) *are*
  the lifecycle program; email supports them.
- **B2B with sales** — onboarding + expansion/upsell matter most; dunning if self-serve
  billing exists; lifecycle messaging assists the human, it doesn't replace them.

## 5.12 Before you move on

A few things worth settling while this chapter is fresh — each one makes the build in
Chapter 6 noticeably smoother:

1. **Pick the first journey** — welcome + onboarding, for the §5.3 reasons (with
   dunning next if you charge; it's the fastest revenue win in the list).
2. **Map each journey to its events** — the one that triggers it and the one that
   exits it. If the exit event is hard to name, that's usually a sign the journey
   doesn't have a clear goal yet.
3. **Sketch your cadence** per stage and a global frequency cap, so the pile-up
   failure from §5.5 can't happen by accident.
4. **Check the deliverability basics** — SPF/DKIM/DMARC on a sending subdomain and
   one-click unsubscribe (§5.6). Cheap to set up now; expensive to retrofit once a
   domain's reputation is damaged.
5. **Choose your tool deliberately** (§5.9) — and if it's code-first, the next
   chapter is the build.
6. **Plan a holdout** for that first journey, so its lift is measured from day one
   rather than argued about later.

You now know *what* to build and *why*. Next, you build it — in code.

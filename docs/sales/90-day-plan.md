# The 90-Day Sales Plan

Open this file every morning. Do the daily block. Update the tracker. Nothing else counts.

| | |

## Week-by-week

Each week has one countable goal. If the number is hit, the week passed. If not, it failed, and next week's first job is the shortfall.

**Week 1, Jul 20 to 26: Warm network. Goal: 15 warm messages sent, 25 targets listed.**
Monday and Tuesday: write the list of every past client, ex-colleague, and person who has seen your work. Send 8 warm messages by Tuesday night, 7 more by Thursday. Wednesday to Friday: build the first 25 cold targets and sign up for 10 of their products so onboarding emails start arriving. Record zero teardowns this week. Warm replies come first.

**Week 2, Jul 27 to Aug 2: First teardowns. Goal: 5 teardowns sent, target list at 50.**
Record and send your first 5 Looms. They will be rough. Send them anyway. Finish the target list to 50. Sign up for 10 more products. Spend 2 hours total, no more, applying to the PostHog partner directory and emailing Resend about their experts channel. Take any warm-intro calls that landed.

**Week 3, Aug 3 to 9: Rhythm. Goal: 8 teardowns sent.**
Two recordings a day, four days a week. Start the follow-up cadence on week 2 sends.

**Week 4, Aug 10 to 16: Goal: 8 teardowns sent.**
Running total 21. Follow-ups now run daily. First calls should be booking. If zero calls have happened by Friday, the problem is the payload or the list. Reread the qualification section, fix one variable, not both.

**Week 5, Aug 17 to 23: Goal: 8 teardowns sent.**
Running total 29. If you have a signed audit by now, deliver it inside the week without dropping outbound below 4 teardowns.

**Week 6, Aug 24 to 30: Midpoint. Goal: 8 teardowns sent, midpoint review done.**
Running total 37. Friday: full review. If fewer than 4 real conversations have happened, tighten the qualification bar and cut the weakest 10 remaining targets for better ones. Do not change the price.

**Week 7, Aug 31 to Sep 6: Goal: 8 teardowns sent.**
Running total 45.

**Week 8, Sep 7 to 13: Goal: final 5 teardowns sent. All 50 done.**
Shift the freed time into follow-ups and calls.

**Week 9, Sep 14 to 20: Goal: every open thread followed up, every warm call taken.**
No new prospecting. Work the pipeline. Push every warm conversation to a yes or a no on the audit.

**Week 10, Sep 21 to 27: Goal: close.**
Ask directly on every live thread: "Do you want the audit booked before the end of the month? I have two slots." That is a fact, you do.

Sep 28 to 30: tally the numbers against the kill criterion. Write the verdict down.

## The daily block

Four hours, Monday to Thursday. Fridays: 2 hours of follow-ups plus the weekly review. Do the block before any other work.

| Time | Task |
|---|---|
| 09:00 to 09:20 | Follow-ups due today. Check the tracker, send them. |
| 09:20 to 11:00 | Teardown 1: 30 min reviewing their onboarding emails and product, 20 min recording in one take, 10 min to write and send the email, log it. |
| 11:00 to 12:30 | Teardown 2, same shape. |
| 13:30 to 14:00 | List maintenance: qualify 2 new targets, sign up for their products. |
| Afternoons | Calls go here. A booked call replaces teardown 2 that day. |

Rules. One take on the Loom, do not re-record. Eight minutes maximum. A teardown is sent the same day it is recorded. If a day is missed, the count carries forward, it does not disappear.

## Finding and qualifying the 50

### Sources, in order of yield

1. **PostHog surfaces.** The PostHog customer stories page, their Slack and community forum, and their startup program announcements. Every named company is a confirmed PostHog user.
2. **Resend surfaces.** Resend's customers and showcase pages, their Twitter/X mentions, and "built with Resend" posts. Confirmed Resend users.
3. **Stack detection on candidates from anywhere else.** Open the site, view source or the network tab: requests to i.posthog.com, us.i.posthog.com or eu.i.posthog.com confirm PostHog. For email: sign up, open the raw source of the first email received, and read the Return-Path and Message-ID headers. resend.com in the headers confirms Resend. customeriomail.com confirms Customer.io. Iterable and Braze name themselves the same way. BuiltWith and the Wappalyzer extension catch the PostHog snippet at scale.
4. **The pain signal.** Companies with a live job posting for "growth", "lifecycle" or "CRM marketing" and nobody currently in the seat on LinkedIn. They have budgeted for this problem and not filled it.
5. **The expensive-tool segment.** BuiltWith lists of Customer.io, Iterable and Braze users in the 11 to 50 headcount band. These companies pay $20k to $60k a year already, so budget is proven.

### Qualification bar, 4 of 5 required

1. Product-led SaaS with self-serve signup you can actually complete.
2. 10 to 50 employees, or visible revenue signals in the $1M to $10M range.
3. A named person on LinkedIn with growth or marketing in their title who is not a founder. That person gets the email.
4. Stack signal: PostHog or Resend confirmed, or a paid ESP (Customer.io, Iterable, Braze) confirmed.
5. The onboarding emails you received are weak, generic, or absent. This is the teardown material, so it is mandatory in practice.

If nobody with a growth title exists, skip the company. You already established the founder does not take this call.

## The call, and getting to a signed audit

30 minutes, four parts.

1. **5 min, their setup.** "Walk me through what happens today when someone signs up. Who owns the emails, and what tool sends them?" Write down the tool and the owner.
2. **15 min, the teardown, extended.** Share your screen, go one level deeper than the video. Ask for one number: "What is your signup-to-activation rate?" If they know it, the roadmap gets a baseline. If they do not, that is finding number one of the audit.
3. **5 min, the offer.** The three numbers, verbatim from the script above.
4. **5 min, the close.** "The audit is the low-risk way to find out if the rest is worth doing. One week, $2,000, credited against the build. I can start Monday. Do you want the slot?"

If yes: same day, send a one-page agreement (scope: full event, email and journey audit plus a 90-day roadmap; timeline: 5 working days from kickoff; price: $2,000, credited against a build signed within 60 days) and an invoice for the full $2,000 up front. The engagement exists when the invoice is paid, not when they say yes. First audit delivered becomes the case study: ask permission for the before and after numbers in the agreement itself.

### The three objections you will actually hit

**"We could build this in-house."**

> You could, and after the build you effectively will have: it is TypeScript in your repo and your team owns it. But it has not happened in the [N] months since you set up [their tool], and the audit is worth doing either way, because the roadmap tells your team what to build even if you never hire me for the build.

**"Who else have you done this for?" (the no-case-study question)**

> On the productized service, you would be first, and I priced the entry for that: $2,000 and one week, small enough to judge me on the output alone. The work itself I have done for years as a fractional growth engineer, and the entire engine is public on GitHub, so unlike an agency you can read exactly what you would be getting before paying anything.

**"$2,000 for an audit is steep" or "can you do a free version."**

> The free version is the 8-minute video you already have. The audit is a full working week: every event, every email, every journey, plus a 90-day roadmap your own team can execute without me. And it costs $0 if you continue to the build, because it is credited. If $2,000 is a hard no, the honest answer is the rest of the service will not fit either.

## Friday review, every week

Answer in writing, one line each:

1. Teardowns sent this week versus the goal. Number versus number.
2. Follow-ups due versus follow-ups sent.
3. Replies and calls this week. What did the best reply respond to?
4. What did I change about the pitch, and was that based on a prospect's words or my own nerves? Only the first is allowed.
5. **The building check.** Run `git log --oneline --since="7 days ago"` on the hogsend repo. Every commit not paid for by a client or required by the sales motion (the /service page counts, a new feature does not) is a retreat. Write the number of retreat commits down on the scorecard. The acceptable number is zero.
6. Am I on pace? 50 teardowns by end of week 8 means the running total must be at least: W2: 5, W3: 13, W4: 21, W5: 29, W6: 37, W7: 45, W8: 50.

## 1 October 2026

Restating the kill criterion. By 30 September: 15 warm messages sent, 50 teardowns sent, 10 or more real conversations held, 1 or more paid engagements at $2,000 or above.

**Branch 1: criterion met.** The market said yes. Deliver flawlessly, take the before-and-after numbers, publish the case study, and raise the audit to $2,500 for the next ten sends. Repeat this playbook with a case study attached. Engine work returns only where clients pull it.

**Branch 2: activity done, no paid engagement.** The market answered and the answer was no, at this offer, at this ICP. Hogsend becomes what it always credibly was: the best portfolio piece a fractional growth engineer has ever had. Take contract and fractional work at $900 to $1,100 a day with the repo as proof. Stop all unpaid product hours. This is not failure, it is information you paid 10 weeks for instead of another 18 months.

**Branch 3: activity not done.** Fewer than 40 teardowns or fewer than 12 warm messages sent. Then there is no market verdict, because the market was never asked, and the diagnosis from July stands: the blocker was never the product, the license, the page, or the price. On this branch the honest options are to rerun these 10 weeks with an accountability structure that makes skipping impossible, or to admit the selling will not happen and choose branch 2 deliberately. Choosing by default is the only wrong move.

The plan fails only in branch 3. Branches 1 and 2 are both wins over not knowing.

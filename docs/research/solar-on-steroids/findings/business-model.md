# Research: business-model

## Solar on Steroids — The Commercial Model

### What the business actually is
Solar on Steroids Ltd (SOS) is a UK performance-marketing agency (Companies House **15845416**, incorporated **18 July 2024**, registered in Manchester, SIC codes 62090 "other IT service activities" + 82990 "other business support") that sells Meta (Facebook/Instagram) video-ad campaigns plus a proprietary revenue-attribution system to residential **solar/renewables installers**. Founders **Joe Garbutt (CEO)** and **Jude Cornish (CCO)** met at school and started the company in March 2024. Their self-reported trajectory: 4 clients at Solar & Storage Live (April 2024) → 6 on a performance deal → ~35–41 clients by late 2025 → "54/60 UK clients" and ~40 team members by mid-2026, now opening a US arm.

The engine of the offer is a stack, not a single service: (1) authentic self-shot **selfie-video testimonials** turned into ads (the "trust layer"), (2) **Meta video ad production at scale** (50–300 ads per shoot from 15–30 creative angles × 5–10 hook variants), (3) **multi-step quiz funnels on Perspective** (they claim Perspective's #1 agency partner, 30k+ leads, £200k/mo traffic), and (4) a **bespoke attribution system** that optimises campaigns on cost-per-quote / cost-per-sale and fires quote/sale conversions back to Meta via CAPI. This report focuses on how the agency *makes money and wins clients*, per the brief.

### Fee structure — three stacked revenue lines
SOS deliberately runs a **"higher retainer, deeper results"** premium model and refuses to compete on price. The money comes from three places:

1. **Setup fee (one-off).** Charged for Meta-ads onboarding; the amount is **not published** — prospects learn it on the discovery call. It funds the "foundations": the on-site video shoot (they bring their own videographer, "non-negotiable"), a custom mobile-first Perspective funnel (not Meta's free lead forms), 50–300 ads from that shoot, a full audit of the client's sales process/reporting, and immediate access to benchmark data pooled from 20+ existing clients spending six figures/month. Their framing: "you spend a lot on the foundations because without them the house falls down."

2. **Monthly retainer (recurring).** The core P&L. SOS says clients pay roughly **~25% more per month than competitors** and explicitly engineer the retainer so that **"your advertising budget is outweighed by our retainer"** — i.e. the retainer is designed to be *larger than* the ad spend it manages, which is unusual and structurally important (it means fee revenue scales with client count, not ad-spend volume). The retainer includes exclusive lead delivery, client-owned ad accounts/assets, quarterly video shoots at no extra cost, media buying + CRO, and the attribution reporting.

3. **Ad spend — client-funded, with a £2,500/mo floor.** Clients pay Meta directly. The **minimum ad spend is £2,500/month (~£80/day)**, justified two ways: (a) Meta's conversion algorithm needs volume to learn (below the floor you get "a handful of leads" and a "data vacuum"), and (b) the fee-to-spend ratio — they don't want agency fees to consume the budget and make ROI "quite hard except in unsaturated markets." Note SOS *used* to cover ad spend itself (see below) and now "occasionally covers spend on trial bases."

A fourth, separate line: they **sell the attribution system itself as IP to other agencies/service businesses for £40,000–£100,000 +VAT** — a productised-IP revenue stream distinct from the installer retainers.

### The performance-bet origin story (now a sales weapon)
At Solar & Storage Live (April 2024) SOS launched with a **radical performance deal: they covered the client's ad spend themselves and got paid only on results** (calls booked / surveys / closed deals). Six clients signed at an 87.5% close rate — "the offer was too good." It forced brutally fast iteration (lead forms → landing pages at 1% → instant-quote → video → Perspective), because every failed test **burned SOS's own cash**. By October 2024 forecast client ad spend hit **£18,000/month of their own money**, which nearly broke them — they funded the gap by building websites and selling non-solar ad work on the side. In **December 2024 they transitioned to the retainer model.** The lesson they draw: self-funded risk taught skills a commission-only shop never learns (because in commission models the client still funds the spend, so the agency has no real exposure).

This story is now weaponised in sales as the **Activ8 / AJ Renewables-style "performance bet"**: for a marquee prospect (e.g. Activ8, positioned as Ireland's largest installer) they offer a free shoot, waived setup fee, and gifted ad spend (~£3k), **paid only if results improve >3×** — Activ8 reportedly got 5× and −79% cost-per-lead. It's a de-risked land-grab used selectively on lighthouse logos, not the standard commercial terms.

### Scarcity, capacity & qualification — the demand controls
SOS positions on **genuine supply constraint, not fake urgency**: "for at least a year we've been supply constrained rather than demand constrained." The controls:
- **Capacity cap:** they target ~**6 new installers/month** and say they hit the cap in the first ~2 weeks; a stated aim to take on only a **maximum of ~16 installers** in a period (this figure sits oddly against a claimed 41–54 active clients — likely a per-cohort/annual-net-new or premium-tier number; see open questions). Overflow → **waitlist for next month's cohort.**
- **Client qualification floor:** prospects must already be doing **≥8 installations/month** to be accepted ("please do not book in unless you're doing that volume"). This guarantees clients can absorb the lead volume and produces clean ROAS case studies.
- **Fulfilment bottleneck is real:** ~22 people dedicated to ops/fulfilment is the stated constraint on taking more clients.

### Location management instead of exclusivity
SOS **does not offer territory exclusivity** in mainland UK. Instead it manages saturation editorially: installer density is set by **population density and affluence** (dense affluent areas north of London support several aggressive advertisers; sparse regions get fewer), and they will **turn a prospect down or waitlist them** if signing would materially hurt an existing client's results. The economic logic they cite: kickstarting a new campaign is expensive, so protecting the results of existing (retained) clients is worth more than onboarding a competitor into the same postcode. This gives de-facto territorial protection without contractual exclusivity — a smart middle path that keeps them un-sued while still ordering demand.

### Contract structure & churn
**Monthly rolling, no lock-in, 30-day notice, no cancellation fee.** Their thesis (Jude Cornish): *"retention is stronger when you don't lock people in."* They claim **full client retention over a ~two-year period / "zero churn"** while growing 6→35+ clients. Whether that survives scrutiny is unknown (no third-party reviews found — no Trustpilot profile, testimonials are all first-party), but the *structural* point stands: no-tie-in shifts all retention risk onto delivered performance, which forces the operational discipline (attribution proving cost-per-sale) that is their actual moat.

### Team composition (~40 people)
The org is built around content throughput + campaign optimisation + systems:
- **Creative/production:** multiple videographers, **15+ full-time editors** (growing ~4/month, plus 3 contract editor slots/week), graphic designers (2+), creative strategists/directors, a dedicated QA reviewer.
- **Media buying / CRO:** media buyers each owning a **portfolio managing £100k+/mo combined ad spend**, with CRO specialists on funnel conversion.
- **Systems & automation:** a "systems architect"/systems-automation operator running **ClickUp + Make + Zapier + AI workflows** and an **API developer** — this team builds the attribution pipeline (CRM polling *and* webhooks, uid session stitching, CAPI dispatch).
- **Client-facing:** account managers / Customer Success Managers, plus a Business Development Manager for sales.
- Sales funnel is **discovery call → iClosed booking widget** (the site itself runs on Framer with PostHog EU for session recording + surveys; notably **no Meta pixel on the agency's own site** — cobbler's-children, or deliberate).

### Estimated revenue & financial signals
Hard numbers are thin. Companies House shows **first accounts filed for the period to 31 July 2025** (a young private company; likely filed as small/micro — no detailed P&L is public), with next accounts due 31 Dec 2026. Third-party aggregators are unreliable here: Latka lists "$40.1K revenue (2023)" and "$0 raised," but the company didn't incorporate until July 2024, so treat that as noise.

A defensible back-of-envelope: at ~41–54 clients each paying a retainer engineered to *exceed* a ≥£2,500/mo ad spend — say a blended retainer of roughly £3,000–£6,000/mo — implies **~£1.5M–£3.5M annualised retainer run-rate**, plus one-off setup fees and occasional £40k–£100k IP sales. Their headline marketing claims (£25M+ residential solar sales driven; £102M all-time client revenue on the public data feed; 8,695 sales at £11,758 AOV) are **client-outcome** figures, not SOS revenue, and are self-reported/"under-reported while in beta." Bootstrapped, no external funding evident.

### US expansion
Founders flew to the US around **19 April 2026** to launch a first US installer cohort, starting in the **Northeast**, with two verbal agreements in hand and a **$1,600 / £1,200 two-sided referral fee** (paid to both introducer and introduced). The stated thesis: US federal residential-solar rebates were cut and the market contracted, so their **"sell on value/education/trust, not incentives"** playbook is well-timed. This is the same offer re-pointed at a larger, more competitive market.

### Structural moat vs. reputational — what would survive being copied
**Reputational / fragile (won't survive a competent clone):**
- "UK's #1 renewables agency," "first to do video ads," "zero churn," Perspective-#1-partner — status claims that erode as competitors copy the format.
- The founders' personal brand (Jude Cornish's content/X/YouTube presence) and lighthouse case studies.
- Headline ROAS outliers (150x) they themselves flag as non-representative.

**Structural / durable (would survive a copy):**
1. **The attribution system as the wedge.** Optimising on **cost-per-sale, not cost-per-lead**, requires stitching CRM deal outcomes (via both polling *and* webhooks, per-CRM) back to ad clicks and firing value-carrying CAPI events. This is genuinely hard engineering + integration surface (GoHighLevel, HubSpot, Salesforce, Monday, Pipedrive, Payaca, bespoke). It's also what makes the no-tie-in contract safe and the premium retainer defensible — this is the part the founder-reader wants to rebuild, and it's the real IP.
2. **Retainer > ad-spend economics.** Decoupling fee revenue from ad-spend volume makes the P&L predictable and high-margin, and the £2,500 floor + ≥8-install qualification filter self-selects clients who can prove ROI.
3. **Content-factory throughput.** 15+ editors turning one shoot into 50–300 ads is an operational cost structure a solo operator can't match; it's a fulfilment moat, though capital-copyable.
4. **Scarcity/location discipline.** Capacity caps + density-based location management + protect-existing-clients preserve case-study quality (every client hits volume), which compounds into more proof and more inbound.
5. **Vertical focus + pooled benchmark data.** New clients inherit conversion data from 20+ installers — a data-network effect that widens with scale and is genuinely hard to bootstrap cold.

**Net:** the copyable-but-durable core to rebuild is the **cost-per-sale attribution + value tracking + multi-model CAPI dispatch engine** married to a **retainer-exceeds-spend fee model** and a **qualification/scarcity filter**; the video factory and brand are execution and marketing, valuable but not the moat.
